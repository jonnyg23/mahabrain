 <h3 align="center">11. CHARMS, UTTERANCES 275-299.</h3>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 275</i>.</p>
 
 <p>415a. To say: N. comes to you, ye falcons,</p>
 
 <p>415b. since (?) your houses are barred off for N.,</p>
 
 <p>415c. his <i>mrḳ</i>-garment of
 ape-skin on his back.</p>
 
 <p>416a. N. opens the double doors (of heaven); N. goes to the boundary of the
 horizon;</p>
 
 <p>40b. N. laid down the <i>mśd.t</i>-garment
 on the ground;</p>
 
 <p>416c. N. became like the Great One who is in Crocodilopolis.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 276</i>.</p>
 
 <p>417a. To say: Thy act is against thee, what thou doest is against thee,</p>
 
 <p>417b. O <i>sksk</i>-serpent, which is in
 his (thy) hole?, the opponent.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 277</i>.</p>
 
 <p>418a. To say: Horus falls because of his eye; the bull (Set) collapses
 because of his testicles.</p>
 
 <p>418b. Fall, collapse!</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterances
 278</i>.</p>
 
 <p>419a. To say: <i>Bȝbi</i> is arisen, he
 is against the chief of Letopolis,</p>
 
 <p>419b. whom that spittle protected; this (spittle) protects every one beloved
 of me.</p>
 
 <p>419c. Thou art loosed, O <i>wfi</i>-serpent.
 Cause N. to be protected.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_96"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 96</font></a></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 279</i>.</p>
 
 <p>420a. To say: N., I have trampled the mud of the water-courses. Thot is the
 protector of N.,</p>
 
 <p>420b. when it is dark, when it is dark.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 280</i>.</p>
 
 <p>421a. To say: Doer, doer; passer, passer;</p>
 
 <p>421b. thy face behind thee; guard thyself against the great door,</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 281</i>.</p>
 
 <p>422a. To say: Punish the serpent, <i>Kbbhḥrwbi</i>,</p>
 
 <p>422b. O lion of <i>phti</i>, O lion of <i>p<u>t</u>ti</i>, the <i>phti</i> (and) <i>p<u>t</u>ti</i>.</p>
 
 <p>422c. Give to me now, <i>ḥrw<u>t</u>wbś</i>,
 meat, now, one pot.</p>
 
 <p>422d. Go, go, serpent, serpent.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 282</i>.</p>
 
 <p>423a. To say: Lo, this foreign country of the mouth of the river, this is
 thy complaint:</p>
 
 <p>423b. "This foreign country of the mouth of the river belongs to me,
 the lord of <i>Ḥknw</i>."</p>
 
 <p>423c. It is <i>Ḫi-tȝw</i> of <i>Ḥknw</i>, this thine ox-god, the renowned,
 against whom this has been done.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 283</i>.</p>
 
 <p>424a. To say: Truly, N. wags his thumb, the left one, against thee.</p>
 
 <p>424b. He gives a sign with it to Min (with his) thunderbolt. O robber, rob
 not.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 284</i>.</p>
 
 <p>425a. To say: He (serpent) whom Atum has bitten has filled the mouth of N.,</p>
 
 <p>425b. while he wound himself up (lit. wound a winding).</p>
 
 <p>425c. The centipede was smitten by the householder, the householder was
 smitten by the centipede.</p>
 
 <p>425d. That lion is inside this lion.</p>
 
 <p>425e. Two bulls fight inside the ibis.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_97"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 97</font></a></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 285</i>.</p>
 
 <p>426a. Thy two drops of poison are on the way to thy two poison-vessels. Spit
 both out now,</p>
 
 <p>426b. for they two are rich in water. O thou who winkest, thou . who art
 (adorned with) a head-band, O <i>Śšȝ.w</i>,</p>
 
 <p>426c. rain, that the serpent may become cowardly and the throat (canal) of
 my heart may be safe;</p>
 
 <p>426d. storm, that the lion may drown himself in water and the throat of the
 heart of the king (?) may be wide.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 286</i>.</p>
 
 <p>427a. To say: O ye, who gurgle like the young of a "water-pest"
 (crocodile), <i><u>t</u>m<u>t</u>i</i>, <i><u>t</u>hn.w</i>,</p>
 
 <p>427b. <i>kbnw</i>, those who glide away!
 The red crowns (i.e. water-flowers) praise</p>
 
 <p>427c. the <i>tiw-šii</i>; the <i>tiw-šii</i> belong to him who has elevated
 the red crowns.</p>
 
 <p>427d. Hail, we two!</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 287</i>.</p>
 
 <p>428a. To say: <i>Nni</i>, his mother; <i>Nni</i>, his mother.</p>
 
 <p>428b. Art thou really here, art thou really here? Lion, get away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 288</i>.</p>
 
 <p>429a. To say: <i>Hki</i>-serpent or <i>hkr.t</i>-serpent, go away</p>
 
 <p>429b. (with) face on the road. Eye of N., look not at him.</p>
 
 <p>429c. Thou shalt not do thy will with N. Get away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 289</i>.</p>
 
 <p>430a. To say: A bull is fallen because of the <i>ś<u>d</u>ḥ</i>-serpent; the <i>ś<u>d</u>ḥ</i>-
 serpent is fallen because of the bull.</p>
 
 <p>430b. Fall, glide away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 290</i>.</p>
 
 <p>43m. To say: Face falls on face; a knife coloured and black, goes out
 against it, until it has swallowed that</p>
 
 <p>431b. which it has seized.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_98"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 98</font></a></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 291</i>.</p>
 
 <p>432a. To say: Thine honour is effaced, O white hole, by him who has escaped
 the <i>fn<u>t</u></i>-worm.</p>
 
 <p>432b. Thine honour is robbed, O white hole, by him who has escaped the <i>fn<u>t</u></i>-worm.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 292</i>.</p>
 
 <p>433a. To say: Thou art seized, thou, O <i>iknhi</i>-serpent;</p>
 
 <p>433b. thy neighbour (?) has seized thee, <i>iknhi</i>-serpent.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 293</i>.</p>
 
 <p>434a. To say: Back, hidden serpent; hide thyself,</p>
 
 <p>434b. and let N. not see thee.</p>
 
 <p>434c. Back, hidden serpent; hide thyself,</p>
 
 <p>434d. and come not to the place where N. is,</p>
 
 <p>434e. lest he pronounce against thee that name of thine, <i>Nmi</i> son of <i>Nmi.t</i>.</p>
 
 <p>435a. A servant (holy person) as the Ennead's pelican (once) fell into the Nile, (so) flee, flee.</p>
 
 <p>435b. Serpent (beast), lie down.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 294</i>.</p>
 
 <p>436a. To say: N. is Horus who comes forth from the acacia, who comes forth
 from the acacia,</p>
 
 <p>436b. to whom it was, commanded: "Be thou aware of the lion," he
 comes forth to whom it was commanded: "Be thou aware of the lion."</p>
 
 <p>437a. N. has come forth from his <i><u>d</u>ni.t</i>-jar,
 after he had passed the night in his <i><u>d</u>ni.t</i>-jar,</p>
 
 <p>437b. and N. appears in the morning.</p>
 
 <p>43 7c. He has come forth from his <i><u>d</u>ni.t</i>-jar,
 after he had passed the night in his <i><u>d</u>ni.t</i>-jar,</p>
 
 <p>437d. and N. appears in the morning.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 295</i>.</p>
 
 <p>438a. To say: The <i>mȝfd.t</i>-lynx
 springs on the neck of the <i>in-di-f</i>-serpent.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_99"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 99</font></a></p>
 
 <p>438b. It repeats it on the neck of the serpent with the raised head (<i><u>d</u>śr-tp</i>).</p>
 
 <p>438c. Who is it who will remain? It is N. who will remain.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 296</i>.</p>
 
 <p>439a. To say: <i><u>T</u><u>t</u>.w</i>-serpent,
 where to? Thou shalt not go. Stand by N.</p>
 
 <p>439b. N. is Geb. <i>Hm<u>t</u></i>-serpent,
 brother of <i>hm<u>t</u>.t</i>-serpent,</p>
 
 <p>439c. should thy father, the <i><u>d</u>miw</i>,
 die?</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 297</i>.</p>
 
 <p>440a. The hand of N. which is come upon thee--</p>
 
 <p>440b. it is a violent one which is come upon thee,</p>
 
 <p>440c. it is the <i>mȝfd.t</i>-lynx,
 which is in the house of life.</p>
 
 <p>440d. She strikes thee in thy face; she scratches thee in thine eyes,</p>
 
 <p>441a. so that thou fallest in thy dung and glidest in thy urine.</p>
 
 <p>441b. Fall, lie down, glide away, so that thy mother Nut may see thee.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 298</i>.</p>
 
 <p>442a. To say: Rē dawns, his uraeus on his head,</p>
 
 <p>442b. against this serpent, which is come out of the earth, (and) which is
 under the fingers of N.</p>
 
 <p>442c. He (N.) cuts off thy head with this knife, which was in the hand of
 the <i>mȝfd.t</i>-lynx, [which lives in the
 house of life];</p>
 
 <p>443a. he draws, (the teeth) which are upon (in) thy mouth; he saps thy
 poison</p>
 
 <p>443b. with those four strings, which were in the service of the sandals of
 Osiris.</p>
 
 <p>443c. Serpent (beast), lie down; bull, glide away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance,
 299</i>.</p>
 
 <p>444a. To say: The uraeus-serpent is for heaven; the centipede of Horus is
 for the earth.</p>
 
 <p>444b. Horus had a sandal as he advanced (towards) the master of the house,
 the bull of the hole,</p>
 
 <p>444c. the combat-serpent. N. will not be beaten,</p>
 
 <p>444d. (for) his protective sycamore is the protective sycamore of N., his
 refuge is the refuge of N.</p>
 
 <p>444e. Whom N. finds in his way, him he eats for himself bit by bit.</p>
