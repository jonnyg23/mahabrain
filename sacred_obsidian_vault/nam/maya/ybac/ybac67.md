 <p><a name="page_161"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 161</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">Identification of Plant Names mentioned in Landa's text</h3>  
 <p><b>tixzula</b>: lirio; probably Hymenocallis americana, Jacq. (Molina Solis).</p>  
 <p><b>ixlaul</b>: laurel; Stemmadenia insignis, Miers.</p>  
 <p><b>nicte</b>: the generic name for Plumerias.</p>  
 <p>yerbamora or nightshade; <b>pahalcan</b>: Solanum nigrum, L.</p>  
 <p>doradilla; <b>muchcoc</b>: Selaginella, lapidophylla, Spring.</p>  
 <p><b>yaxpahalche</b>: Piper Gaumeri, Trel.</p>  
 <p>zarzaparilla; <b>coceeh</b>: Smilax mexicana, Griseb.</p>  
 <p>"kind of hemp": <b>halal</b>? Scirpus validus, Vahl.</p>  
 <p><b>ixim</b>: maize; Zea mayz, L.</p>  
 <p>beans, frijoles; <b>buul</b>: Phaseolus vulgaris, L.</p>  
 <p>peppers: Capsicum genus.</p>  
 <p>"root, growing like the turnip . . . is the fruit": <b>chicam</b>?; jícama; Pachyrhizus erosus L.</p>  
 <p>"root which grows like the turnip, short, fat, and round": <b>chicam</b>, jicama; Pachyrrhizus erosus, L.</p>  
 <p>"root that grows under the earth"; peanut? Arachis hypogaea, Schlecht, Cham.</p>  
 <p>"there are two other kinds of good roots they use as food":</p>  
 <p>   a<b>iz</b>, camote, sweet potato; Ipomoea batatas L.</p>  
 <p>   b<b>maxcal</b>, <b>macal</b>, yam; Dioscorea alata L.</p>  
 <p>"tree with fruit like round gourds": jícara? <b>luch</b>; Crescentia cujete, L.</p>  
 <p>"smaller gourds": <b>lac</b>? Lagenaria siceraria, Standl.</p>  
 <p>"incense tree": <b>pom</b>, copal; Protium copal, Engl.</p>  
 <p>"handsome tree that grows by wells": <b>yaxche</b>, ceiba; Ceiba pentandra, L.</p>  
 <p><b>kulche</b>: cedar; Cedrela mexicana, M. Roemer.</p>  
 <p>"sort of yellowish tree": <b>ya</b>, sapote; Achras sapota L.</p>  
 <p>brasil; <b>chacte</b>: Caesalpinia platyloba, Wats.</p>  
 <p><b>zoon</b>: Guaiacum sanctum L.</p>  
 <p>"causes sores": <b>chechem</b>; Metopium Brownei Jacq.</p>  
 <p>"tree with double thorns": <b>subim</b>, cornezuelo; Acacia Collinsii, Safford.</p>  
 <p>"use for tying in building": <b>hol</b>; Hibiscus tiliaceus L.</p>  
 <p>"another . . . of which they make bows and lances": <b>chulul</b>?; Apoplanesia paniculata, Presl.</p>  
 <p>"for pillows is superior to tow": <b>piim</b>; Ceiba aesculifolia HBK.</p>  
 <p>"wine-tree which the Indians esteem so highly": <b>balche</b>; Lonchocarpus longistylus, Pittier.</p>  
 <p><b>niiche</b>: grapes; Coccoloba uvifera L.</p>  
 <p><b>abal</b>: plums; Spondias mombin L., or S. purpurea L.</p>  
 <p>bananas: platanos; Musa paradisiaca L., or M. sapientum L.</p>  
 <p>"very large tree that bears a large, longish fruit": <b>chacal haas</b>; Calocarpum mammosum L.</p>  
 <p><b>ya</b>: sapote; Achras sapota L.</p>  
 <p><b>ox</b>: ramón; Brosimum alicastrum, Sw.</p>  
 <p>"another (tree) exceedingly beautiful which bears a fruit like large eggs": <b>put</b>, papaya; Carica papaya L.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_162"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 162</font></a></p>  
 <p><b>uayam</b>: guaya; Talisia olivaeformis HBK.</p>  
 <p><b>pichi</b>: guayava; Psidium guajava L.</p>  
 <p><b>on</b>: aguacate; Persea americana, Mill.</p>  
 <p>"this tree bears a small, tasty, yellow fruit": <b>chucum</b>? Pithecolobium albicans, Kunth.</p>  
 <p>"there are artichokes that are very spiny and ugly": <b>chacuob</b>, pitahaya; Cereus undatus, Haw.</p>  
 <p>"small, rather spiny tree bearing a fruit shaped like a slender cucumber": <b>chom</b>? Bromelia karatas L.</p>  
 <p>"small tree . . . bearing spiny pods like chestnuts": <b>kuxub</b>, achiote; Bixa orellana L.</p>  
 <p>"one kind (of palm) serves for the thatching of the houses": <b>xaan</b>, huano; Inodes japa, Standl.</p>  
 <p>"the other is a low, spiny palm": <b>tuk</b>? Acrocomia mexicana, Karw.?</p>  
 <p>"the other kind (of cotton tree) ... lasts five or six years"; <b>taman</b>; Gossypium barbadense L or G. Schottii Watt, Wild and Cult.</p>

 <p>It should be noted that the pronunciation of all Maya words is as in Spanish, with the following exceptions: <b>c</b> is always hard; <b>x</b> is sounded like <i>sh</i> in English, <b>Xiu</b> being like <i>Shu</i>. The letter <b>k</b> and the stop consonants <b>p</b> <b>t</b> <b>ch</b> and <b>tz</b>, when marked by an apostrophe, as <b>p</b> <b>t</b> <b>ch</b> <b>tz</b>, are preceded by a muscular tension and then sounded explosively, the usual technical term being 'glottal stop.' Thus <b>tz</b> sounds to the English ear as if it were <b>dz</b>, and is usually so written in Yucatan, as in the town <b>Dzitás</b>, near <a href="errata.htm#10">Chichén</a> Itzá. The Mayance languages lack our 'sonants' <b>d</b>, hard <b>g</b>, <b>j</b>, <b>z</b>, as also <b>f</b>; Yucatecan Maya also lacks the <b>r</b>. Double vowels are to be sounded as such, with accent on the first.</p>
