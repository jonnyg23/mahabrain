 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 28</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 To whatsoever evil tongue can tell<br>
 Of womankind King Rodomont gives ear;<br>
 Then journeys homeward; but that infidel<br>
 Finds by the way a place he holds more dear.<br>
 Here him new love inflames for Isabel;<br>
 But so the wishes of the cavalier<br>
 A friar impedes, who with that damsel wends,<br>
 Him by a cruel death the felon ends.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 Ladies, and all of you that ladies prize,<br>
 Afford not, for the love of heaven, an ear<br>
 To this, the landlord’s tale, replete with lies,<br>
 In shame and scorn of womankind; though ne’er<br>
 Was praise or fame conveyed in that which flies<br>
 From such a caitiff’s tongue; and still we hear<br>
 The sottish rabble all things rashly brand,<br>
 And question most what least they understand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 Omit this canto, and—the tale untold—<br>
 My story will as clear and perfect be;<br>
 I tell it, since by Turpin it is told,<br>
 And not in malice or in rivalry:<br>
 Besides, that never did my tongue withhold<br>
 Your praises, how you are beloved by me<br>
 To you I by a thousand proofs have shown,<br>
 Vouching I am, and can but be, your own.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 Let him who will, three leaves or four pass-by,<br>
 Nor read a line; or let him, who will read,<br>
 As little of that landlord’s history,<br>
 As of a tale or fiction, make his creed.<br>
 But to my story:—When his auditory<br>
 He saw were waiting for him to proceed,<br>
 And that a place was yielded him, o’eright<br>
 The cavalier, he ’gan his tale recite:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 “Astolpho that the Lombard sceptre swayed,<br>
 Who was King Monacho, his brother’s heir,<br>
 By nature with such graces was purveyed,<br>
 Few e’er with him in beauty could compare:<br>
 Such scarce Apelles’ pencil had pourtrayed,<br>
 Zeuxis’, or worthier yet, if worthier were:<br>
 Beauteous he was, and so by all was deemed,<br>
 But far more beauteous he himself esteemed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 “He not so much rejoiced that he in height<br>
 Of grandeur was exalted o’er the rest,<br>
 And that, for riches, subjects, and for might,<br>
 Of all the neighbouring kings he was the best,<br>
 As that, superior to each other wight,<br>
 He beauty was throughout the world confest.<br>
 This pleased the monarch, who the praise conferred,<br>
 As that wherein he most delighted, heard.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 “Faustus Latinus, one of his array,<br>
 Who pleased the king, a Roman cavalier,<br>
 Hearing ofttimes Astolpho now display<br>
 The beauties of his hand, now of his cheer,<br>
 And, questioned by that monarch, on a day,<br>
 If ever in his lifetime, far or near,<br>
 He any of such beauty had espied,<br>
 To him thus unexpectedly replied:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 “Faustus to him replied: ‘By what I see,<br>
 And what I hear, is said by every one,<br>
 Few are there that in beauty rival thee;<br>
 And rather I those few confine to one:<br>
 Jocundo is that one, my brother he;<br>
 And well I ween that, saving him alone,<br>
 Thou leavest all in beauty far behind;<br>
 But I in him thy peer and better find.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 “Impossible Astolpho deemed the thing,<br>
 Who hitherto had thought the palm his own;<br>
 And such a longing seized the Lombard king<br>
 To know that youth whose praises so were blown,<br>
 He prest, till Faustus promised him to bring<br>
 The brother praised by him, before his throne,<br>
 Though ’twould be much if thither he repaired,<br>
 (The courier added) and the cause declared:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 “Because the youth had ne’er been known to measure,<br>
 In all his life, a single pace from Rome;<br>
 But, on what Fortune gave him, lived at leisure,<br>
 Contented in his own paternal dome;<br>
 Nor had diminished nor encreased the treasure,<br>
 Wherewith his father had endowed that home;<br>
 And he more distant would Paris deem<br>
 Than Tanais another would esteem;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 “And that a greater difficulty were<br>
 To tear Jocundo from his consort; who<br>
 Was by such love united to that fair,<br>
 No other will but hers the husband knew:<br>
 Yet at his sovereign’s hest he would repair<br>
 To seek the stripling, and his utmost do.<br>
 The suit with offers and with gifts was crowned,<br>
 Which for that youth’s refusal left no ground.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 “Faustus set forth, and, after few days’ ride,<br>
 Reached Rome, and his paternal mansion gained:<br>
 There with entreaties so the brother plied,<br>
 He to that journey his consent obtained;<br>
 And wrought so well (though difficult to guide)<br>
 Silent even young Jocundo’s wife remained;<br>
 He showing her what good would thence ensue,<br>
 Besides what gratitude would be her due.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 “Jocundo names a time to wend his way,<br>
 And servingmen meanwhile purveys and steeds;<br>
 And a provision makes of fair array;<br>
 For beauty borrows grace from glorious weeds.<br>
 Beside him or about him, night and day,<br>
 Aye weeping, to her lord the lady reads;<br>
 She knows not how she ever can sustain<br>
 So long an absence, and not die with pain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 “For the mere thought produced such misery,<br>
 It seemed from her was ravished her heart’s core.<br>
 —‘Alas! my love (Jocundo cried) let be<br>
 Thy sorrows’—weeping with her evermore—<br>
 ‘So may this journey prosper! as to thee<br>
 Will I return ere yet two months are o’er;<br>
 Nor by a day o’erpass the term prescribed,<br>
 Though me the king with half his kingdom bribed.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 “This brought his troubled consort small content:<br>
 She that the period was too distant said,<br>
 And that ’twould be a mighty wonderment,<br>
 If her, at his return, he found not dead.<br>
 The grief which, day and night, her bosom rent,<br>
 Was such, that lady neither slept nor fed:<br>
 So that for pity oft the youth repented<br>
 He to his brother’s wishes had consented.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 “She from her neck unloosed a costly chain<br>
 That a gemmed cross and holy reliques bore;<br>
 Which one, a pilgrim of Bohemia’s reign,<br>
 Had gathered upon many a distant shore;<br>
 Him did her sire in sickness entertain,<br>
 Returning from Jerusalem of yore;<br>
 And hence was made that dying pilgrim’s heir:<br>
 This she undoes, and gives her lord to wear;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 “And round his neck entreats him, for her sake,<br>
 That chain in memory of herself to wind:<br>
 Her gift the husband is well pleased to take;<br>
 Not that a token needs his love to bind:<br>
 For neither time, nor absence, e’er will shake,<br>
 Nor whatsoever fortune is behind,<br>
 Her memory, which, rooted fast and deep,<br>
 He still has kept, and after death will keep.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 “The night before that morning streaked the sky,<br>
 Fixt for his journey, to his sore dismay,<br>
 Her husband deemed that in his arms would die<br>
 The wife from whom he was to wend his way.<br>
 She slumbered not: to her a last goodbye<br>
 He bade, while yet it lacked an hour of day,<br>
 Mounted his nag, and on his journey sped;<br>
 While his afflicted spouse returned to bed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 “Jocundo was not two miles on his road,<br>
 When he that jewelled cross recalled to mind;<br>
 Which he beneath his pillow had bestowed,<br>
 And, through forgetfulness, had left behind.<br>
 ‘Alas! (the youth bethought him) in what mode<br>
 Shall I excuse for my omission find,<br>
 So that from this my consort shall not deem<br>
 I little her unbounded love esteem?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 “He pondered an excuse; then weened ’twould be<br>
 Of little value, if it were exprest<br>
 By page or other—save his embassy<br>
 He did himself; his brother he addrest;<br>
 ‘—Now to Baccano ride you leisurely,<br>
 And there at the first inn set-up your rest;<br>
 For I must back to Rome without delay;<br>
 But trust to overtake you by the way.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 “ ‘No other but myself my need could do.<br>
 Doubt not but I shall speedily be back.’<br>
 —No servant took he, but, with an adieu,<br>
 Jocundo, at a trot, wheeled round his hack,<br>
 And when that cavalier the stream was through,<br>
 The rising sun ’gan chase the dusky rack.<br>
 At home he lighted, sought his bed, and found<br>
 The consort he had quitted sleeping sound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 “He, without saying aught, the curtains drew,<br>
 And, what he least believed, within espied;<br>
 For he beneath the quilt, his consort true<br>
 And chaste, saw sleeping at a stripling’s side.<br>
 Forthwith Jocundo that adulterer knew,<br>
 By practice, of his features certified,<br>
 In that he was a footboy in his train,<br>
 Nourished by him, and come of humble strain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 “To imagine his distress and wonderment,<br>
 And warrant it, that other may believe,<br>
 Is better than to make the experiment,<br>
 And, like this wretch, the cruel proof receive:<br>
 By anger stirred, it was his first intent<br>
 To draw his sword, and both of life bereave;<br>
 But love, which spite himself, he entertained<br>
 For that ungrateful woman, him restrained.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 “You see if like a vassal he obeyed<br>
 This ribald Love, who left him not the force<br>
 To wake her, lest to know her guilt surveyed,<br>
 Should in his consort’s bosom move remorse.<br>
 As best he could, he forth in silence made,<br>
 The stair descended, and regained his horse.<br>
 Goaded by Love, he goads his steed again,<br>
 And ere they reach their inn rejoins his train.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 “His change of mien to all was manifest;<br>
 All saw his heart was heavy; yet not one,<br>
 Mid these, in any sort, the reason guessed,<br>
 Nor read the secret woe which caused his moan;<br>
 All thought he had to Rome his steps addrest,<br>
 Woe to the town, surnamed of horns, had gone.<br>
 That Love has caused the mischief all surmise,<br>
 Though none of them conjectures in what wise.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 “His brother weened he was in grief immersed<br>
 For his deserted wife: he, on his side,<br>
 For other reason, inly chafed and cursed,<br>
 —That she was but too well accompanied.<br>
 Meanwhile, with swelling lips and forehead pursed,<br>
 The ground that melancholy stripling eyed.<br>
 Faustus, who vainly would apply relief,<br>
 Ill cheered him, witless what had caused his grief.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 “He for his sore an evil salve had found,<br>
 And, where he should retire, encreased his woes;<br>
 Who, with the mention of his wife, that wound<br>
 Inflamed and opened, which he sought to close.<br>
 He rests not night nor day, in sorrow drowned;<br>
 His appetite is gone, with his repose,<br>
 Ne’er to return; and (whilom of such fame)<br>
 His lovely visage seems no more the same.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 “His eye-balls seem deep-buried in his head,<br>
 His nose seems grown—his cheeks are pined so sore—<br>
 Nor even remains (his beauty so is fled)<br>
 Enough to warrant what he was before.<br>
 Such fever burns him, of his sorrow bred,<br>
 He halts on Arbia’s and on Arno’s shore;<br>
 And, if a charm is left, ’tis faded soon,<br>
 And withered like a rose-bud plucked at noon.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 “Besides that Faustus sorrowed to descry<br>
 Him so bested; worse cause for sorrowing<br>
 Was to that courtier to appear to lie<br>
 Before Astolpho; he was pledged to bring<br>
 One that was fairest deemed in every eye,<br>
 Who must appear the foulest to that king;<br>
 Yet he continued on his way to wend,<br>
 And brought him to Pavia in the end.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 “Not that forthwith he lets the youth be seen,<br>
 Lest him the king of little wit arraign;<br>
 He first by his dispatches lets him ween,<br>
 That thither he Jocundo brings with pain:<br>
 Saying, that of his beauteous air and mien<br>
 Some secret cause of grief had been the bane,<br>
 Accompanied by a distemper sore:<br>
 So that he seemed not what he was before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 “Glad was the monarch, of his coming taught,<br>
 As of a friend’s arrival he could be;<br>
 Since in the universal world was nought,<br>
 That he so much desired as him to see:<br>
 Nor was the Lombard’s king displeased in ought<br>
 To mark his guest’s inferiority;<br>
 Though, but for his misfortune, it was clear,<br>
 He his superior would have been or peer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 “Lodged by him in his palace, every day<br>
 And every hour, the stranger youth he sees,<br>
 Studious to honour him, and bids purvey<br>
 Store of provision for his better ease.<br>
 While still his thoughts to his ill consort stray,<br>
 Jocundo languishes; nor pastimes please<br>
 That melancholy man; nor music’s strain<br>
 One jot diminishes his ceaseless pain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 “Above his chambers, on the upper floor,<br>
 Nearest the roof, there was an ancient hall:<br>
 Thither, in solitary mood, (for sore<br>
 Pastime and company, the stripling gall,)<br>
 He aye betakes himself; while evermore<br>
 Sad thoughts some newer cause of grief recall.<br>
 He here (who would believe the story?) found<br>
 A remedy unhoped, which made him sound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 “At that hall’s farther end, more feebly lighted,<br>
 (For windows ever closed shut out the day)<br>
 Where one wall with another ill united,<br>
 He, through the chink, beheld a brighter ray:<br>
 There laid his eye, and saw, what he had slighted<br>
 As hard to credit, were it but hearsay:<br>
 He hears it not, but this himself descries;<br>
 Yet hardly can believe his very eyes.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 “He of the Queen’s apartment here was sight,<br>
 Her choicest and her priviest chamber, where<br>
 Was never introduced whatever wight,<br>
 Save he most faithful was esteemed: he there,<br>
 As he was peeping, saw an uncouth fight;<br>
 A dwarf was wrestling with the royal fair;<br>
 And such that champion’s skill, though undergrown,<br>
 He in the strife his opposite had thrown.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 “As in a dream, Jocundo stood, beside<br>
 Himself, awhile of sober sense bereaved;<br>
 Nor, but when of the matter certified,<br>
 And sure it was no dream, his sight believed.<br>
 —‘A scorned and crooked monster,’ (then he cried,)<br>
 ‘Is, as her conqueror, by a dame received,<br>
 Wife of the comeliest, of the curtiest wight,<br>
 And greatest monarch; Oh! what appetite!’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 “And he the consort to whom he was wed,<br>
 Her he most used to blame, recalled to mind,<br>
 And, for the stripling taken to her bed,<br>
 To deem the dame less culpable inclined:<br>
 Less of herself than sex the fault he read,<br>
 Which to one man could never be confined:<br>
 And thought, if in one taint all women shared,<br>
 At least his had not with a monster paired.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 “To the same place Jocundo made return,<br>
 At the same hour, upon the following day;<br>
 And, putting on the king the self-same scorn,<br>
 Again beheld that dwarf and dame at play:<br>
 And so upon the next and following morn;<br>
 For—to conclude—they made no holiday:<br>
 While she (what most Jocundo’s wonder moved)<br>
 The pigmy for his little love reproved.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 “One day, amid the rest, the youth surveyed<br>
 The dame disordered and opprest with gloom;<br>
 Having twice summoned, by her waiting-maid,<br>
 The favoured dwarf, who yet delayed to come;<br>
 A third time by the lady sent, she said:<br>
 —‘Engaged at play, Madonna, is the groom,<br>
 Nor, lest he lose a doit, his paltry stake,<br>
 Will that discourteous churl his game forsake.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 “At such strange spectacle, the Roman knight<br>
 Cleared up his brow, his visage and his eyes;<br>
 He jocund, as in name, became in sprite,<br>
 And changed his tears for smiles; with altered guise,<br>
 He waxed ruddy, gay, and plump in plight,<br>
 And seems a cherubim of Paradise.<br>
 So that such change with wonderment all see,<br>
 Brother and king, and royal family.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 “If from the youth Astolpho wished to know<br>
 From whence this sudden light of comfort came,<br>
 No less Jocundo this desired to show,<br>
 And to the king such injury proclaim:<br>
 But willed that like himself he should forego<br>
 Revenge upon the author of that shame.<br>
 Hence, that he might discern her guilt, yet spare,<br>
 He made him on the Agnus Dei swear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 “He made him swear that he, for nothing said,<br>
 Or seen, which might to him displeasing be,<br>
 (Though he, in what he should discover, read<br>
 An outrage offered to his majesty,)<br>
 Would, now or ever, venge him on his head:<br>
 Moreover him he bound to secrecy;<br>
 That the ill doer ne’er, through deed or word,<br>
 Might guess his injured king that case had heard.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 “The monarch, who to every thing beside<br>
 Could better have given credit, freely swore:<br>
 To him the cause Jocundo signified,<br>
 Why he had many days lamenting sore;<br>
 —Because he had his evil wife espied<br>
 In the embraces of a serjeant poor;<br>
 And vowed he should in fine have died of grief,<br>
 If he for longer time had lacked relief.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 “But that within his highness’ palace said,<br>
 He had witnessed what had much appeased his woe;<br>
 For, if foul shame had fallen upon his head,<br>
 At least he was not single; saying so,<br>
 He to that chink the Lombard monarch led,<br>
 Who spied the mannikin of hideous show.<br>
    (Lines 7 &amp; 8 untranslated by Rose)</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 “You may believe he shameless deemed that act,<br>
 Without my swearing it; he, at the sight,<br>
 It seemed, would go distraught,—with fury racked,<br>
 He against every wall his head would smite—<br>
 Would cry aloud—would break the solemn pact,<br>
 Yet kept parforce the promise he had plight;<br>
 And gulped his anger down and bitter scorn;<br>
 Since on the holy water he had sworn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 “Then to Jocundo: ‘What remains to me<br>
 To do in this misfortune, brother, speak;<br>
 Since vengeance with more noted cruelty<br>
 Thou wilt not let me on the sinners wreak.’<br>
 (Jocundo answered) ‘Let these ingrates be;<br>
 And try we if all women are as weak;<br>
 And if the wives of others can be won<br>
 To do what others by our own have done.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 “ ‘Both fair and youthful, measured by this scale,<br>
 Nor easily our equals shall we find;<br>
 What woman but to us shall strike her sail,<br>
 If even to the ugly these are kind?<br>
 At least, if neither youth nor grace avail,<br>
 The money may, with which our bags are lined;<br>
 Nor will I that we homeward more return,<br>
 Ere the chief spoils we from a thousand earn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 “ ‘Long absence, seeing with a distant part,<br>
 Converse with different women, oft allay,<br>
 As it would seem, the troubles of a heart,<br>
 Whereof Love’s angry passions make their prey.’<br>
 The king is pleased to hear the youth impart<br>
 This counsel, nor his journey will delay:<br>
 Thence on their road, with but two squires beside,<br>
 He and the Roman knight together ride.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 “Disguised they go through France and Italy,<br>
 They Flanders next and England scower, and where<br>
 A woman they of lovely visage spy,<br>
 Aye find the dame complaint with their prayer.<br>
 They upon some bestow what others buy,<br>
 And oft replaced their squandered treasures are.<br>
 Our travellers to the wives of many sued,<br>
 And by as many other dames were wooed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 “By solid proof those comrades ascertain,<br>
 Here tarrying for a month, and there for two,<br>
 That their own wives are of no other vein<br>
 Than those of others, and as chast and true.<br>
 After some season, wearied are the twain<br>
 With ever running after something new:<br>
 For, without risk of death, thus evermore<br>
 The intruders ill could enter other’s door.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 “—’Twere best to find a girl whose natural bent<br>
 And face to both of us should pleasing be;<br>
 A girl, that us in common might content,<br>
 Nor we in her find cause for jealousy;<br>
 And wherefore wouldst thou that I should lament<br>
 More than with other, to go halves with thee?’<br>
 (Exclaimed Astolpho) ‘well I know is none,<br>
 Of all the female sex, content with one.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 “ ‘One damsel that in nought shall us constrain,<br>
 —Then only, when disposed to please the fair—<br>
 Will we in peace and pleasure entertain,<br>
 Nor we, about her, have dispute or care.<br>
 Nor, deem I, she with reason could complain:<br>
 For if two fell to every other’s share,<br>
 Better than one might she keep faith with two;<br>
 Nor haply we such frequent discord view.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 “Much seems the king’s proposal to content<br>
 The Roman youth; and thus it is, the twain,<br>
 To execute Astolpho’s project bent,<br>
 Journey by many a hill and many a plain;<br>
 And find at last, well fitting their intent,<br>
 The daughter of a publican of Spain,<br>
 Of presence and of manners framed to win;<br>
 Whose father at Valencia kept his inn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 As yet, upon the bloom of spring, the maid<br>
 Was a fresh flower that scarce began to blow:<br>
 Her sire with many children was o’erlaid,<br>
 And was to poverty a mortal foe.<br>
 Hence ’tis an easy matter to persuade<br>
 Mine host his buxom daughter to forego,<br>
 And let them, where they will the damsel bear;<br>
 In that to treat her well the travellers swear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
    (Lines 1-6 untranslated by Rose)<br>
 They to Zattiva come upon the day<br>
 That from Valencia they had bent their way.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 “The travellers from their inn to street and square<br>
 And places, public and divine, resort;<br>
 Who, wheresoever they had made repair,<br>
 Themselves were so accustomed to disport,<br>
 The girl is with the valets left in care,<br>
 Who make the beds, and wearied hackneys sort:<br>
 While others in the hostel-kitchen dight<br>
 The meal against their lords’ return at night.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 “As groom, a stripling in the hostel plied,<br>
 Who in the other landlord’s house had been:<br>
 He, from her childhood at the damsel’s side,<br>
 Had joyed her love: they, without change of mien,<br>
 On meeting, closely one another eyed,<br>
 Since either apprehended to be seen:<br>
 But when alone—now left together—raised<br>
 Their eyelids and on one another gazed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 “The stripling asked her whitherward they sped,<br>
 And of the two which claimed her as his right;<br>
 This, point by point, to him Flammetta read;<br>
 Flammetta she, the Greek that boy was hight.<br>
 ‘—When I had hoped the time was coming,’ said<br>
 The Greek—‘that I should live with thee, my light,<br>
 Flammetta, thou, alas! art lost to me,<br>
 Nor know I if I more thy face shall see.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 “ ‘I to the bitter dregs the cup must drain<br>
 Of promised sweets; since thou art others’ prey.<br>
 ’Twas my design, having with mickle pain<br>
 And labour sore, some money put away,<br>
 Which I had hoarded out of frequent gain<br>
 From parting guests, and from my yearly pay,<br>
 To seek again Valencia, and demand<br>
 Thee from thy sire in lawful wedlock’s band.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 “The damsel shrugs her shoulders, and complains;<br>
 And—that he is too late—is her reply.<br>
 The Greek laments and sobs, and partly feigns:<br>
 ‘—Wilt thou (he answered her) thus let me die?<br>
 Let me, at least, exhale my amorous pains!<br>
 Let me, but once, in thine embrace lie!<br>
 For every moment in thy presence spent,<br>
 Ere thou depart, will make me die content.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 “To him the damsel, full of pity, cries:<br>
 ‘Believe, I covet this no less than thee;<br>
 But here, surrounded by so many eyes,<br>
 Is neither time nor opportunity.’<br>
 ‘—I feel assured’ (to her that youth replied)<br>
 ‘Were I beloved by you, as you by me,<br>
 This very night you would find out a place<br>
 Wherein to solace us some little space.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
    (Stanza LXI untranslated by Rose)</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 “She bade him come—when she awhile had thought—<br>
 When he believed that all asleep were laid;<br>
 And how by him her chamber should be sought,<br>
 And how he should return, at full, displaid.<br>
 The cautious stripling did as he was taught,<br>
 And, when he found all silent, thither made:<br>
 He pushed, till it gave way, the chamber-door,<br>
 And, upon tiptoes, softly paced the floor.<br>
 
         LXIII - LXX<br>
    (Stanzas LXIII - LXX untranslated by Rose)</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 “Gazing on one another, with surprise,<br>
 The monarch and Jocundo are confused;<br>
 Nor even to have heard a case surmise<br>
 Of two, that ever thus had been abused:<br>
 Then laughed so, that they sate with winking eyes,<br>
 And open mouth, and lungs which breath refused;<br>
 And, wearied with the mirth her tale had bred,<br>
 Fell backwards, both, exhausted on the bed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 “When they had laughed so loud a laugh, the dew<br>
 Stood in their eyes, and each with aching breast<br>
 Remained, the pair exclaimed: ‘What shall we do<br>
 In order not to be a woman’s jest?<br>
 Since we, with all our heed, between us two,<br>
 Could not preserve the one by us possest,<br>
 A husband, furnished with more eyes than hair,<br>
 Perforce must be betrayed with all his care.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 “ ‘A thousand, beauteous all, have we found kind,<br>
 Nor one of those so many has stood fast.<br>
 If tried, all women we by proof should find<br>
 Like these; but be the experiment our last.<br>
 Then we may deem our own not worse inclined<br>
 Than are the wives of others, and as chaste:<br>
 And, if like others we our own discern,<br>
 I hold it best that we to them return.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 “When they have come to this resolve, they, through<br>
 Flammetta, call the youth into their bower;<br>
 And with the girl her leman, in the view<br>
 Of many, gift, and add a fitting dower.<br>
 They mount, and to the east their way pursue,<br>
 Accustomed westward hitherto to scower;<br>
 To their deserted wives again repair,<br>
 Nor of their after deeds take farther care.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 Here paused mine host; to whom on every side<br>
 His audience had with careful heed attended.<br>
 Rodomont listened, nor a word replied,<br>
 Until the landlord’s story was suspended.<br>
 Then—“Fully I believe,” that paynim cried,<br>
 “The tale of women’s frauds would ne’er be ended;<br>
 Nor could that man in any volume note<br>
 The thousandth part, who would their treasons quote.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 Of sounder judgement, ’mid that company,<br>
 There was an elder, one more wise and bold;<br>
 That undefended so the sex to see,<br>
 Was inly wroth, and could no longer hold:<br>
 To the relater of that history<br>
 He turned; and, “Many things we have been told”<br>
 (Exclaimed that ancient) “wherein truth is none,<br>
 And of such matters is thy fable one.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 “Him I believe not, that told this truth to you,<br>
 Though in all else he gospel-truths exprest;<br>
 As less by his experience, than untrue<br>
 Conceit respecting women prepossest.<br>
 The malice which he bears to one or two,<br>
 Makes him unjustly hate and blame the rest.<br>
 But you shall hear him, if his wrath o’erblow,<br>
 Yet greater praise than blame on these bestow.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 “And he a larger field for speaking well<br>
 Will find, than blaming womankind withal;<br>
 And of a hundred worthy fame may tell,<br>
 For one whose evil deeds for censure call.<br>
 He should exalt the many that excel,<br>
 Culled from the multitude, not rail at all,<br>
 If otherwise your friend Valerio said,<br>
 He was by wrath, and not by reason, led.<br>
 
         LXXIX - LXXXIII<br>
    (Stanzas LXXIX - LXXXIII untranslated by Rose)</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 So reasoning, that just elder and sincere,<br>
 With ready instances, supports his creed;<br>
 Showing there many women are who ne’er<br>
 Sinned against chastity, in word or deed:<br>
 But him with impious visage and severe<br>
 The paynim scared, ill pleased the truth to read.<br>
 So that, through fear, he further speech forbore,<br>
 But changed not therefore aught his former lore.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 Having stopt further question in this wise,<br>
 The paynim monarch from the table rose:<br>
 Then lays him on his bed, till from the skies<br>
 The dusky shades depart, and morning glows:<br>
 But spends a larger part of night in sighs<br>
 At his liege-lady’s sin, than in repose.<br>
 Rodomont thence departs at dawn of day,<br>
 Resolved by water to pursue his way.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 For with such care for his good horse’s plight,<br>
 As is becoming a good cavalier,<br>
 The courser fair and good, made his in spite<br>
 Of young Rogero and Circassia’s peer;<br>
 Seeing he, for two days, that horse’s might<br>
 Had taxed too hardly in his long career,<br>
 —As well he for his ease embarked the steed,<br>
 As to pursue his way with better speed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 He straight makes launch the vessel from the marge,<br>
 And bids put forth the oars from either side:<br>
 Nor big nor deeply laden, she, at large,<br>
 Descends the Saone, transported by the tide.<br>
 Care never quits him, though the shifting barge<br>
 The king ascend, or nimble horse bestride:<br>
 This he encounters aye on prow or poop,<br>
 And bears behind him on his courser’s croup;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 Rather within his head or heart always<br>
 Care sits; whence every comfort is o’erthrown:<br>
 No remedy the wretched man surveys,<br>
 In that his enemies are in the town.<br>
 From others hope is none; since they who raise<br>
 This fearful war against him, are his own:<br>
 Vext by that cruel one, aye night and day,<br>
 Whom he might hope to find his natural stay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 Rodomont navigates the day and night<br>
 Ensuing, aye by heavy thoughts opprest;<br>
 Nor can he ever banish the despite,<br>
 Suffered from King and Lady, from his breast.<br>
 The self-same grief sate heavy on his sprite<br>
 Aboard the bark, as when his steed he prest.<br>
 Such fire was not by water to be drowned,<br>
 Nor he his nature changed by changing ground.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 As the sick man who with a fever grows,<br>
 And, weak and weary, shifts his place in vain,<br>
 Whether he right or left himself bestows,<br>
 And hopes in turning some relief to gain,<br>
 Finds neither on this side nor that repose,<br>
 But everywhere encounters equal pain;<br>
 The pagan monarch so found small relief,<br>
 By land or water, for his secret grief.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 Rodomont brooked no more aboard to stay,<br>
 But bade them land him, and by Lyons hied;<br>
 By Vienne and Valence next took his way,<br>
 And the rich bridge in Avignon descried.<br>
 For these and more, which ’twixt the river lay<br>
 And Celtiberian hills upon that side,<br>
 (Theirs, from the day they conquered the champaigne)<br>
 Obeyed the kings of Afric and of Spain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 To pass to Afric straight, the cavalier<br>
 Kept to the right, towards Acquamorta’s shore,<br>
 And lighted on a stream and hamlet, dear<br>
 To Ceres and to Bacchus, which that Moor<br>
 Found quitted by the peasants, in their fear,<br>
 As often by the soldier harried sore.<br>
 The beach upon one side broad ocean laved,<br>
 And on the other yellow harvests waved.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 Here, newly built upon a hillock’s crest,<br>
 A little church the Saracen espied;<br>
 Abandoned by its priesthood, like the rest,<br>
 For war was flaming upon every side.<br>
 Rodomont of this place himself possest;<br>
 Which, from its site, as well as lying wide<br>
 Of fields, from whence he tidings loathed to hear,<br>
 So pleased him, he for it renounced Argier.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 He changed his scheme of seeking Afric’s land,<br>
 (So this fair spot seemed fit for his behoof!)<br>
 And here housed carriages, and steed, and band,<br>
 Together with himself, beneath one roof,<br>
 At few leagues’ distance, did Montpelier stand,<br>
 And other wealthy towns, not far aloof.<br>
 The village was upon a river’s side,<br>
 So that its every need might be supplied.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 Here standing, full of thought, upon a day,<br>
 (Such was his common wont) the paynim spied,<br>
 Advancing by a narrow path, which lay<br>
 Through a green meadow, from the adverse side,<br>
 A lovely damsel, that upon her way<br>
 Was by a bearded monk accompanied;<br>
 And these behind them led a lusty steed,<br>
 Who bore a burden, trapt with sable weed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVI.</td><td>
 Who that attendant monk and damsel were,<br>
 And what that burden, will to you be clear,<br>
 Remembering Isabella is the fair,<br>
 Charged with the corse of her Zerbino dear:<br>
 I left her, where from Provence, in the care<br>
 Of that good sire, she bowned herself to steer,<br>
 By whom persuaded, had the lady given<br>
 The remnant of her virtuous life to heaven.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVII.</td><td>
 Although in her pale face and troubled guise,<br>
 The sorrow of that dame is manifest,<br>
 Although two fountains are her streaming eyes,<br>
 And sobs aye issue from her burning breast,<br>
 And more beside of suffering testifies,<br>
 With what a load of grief she is opprest,<br>
 Yet, in her faded cheek such beauties meet,<br>
 Love and the Graces there might fix their seat.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVIII.</td><td>
 As soon as he of Sarza saw appear<br>
 The beauteous dame, he laid the thought aside<br>
 Of hatred to that gentle race and dear,<br>
 By whom alone the world is glorified;<br>
 And best by Isabel the cavalier<br>
 Believed his former love would be supplied,<br>
 And one love by another be effaced,<br>
 As bolt by bolt in timber is displaced.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIX.</td><td>
 Her with the kindest mien and mildest tone<br>
 That he could fashion, met the Sarzan knight;<br>
 To whom the dame her every thought made known;<br>
 And said, when she was questioned of her plight,<br>
 She would with holy works—this world forgone—<br>
 Seek favour in her Heavenly Father’s sight.<br>
 Loud laughed that godless paynim at the thought,<br>
 Who every faith and worship held at nought;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">C.</td><td>
 And said that she from reason wandered wide,<br>
 And termed her project sudden and unsound;<br>
 Nor deemed her less to blame than those who hide,<br>
 Through greediness, their treasure under ground,<br>
 And keep it from the use of all beside,<br>
 Though hence no profit to themselves redound.<br>
 Rightly were prisoned lion, snake, and bear,<br>
 But ill whate’er is innocent and fair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CI.</td><td>
 The monk, that to this talk has lent an ear,<br>
 Prompt with advice that mournful dame to stay,<br>
 And lest she quit her course, prepared to steer<br>
 His bark, like practised pilot, on her way,<br>
 A sumptuous table, rich in spiritual cheer,<br>
 Had speedily bestirred him to array;<br>
 But, born with evil taste, that paynim rude<br>
 No sooner tasted, than he loathed, the food.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CII.</td><td>
 And having interrupted him in vain,<br>
 Nor having power to make him stint his lore,<br>
 That paynim, stirred to fury, broke the rein<br>
 Of patience, and assailed the preacher hoar.<br>
 But haply wearisome might seem the strain,<br>
 If I upon this theme dilated more:<br>
 So here I close, nor words will idly spend,<br>
 Admonished by that ancient’s evil end.</td></tr>
