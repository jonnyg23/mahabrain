 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 32</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 To her that does for her Rogero stay,<br>
 Tidings are brought which irk the damsel sore,<br>
 That fair Marphisa caused the youth’s delay;<br>
 She bent to slay her, grieving evermore,<br>
 Departs, and overtakes, upon the way,<br>
 Ullania with the three kings who rode before.<br>
 These she o’ercomes, and had o’ercome that maid,<br>
 But that an evil law she disobeyed.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 I recollect that I was bound to sing<br>
 (I promised so, but it escaped my mind)<br>
 Of a suspicion, fraught with suffering<br>
 To Bradamant of more displeasing kind,<br>
 And made by keener and more venomed sting<br>
 Than caused that other wound, wherewith she pined,<br>
 Which, hearing Richardet his news impart,<br>
 Had pierced her breast and preyed upon her heart.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 So was I bound to sing, but I begun<br>
 Another song, Rinaldo crossed my way,<br>
 And then those deeds by savage Guido done,<br>
 Kept me employed and caused no small delay;<br>
 And so from subject I to subject run,<br>
 That I forgot of Bradamant to say.<br>
 I now remember, and will tell you, ere<br>
 You of Rinaldo or Gradasso hear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 But it behoves, ere more of these be said,<br>
 I should awhile of Agramant discourse,<br>
 Who had from that night’s raging fire conveyed<br>
 To Arles, the remnant of his scattered force:<br>
 Since to unite his troops, and furnish aid<br>
 And victual, ’twas a place of much resource,<br>
 Seated upon a river, nigh the shore,<br>
 With Spain in front and Africa before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 With horse and foot, of good or evil sort,<br>
 Marsilius throughout Spain their loss repairs;<br>
 And each armed back in Barcellona’s port,<br>
 Furnished through love or fear, for sea prepares.<br>
 The Moor to council daily calls his court;<br>
 Nor care nor cost the watchful monarch spares:<br>
 Meanwhile sore taxes and repeated cess,<br>
 All Africa’s o’erburdened towns oppress.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 He offers Rodomont, if to his side<br>
 He will return, but offers him in vain,<br>
 Renowned Almontes’ daughter, as a bride;<br>
 His cousin she, her portion Oran’s reign.<br>
 He lures not from his bridge that knight of pride,<br>
 Who has so many sells, such plate and chain<br>
 Collected there, from cavaliers o’erthrown,<br>
 As serve to hide the monumental stone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 Marphisa would not such a course pursue:<br>
 Nay, the redoubted damsel hearing said<br>
 That Agramant, subdued by Charles’s crew,<br>
 —His choicest warriors taken, chased, or dead—<br>
 In Arles was sheltered with his broken few,<br>
 Thither, unbidden by the monarch, sped,<br>
 Prompt to assist him with her friendly blade;<br>
 And proffered purse and person in his aid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 As a free gift to him the martial fair<br>
 Brunello bore, nor had she done him wrong.<br>
 He, for ten days and nights, to swing in air,<br>
 Had sorely feared, from lofty gallows hung:<br>
 But seeing him unhelped by force or prayer<br>
 Of any one amid the paynim throng,<br>
 She thought foul scorn to stain her generous hands<br>
 With such base blood, and loosed the losel’s bands.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 She pardoned every ancient injury,<br>
 And him to Agramant in Arles conveyed.<br>
 Well may you fancy with what joy and glee<br>
 The monarch greeted her who brought him aid;<br>
 He in Brunello’s fate wills all shall see<br>
 In what esteem he holds that warlike maid;<br>
 For he in earnest does upon her foe<br>
 What fierce Marphisa menaced but in show.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 The hangman hung his corpse in desert field,<br>
 The craving vulture and the crow to feed.<br>
 Rogero, that erewhile had been his shield,<br>
 And from the noose that caitiff would have freed,<br>
 Heaven’s justice willed, now lay with wound unhealed,<br>
 Nor could assist the craven in his need;<br>
 And when the news were known, the knot was tied;<br>
 So that Brunello, unassisted, died.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 This while does good duke Aymon’s daughter mourn,<br>
 Because those twenty days so slowly trail:<br>
 —Which term elapsed—Rogero should return,<br>
 And be received into her church’s pale.<br>
 Time halts not more with him to foreign bourne<br>
 Exiled, with prisoner pent in noisome jail,<br>
 Pines the poor wretch for liberty and light,<br>
 Or his loved land, desired and gladsome sight!</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 Aye sick with hope deferred, the expecting maid,<br>
 That Phœbus’ steeds were foundered one while deemed;<br>
 Then that his wheels were out of frame, so stayed,<br>
 Beyond the wonted term, his chariot seemed.<br>
 Yet longer than that day when Faith delayed<br>
 The sun, which on the righteous Hebrew beamed,<br>
 Or than that night Alcides was conceived,<br>
 She every day and every night believed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 How oft of dormouse, badger, or of bear,<br>
 The heavy slumber would she fain partake!<br>
 For she that time in sleep would waste and wear;<br>
 Nor such prolonged repose desired to break;<br>
 Nor wished the damsel any sound to hear,<br>
 Until Rogero’s voice should her awake:<br>
 But not alone is this beyond her power;<br>
 She cannot close her eyes one single hour.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 She here and there, throughout the livelong night,<br>
 Tosses and turns, nor ever finds repose;<br>
 And still, impatient for the dawn of light,<br>
 From time to time she to her window goes,<br>
 To see if Tithon’s spouse the lily white<br>
 Yet scatters mingled with the crimson rose.<br>
 Nor less desires the damsel, when ’tis morn,<br>
 To see the golden stars the heaven adorn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 When, saving some four days, the term was ended,<br>
 Appointed for the youthful warrior’s stay,<br>
 She, full of hope, the messenger attended<br>
 From hour to hour, that should arrive, and say,<br>
 “Behold Rogero comes”; and oft ascended<br>
 A turret, from whose top she might survey<br>
 Gay champaign, wood, and, mid the wide expanse,<br>
 A portion of the road, that led to France.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 When shining arms at distance she perceives,<br>
 Or any thing that speaks a cavalier,<br>
 ’Tis her desired Rogero, she believes;<br>
 And her fair eyes and brows are seen to clear.<br>
 If footman, or unarmed, the maid conceives,<br>
 It is a courier from the youthful peer;<br>
 And, though fallacious every hope she feeds,<br>
 Another and another aye succeeds.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 And then she arms, and will the warrior meet;<br>
 And from the hill descends into the plain:<br>
 She finds him not, and to Montalban’s seat<br>
 Hopes he by other road his way has ta’en.<br>
 In the design, wherewith she moved her feet<br>
 From thence, she to her fort returns in vain;<br>
 Nor finds him here nor there; meanwhile expired<br>
 The period whose approach she so desired.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 —The period so prefixt o’erpast by one,<br>
 By two, three, six, by eight, by twenty days—<br>
 She seeing not her spouse, and tidings none<br>
 Receiving of the youth, laments ’gan raise,<br>
 Which had from snake-haired Furies pity won,<br>
 In those dark realms that Rhadamanthus sways.<br>
 She smote her eyes divine, and bosoms fair;<br>
 She rent the tresses of her golden hair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 “Can it be true?”—(she cried)—“Shall I be fain<br>
 To follow one, that strives to hide and fly?<br>
 Esteem a man that has me in disdain?<br>
 Pray him that never hears my suppliant cry?<br>
 Suffer who hates me o’er my heart to reign?<br>
 One that his lofty virtues holds so high,<br>
 ’Twere need some heaven-born goddess should descend<br>
 From realms above, his stubborn heart to bend?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 “Proud youth! he knows my worship and my love,<br>
 Nor me will have for lover or for slave.<br>
 The cruel stripling knows what pangs I prove,<br>
 Yet will not aid me till I am in my grave.<br>
 Nor let me tell my sorrows, lest they move<br>
 Him his perverse and evil will to wave;<br>
 Shunning me like malignant asp, that fears<br>
 To change his mood, if he the charmer hears.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 “Ah! Love, arrest this wight who runs so free,<br>
 Outstripping my slow feet, or me install<br>
 In the condition whence thou tookest me,<br>
 Such as I was, ere thine or other’s thrall.<br>
 —Alas! how vain the hope! that thou shouldst be<br>
 Ever to pity moved by suppliant call,<br>
 Who sport, yea feed and live, in streams that rise<br>
 From the distracted lover’s brimming eyes.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 “But, woe is me, alas! and, what can I<br>
 Save my irrational desire lament?<br>
 Which makes me soar a pitch so passing high,<br>
 I reach a region, where my plumes are brent;<br>
 Then, unsustained, fall headlong from the sky;<br>
 Nor ends my woe; on other flight intent,<br>
 Again I imp my wings, again I soar;<br>
 To flame and fall, tormented evermore.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 “Yea; rather of myself should I complain,<br>
 Than the desire, to which I bared my breast<br>
 Whereby was Reason hunted from her reign,<br>
 And all my powers by stronger force opprest.<br>
 Thus borne from bad to worse, without a rein,<br>
 I cannot the unbridled beast arrest;<br>
 Who makes me see I to destruction haste,<br>
 That I more bitterness in death may taste.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 “Yet, ah! why blame myself? Wherein have I<br>
 Ever offended, save in loving thee?<br>
 What wonder was it then that suddenly<br>
 A woman’s feeble sense opprest should be?<br>
 Why fence and guard myself, lest bearing high,<br>
 Wise words, and beauty rare should pleasure me?<br>
 Most wretched is the mortal that would shun<br>
 To look upon the visage of the sun.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 “Besides that me my destiny entrained,<br>
 Words, worthy credence, moved me much, that drew<br>
 A picture of rare happiness, ordained<br>
 As meed of this fair unless to ensue.<br>
 If these persuasive words were false and feigned,<br>
 If famous Merlin’s counsel was untrue,<br>
 Wrath at the wizard may I well profess;<br>
 But cannot therefore love Rogero less.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 “Both Merlin and Melissa have I need<br>
 To blame, and shall for ever blame the twain,<br>
 That, to exhibit suckers of my seed,<br>
 Conjured up spirits from infernal reign,<br>
 Who with this empty hope my fancy feed,<br>
 Me in perpetual bondage to detain.<br>
 Nor other cause for this can I suppose,<br>
 Save that they grudge me safe and sweet repose.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 Sorrow the maid so wholly occupies,<br>
 Room has she none for comfort or for rest.<br>
 Yet, maugre her affliction, Hope will rise,<br>
 And form a lodgement in her harassed breast;<br>
 And to the damsel’s memory still supplies<br>
 Rogero’s parting words to her addrest;<br>
 So makes her, in all seeming facts’ despite,<br>
 Await from hour to hour the youthful knight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 For a month’s space beyond those twenty days<br>
 This hope affords fair Bradamant content:<br>
 Hence sorrow not on her so heavy weighs<br>
 As it would else her harassed soul have shent.<br>
 She, one day that along the road she strays,<br>
 By which she oft to meet Rogero went,<br>
 Hears tidings, that of Hope—last comfort left—<br>
 (Like every other good) her breast bereft.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 Bound homeward from the hostile camp, where lay<br>
 King Agramant, she met a Gascon knight,<br>
 A prisoner to those paynims, from the day,<br>
 That fought nigh Paris was the famous fight.<br>
 The damsel prest him all he knew to say:<br>
 Then to the point she covets led the knight:<br>
 Asks of Rogero, on that theme abides,<br>
 Listens to that, not aught inquires besides.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 Of him a full account did he afford,<br>
 As well acquainted with the court; he said<br>
 How, matched with Mandricardo, strove that lord,<br>
 And layed the martial king in combat dead.<br>
 And how, sore wounded by the Tartar’s sword,<br>
 Above a month the stripling kept his bed:<br>
 And had the stranger here but closed his news,<br>
 Well might his tale the missing knight excuse.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 But then subjoins the Gascon cavalier<br>
 How in the Moorish camp a damsel lies,<br>
 By name Marphisa hight, of beauteous cheer,<br>
 Bold and as skilled in arms of every guise,<br>
 Who loves Rogero and to him is dear;<br>
 And then the host so rarely sundered spies,<br>
 That every one, throughout the paynim train,<br>
 Deems that betrothed in wedlock are the twain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 And hope, when healed shall be the youthful knight,<br>
 The marriage of those lovers will succeed;<br>
 (For sure) with pleasure and sincere delight,<br>
 Those tidings paynim prince and monarch read:<br>
 Since, knowing either’s superhuman might,<br>
 They augur, from their loins will spring a breed,<br>
 In little season, which shall pass in worth<br>
 The mightiest race that ever was on earth.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 What he rehearsed, the Gascon knight believed,<br>
 Nor without cause believed the news he bore,<br>
 A rumour universally received<br>
 And bruited through the squadrons of the Moor;<br>
 Who had that notion of their love conceived<br>
 From signs of kindness witnessed evermore.<br>
 For—good or bad—though from one mouth it flows,<br>
 Fame to a boundless torrent quickly grows.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 That she with him had brought the Paynim aid,<br>
 And ne’er was seen without the cavalier,<br>
 The first foundation of the rumour layed:<br>
 But what confirmed that fame in every ear,<br>
 Was, that she, having from the camp conveyed<br>
 The thief Brunello (as I sang whilere)<br>
 As if alone to see Rogero brought,<br>
 Had to the camp returned, uncalled, unsought.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 She solely to the camp had ta’en her way,<br>
 To visit him that on a sick-bed smarted;<br>
 Nor once alone; but often all the day<br>
 There passed that maid, and but at eve departed:<br>
 Who gave yet greater cause of her to say,<br>
 That—known as one so haughty and hard-hearted,<br>
 Who all the world despised—she now was grown<br>
 Benign and humble to the Child alone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 When Bradamant the Gascon’s story heard,<br>
 That lady suffered such tormenting pain,<br>
 Such cruel woe her inmost bosom stirred,<br>
 From falling she preserved herself with pain.<br>
 She turned her courser round, without a word,<br>
 Inflamed with jealousy and fierce disdain:<br>
 From her all hope the wretched damsel spurns,<br>
 And to her chamber breathing wrath returns.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 Turned on her face, her body on the bed,<br>
 Armed as she is, th4e grieving damsel throws,<br>
 And that the sad lament by sorrow bred,<br>
 May be unheard of any, bites the clothes;<br>
 And so, repeating what the stranger said,<br>
 To such a pitcher her smothered anguish grows,<br>
 Her plaints no longer able to restrain,<br>
 So vents the maid parforce her piteous pain:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 “Who ever can be trusted? woe is me!<br>
 All false and cruel well may be esteemed,<br>
 If thou, Rogero, false and cruel be,<br>
 That I so pious and so faithful deemed.<br>
 What foul and felon act, what treachery,<br>
 Was ever yet by tragic poet dreamed,<br>
 But will fall short of thine, if thou wilt set<br>
 The sum of my desert, against thy debts?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 “Wherefore, Rogero, since no cavalier<br>
 Mates thee in beauteous form and daring feat,<br>
 Since thou in matchless valour hast no peer,<br>
 And none with thee in gentleness compete,<br>
 Why cannot we, ’mid godlike gifts and clear,<br>
 Allow thee truth, thy graces to complete?<br>
 The praise of spotless truth to thee allow,<br>
 To which all other virtues yield and bow?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 “Knowest thou not, without it, worthless are<br>
 All gentle bearing and all martial might?<br>
 As there is nothing, howsoever fair,<br>
 That can be seen without the aid of light.<br>
 Easily mightest thou a maid ensnare,<br>
 Lord as thou was, and idol in her sight.<br>
 Her with thy honied words thou might’st have won,<br>
 To deem that cold and darksome was the sun.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 “Cruel, what sin can trouble thee, if thou<br>
 Do’st not her murder who loved thee repent?<br>
 If held so lightly be a breach of vow—<br>
 Beneath what burden will thy heart be bent?<br>
 What treatment will thine adversary know,<br>
 If one who loves like me thou so torment?<br>
 Justice is none in heaven, I well may say,<br>
 If Heaven its vengeance for my wrongs delay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 “If of all human sins of deepest dye<br>
 Be fell ingratitude; if doomed to smart<br>
 For this, the fairest angel of the sky<br>
 Was banished into foul and darksome part;<br>
 If mighty sins for mighty vengeance cry,<br>
 Where due atonement cleanses not the heart;<br>
 Beware lest thou beneath such vengeance groan,<br>
 Ingrate! that wouldest not thy sin atone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 “Cruel Rogero, I of theft, beside<br>
 All other sins, may justly thee arraign.<br>
 That thou my heart has ravished form my side,<br>
 —Of this offence I will not, I complain—<br>
 But, having made it mine, that thou defied<br>
 All right, and took away thy gift again.<br>
 Restore it; well thou know’st what pains requite<br>
 His sin, who keeps what is another’s right.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 “Thou hast left me, Rogero; thee to leave,<br>
 Alas! I neither will nor power possess.<br>
 But will and power have I my life to reave,<br>
 To scape from this o’erwhelming wretchedness.<br>
 To die at strife with thee alone I grieve:<br>
 For, had the gods so pleased my lot to bless,<br>
 As to require my life, when loved of thee,<br>
 Never so welcome had been death to me.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 Resolved to die, ’twas so the damsel cried;<br>
 And starting from her bed, by passion warmed,<br>
 To her left breast her naked sword applied;<br>
 Then recollected she was wholly armed.<br>
 Meanwhile her better Spirit, at her side,<br>
 With these persuasive words her fury charmed:<br>
 “O lady, born to such illustrious name!<br>
 Would’st thou conclude thy life with such foul shame?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 “Were it not better to the field to go,<br>
 Where aye thy breath with glory may be spent?<br>
 There, should Rogero chance to lay thee low,<br>
 He to have slain thee haply may repent;<br>
 But, should his faulchion deal the mortal blow,<br>
 What death could ever yield thee more content?<br>
 Reason it were thou should’st by him be slain,<br>
 Who dooms thee living to such passing pain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 “Haply of that Marphisa, too, before<br>
 Thou die, thou yet may deadly vengeance take,<br>
 Who with dishonest love and treacherous lore<br>
 Did thy beloved Rogero’s fealty shake.”<br>
 This seemed to please the mournful lady more<br>
 Than her first thought; and she forthwith bade make<br>
 A mantle for her arms, which should imply<br>
 Her desperation and desire to die.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 The vest is of that colour which is spied<br>
 In leaf, when gray and yellow are at strife;<br>
 When it is gathered from the branch, or dried<br>
 Is the green blood, that was it’s parent’s life.<br>
 Embroidered is the surcoat’s outer side<br>
 With stems of cypress which disdain the knife;<br>
 Which shoot not, when by biting steel laid low.<br>
 A habit well according with her woe.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 She took the courser that was wont to bear<br>
 Astolpho, and with him the lance of gold,<br>
 By whose sole touch unhorsed all champions were.<br>
 Needless anew I deem it to unfold<br>
 Why by Astolpho given, and when and where,<br>
 Or how that spear obtained the warrior bold.<br>
 The lady took the lance, but nothing guessed<br>
 Of the stupendous virtue it possessed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 Without attendants, without squire, alone,<br>
 The hill descending by the nearest way,<br>
 Toward Paris is the mournful damsel gone,<br>
 Where camped erewhile the Moorish forces lay;<br>
 For yet to her the tidings were unknown,<br>
 That good Rinaldo and his bold array<br>
 Had raised, with Charles’ and Malagigi’s aid,<br>
 The siege the paynims had to Paris laid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 —Cadurci, and Cahors city left behind—<br>
 Bradamant sees the mountain, far and near,<br>
 Whence Dordogne’s waters to the valley wind;<br>
 And Montferrant’s and Clermont’s towers appear:<br>
 When she, a lady fair, of semblance kind,<br>
 Beholds, by that same road, towards her steer.<br>
 Three knights were nigh, and—at the pommel hung—<br>
 A buckler from the damsel’s saddle swung.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 Before the lady and behind her ride<br>
 More squires and maids, a numerous company.<br>
 Fair Bradamant of one that past beside<br>
 Demanded who the stranger dame might be?<br>
 “That lady to the king of France” (replied<br>
 The squire) “is sent upon an embassy<br>
 From THE LOST ISLE, which lies mid seas that roll<br>
 Their restless waves beyond the northern pole.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 “Some THE LOST ISLE, some Iceland call the reign<br>
 Whereof a royal lady fills the throne;<br>
 Whose charms (before those charms all beauties wane)<br>
 Are such as Heaven had dealt to her alone.<br>
 The shield you see she sends to Charlemagne,<br>
 But with the pact and purpose plainly shown,<br>
 He should confer it on the knight, whose worth<br>
 Is, in his judgment, fairest upon earth.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 “She, as she deems herself (and it is true<br>
 She is the fairest of all womankind),<br>
 A cavalier, that should in heart and thew<br>
 Surpass all other warriors, fain would find;<br>
 Resolved, should her a hundred thousand woo,<br>
 None shall unfix the purpose of her mind;<br>
 —But he, held worthiest by the world’s accord,<br>
 Alone shall be her lover and her lord.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
 “In France, in royal Charles’s famous court,<br>
 The damsel hopes to find the cavalier,<br>
 Who in a thousand feats of high report<br>
 Has shown that he excels each puissant peer.<br>
 All three are monarchy who the dame escort,<br>
 And what their kingdoms ye as well shall hear.<br>
 One Sweden rules, one Gothland, Norway one;<br>
 Surpast in martial praise by few or none.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 “These three, whose kingdoms at some distance lie,<br>
 Yet the least distant lie from the LOST ISLE,<br>
 (Because few mariners its shore descry,<br>
 As little known, that island so they style),<br>
 Wooed and yet woo her for a wife, and vie<br>
 In valour, and, to win the lady’s smile,<br>
 Illustrious deeds have done, which Fame shall sound,<br>
 While Heaven shall circle in its wonted round.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 “Yet she not these will wed, nor cavalier<br>
 That does not, as she deems, all else excell.<br>
 —‘Lightly I hold your proof of valour here,’<br>
 (Those northern monarchs was she wont to tell)<br>
 ‘And if, like sun amid the stars, one peer<br>
 Outshines his fellows, him I honour well:<br>
 But therefore hold him not, in fierce alarms,<br>
 Of living men the bravest knight at arms.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 “ ‘To Charlemagne, whom I esteem and hold<br>
 As wisest among reigning kings, by me<br>
 Shall be dispatched a costly shield of gold,<br>
 On pact and on condition, that it be<br>
 Bestowed on him, deemed boldest of the bold,<br>
 Amid the martial ranks of chivalry.<br>
 Serves the king Charlemagne or other lord,<br>
 I will be governed by that king’s award.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 “ ‘If when King Charles the buckler shall receive<br>
 And give to one so stout, that best among<br>
 All others he that warrior shall believe,<br>
 Do they to his or other court belong.<br>
 For me the golden buckler shall retrieve<br>
 One of you three, in his own virtue strong;<br>
 My every love and thought shall he possess;<br>
 Him for my spouse and lord will I confess.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 “Moved by these stirring speeches, hither hie<br>
 From that wide-distant sea, those monarchs bold,<br>
 Resolved to win the buckler, or to die<br>
 Beneath his hand who has that shield of gold.”<br>
 Bradamant ponders much the squire’s reply:<br>
 He give his horse the head—his story told—<br>
 And plies him so with restless heel and hand,<br>
 He overtakes the damsel’s distant band.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 After him gallops not, nor hurries ought,<br>
 Bradamant, who pursues her road at ease:<br>
 Much evermore evolving in her thought<br>
 Things that may chance, she finally foresees<br>
 That through the buckler by that damsel brought,<br>
 Will follow strife and boundless enmities,<br>
 Amid king Charles’s peerage and the rest,<br>
 If with that shield he shall reward the best.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
 This grieved the damsel’s heart, but far above<br>
 That grief, the former fear her heard did goad;<br>
 That young Rogero had withdrawn his love<br>
 From her, and on the warlike queen bestowed.<br>
 So buried in the thoughts wherewith she strove,<br>
 Was Bradamant, she heeded nor her road,<br>
 Nor took she care where, at the close of light,<br>
 To find befitting shelter for the night.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 As when from squall, or other chance, a barge<br>
 Drives from the river-side, where late it lay,<br>
 Under no mariner or pilot’s charge,<br>
 The winds and waves at will transport their prey;<br>
 So Rabican with Bradamant, at large,<br>
 —She musing on Rogero—wends his way.<br>
 For thence, by many miles, was distant wide<br>
 That mind which should her courser’s bridle guide.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIII.</td><td>
 She raised her eyes at last, and saw the sun<br>
 Had turned his back on Bocchus’ towers and wall;<br>
 Then, like a cormorant, his journey done,<br>
 Into his nurse’s lap beheld him fall,<br>
 Beyond Marocco; and for her to run<br>
 To tree, for shelter from the rising squall,<br>
 Had been a foolish thought; for now ’gan blow<br>
 A blustering wind, which threatened rain or snow.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIV.</td><td>
 To better speed fair Bradamant aroused<br>
 Her courser, yet but little way did ride,<br>
 When with his flock, which on the champaign browsed,<br>
 Leaving the fields, a shepherd she espied.<br>
 To him where, well or ill, she might be housed,<br>
 —With many instances the maid applied—<br>
 For never house could such ill shelter yield,<br>
 But that in rain ’twere worse to lodge afield.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXV.</td><td>
 To her the shepherd said, “I know of none<br>
 Whereto I could direct you, near at hand.<br>
 At least six leagues are distant all, but one,<br>
 Named TRISTRAM’S TOWER, throughout the neighbouring land.<br>
 But not to all men is the door undone;<br>
 For it behoves that they, with lance in hand,<br>
 Achieve their footing first and the defend,<br>
 Who to be lodged within its walls pretend.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVI.</td><td>
 “If there be room within, to stranger knight<br>
 The castellain gives kindly welcome there:<br>
 But is a lodging claimed by other wight,<br>
 To joust with all new comers makes him swear:<br>
 If none, he need not move; but arms and fight<br>
 He must what stranger thither shall repair;<br>
 And he that worst his warlike arms shall ply,<br>
 Must wander forth beneath the naked sky.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVII.</td><td>
 “If two. three, four, or more, seek shelter, they<br>
 That first arrive, in peace their quarters take.<br>
 Who follows, has a harder game to play;<br>
 For war upon those many must he make.<br>
 So, if one only in that mansion stay,<br>
 He with those two, or more, a lance must break.<br>
 Then with as many others as succeed:<br>
 Thus he what strength he has shall sorely need.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVIII.</td><td>
 “As well, if wife or maid seek that repair,<br>
 (Is she alone, is she accompanied),<br>
 And afterwards another, the most fair<br>
 Is housed; that other must without abide.”<br>
 Bradamant asked the kindly shepherd where<br>
 That castle stood; and he with signs replied<br>
 As well as words, and pointed with his hand<br>
 Where, five or six miles wide, the tower did stand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIX.</td><td>
 Though Rabican’s good paces merit praise,<br>
 To hurry him the damsel had no skill,<br>
 By those so passing foul and broken ways,<br>
 (By season somewhat rainy rendered ill)<br>
 So, as to reach the tower, ere Night o’erlays<br>
 The world, whose every nook dark shadows fill.<br>
 Arrived, that lady finds the portal barred,<br>
 And that she seeks a lodging tells the guard.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXX.</td><td>
 He answers that the place is occupied<br>
 By dame and knight already housed, who, met<br>
 About the fire, in that chill evening-tide,<br>
 Wait till their supper be before them set.<br>
 To him that maid: “The board is not supplied,<br>
 I deem, for them, unless the meal be eat.<br>
 Now, say I wait their coming.” (she pursues,)<br>
 Who know and will observe your castle’s use.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 The guard his message bore, where at their ease<br>
 Reposed the weary cavaliers; his tale<br>
 Not overlikely was those kings to please;<br>
 For cold and peevish blew the wintry gale,<br>
 And now fast fell the rain; yet, forced to seize<br>
 Their arms, they slowly don the martial mail.<br>
 The rest remain within; while they proceed<br>
 Against the damsel, but with little speed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 Three cavaliers they were, of might so tried,<br>
 Few champions but to them in prowess yield,<br>
 The same that she that very day, beside<br>
 The courier maid, encountered in the field,<br>
 They that in Iceland boasted, in their pride,<br>
 To bear away from France the golden shield:<br>
 Who (for they had the martial maid outrode)<br>
 Arrived before her at that lord’s abode.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 In feats of arms few warriors were more stout;<br>
 But she besure will be among those few,<br>
 She, that on no account will wait without,<br>
 Fasting and wet, night’s weary watches through.<br>
 Within from window and from lodge, the rout<br>
 Look forth, and will the joust by moonlight view,<br>
 Which streams from underneath a covering cloud;<br>
 Albeit the furious rain beats fast and loud.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 Such transport as the longing gallant cheers,<br>
 About to seize the stolen fruits of love,<br>
 When, after long delay, the listener hears<br>
 The bold within its socket softly move,<br>
 Such transport cheered her, of those cavaliers<br>
 The prowess and the pith a-fire to prove,<br>
 When now the opened portals she descried,<br>
 And drawbridge dropt, and issuing knights espied.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 When she beheld, how, of the drawbridge clear<br>
 Those knights, together or scarce sundered, came,<br>
 She took her ground; and next in fierce career,<br>
 With flowing bridle, drove the furious dame,<br>
 Levelling against those kings that virtuous spear,<br>
 Her cousin’s gift, which never missed its aim;<br>
 Whose touch each warrior must unseat parforce;<br>
 Yea Mars, should Mars contend in mortal course.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 The king of Sweden, foremost of those knights,<br>
 In falling too is foremost of the train;<br>
 With such surpassing force his helmet smites<br>
 That spear, which never yet was couched in vain.<br>
 Gothland’s good king next meets the maid, and lights<br>
 With feet in air, at distance on the plain.<br>
 The third (unhorsed by Aymon’s beauteous daughter)<br>
 Half buried lies in mire and marshy water.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 When at three strokes she had unhorsed them all,<br>
 Lighting with head on earth and heels in air,<br>
 Retiring from the field, she sought the Hall,<br>
 In search of lodging; but, ere harboured there,<br>
 To issue forth, at whosoever’s call,<br>
 Is, by the warder’s hest, obliged to swear.<br>
 That lord who well had weighed her famous feats,<br>
 The damsel with surpassing honour greets.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 So does by her the lady, that erewhile<br>
 Had thither journeyed, with those monarchs three,<br>
 As I related, sent from the LOST ISLE<br>
 To France’s king, upon an embassy.<br>
 Kind as she is and affable of style,<br>
 She renders back the stranger’s courtesy;<br>
 Rises to welcome her with smiling air,<br>
 And to the fire conducts that warlike fair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIX.</td><td>
 As Bradamant unarms, and first her shield,<br>
 And after puts her polished casque away,<br>
 A caul of shining gold, wherein concealed<br>
 And clustering close, her prisoned tresses lay,<br>
 She with the helmet doffs; and now revealed,<br>
 (While the long locks about her shoulders play,)<br>
 A lovely damsel by that band is seen,<br>
 No fiercer in affray than fair of mien.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXX.</td><td>
 As when the stage’s curtain is uprolled,<br>
 Mid thousand lamps, appears the mimic scene,<br>
 Adorned with arch and palace, pictures, gold,<br>
 And statues; or, as limpid and serene<br>
 The sun his visage, glorious to behold,<br>
 Unveils, emerging from a cloudy screen;<br>
 So when the lady doffs her iron case,<br>
 All paradise seems opened in her face.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXI.</td><td>
 Already so well-grown and widely spread<br>
 Were the bright tresses which the hermit shore,<br>
 These, gathered in a knot, behind her head,<br>
 Though shorter than their wont, the damsel wore;<br>
 And he, that castle’s master, plainly read,<br>
 (Who often had beheld her face before)<br>
 That this was Bradamant; and now he paid<br>
 Yet higher honours to the martial maid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXII.</td><td>
 With modest and with mirthful talk this while,<br>
 Seated about the fire, they feed the ear;<br>
 And in this way the weary time beguile<br>
 Till they are heartened with more solid cheer.<br>
 If new or ancient were his castle’s style,<br>
 (Bradamant asks the courteous cavalier)<br>
 By whom begun, and how it took its rise?<br>
 And thus that castellain to her replies.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIII.</td><td>
 “When Pharamond of France possessed the throne,<br>
 His son, prince Clodion, had a mistress rare;<br>
 And damsel in that ancient age was none<br>
 More graceful, beauteous, or more debonair;<br>
 So loved of Pharamond’s enamoured son,<br>
 That he lost sight no oftener of the fair<br>
 Than Io’s shepherd of his charge whilere:<br>
 For jealous as enamoured was the peer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 “Her in this mansion, which his sire bestowed,<br>
 He kept, and rarely issued from his rest:<br>
 With him were lodged ten cavaliers, allowed<br>
 Through France to be the boldest and the best.<br>
 Hither, while in this castle he abode,<br>
 Sir Tristram and a dame their course addrest:<br>
 Whom from a furious giant, in her need,<br>
 Short time before that gentle knight had freed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 “Sir Tristram and his lady reached the Hall,<br>
 When now the sun had Seville left behind.<br>
 They for admission on the porter call,<br>
 Since they for ten miles round no shelter find,<br>
 But Clodion, that loved much, and was withal<br>
 Sore jealous, was determined in his mind<br>
 No stranger in his keep should ever inn,<br>
 So long as that fair lady lodged therein.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 “When, after long entreaties made in vain,<br>
 The castellain refused to house the knight,<br>
 He said, ‘What supplication cannot gain,<br>
 I hope to make thee do in they despite’;<br>
 And loudly challenged him, with all his train,<br>
 Those ten which he maintained, to bloody fight;<br>
 Offering, with levelled lance and lifted glaive,<br>
 To prove Sir Clodion a discourteous knave;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 “On pact, if he sate fast, and overthrown<br>
 Should be the warder, and his warlike rout,<br>
 He in that castle should be lodged alone,<br>
 And Clodion with his knights remain without.<br>
 Against him goes the king of France’s son,<br>
 At risque of death, to venge that galling flout;<br>
 But falls astound; the rest partake his fate,<br>
 And on the losers Tristram bars the gate.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 “Entering the tower, he finds her harboured there<br>
 Whereof I spake, so dear in Clodion’s eyes;<br>
 Whom SHE had equalled with the loveliest fair,<br>
 Nature, so niggard of such courtesies.<br>
 With her Sir Tristram talks, while fell despair<br>
 Aye racks the houseless prince in horrid wise.<br>
 Who prays the conquering knight, with suppliant cry,<br>
 Not to his arms the damsel to deny.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 “Though she small worth in Tristram’s sight possess,<br>
 Nor any, saving Yseult, please his sight,<br>
 Nor other dame to love or to caress,<br>
 The philtre, drunk erewhile, allows the knight;<br>
 Yet, for he would that foul discourteousness<br>
 Of Clodion with a fit revenge requite,<br>
 He cries, ‘I deem it were foul wrong and sore,<br>
 If so such beauty I should shut the door.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 “ ‘And, should Sir Clodion grieve beneath the tree<br>
 To lodge alone, and company demand;<br>
 Although less beautiful, I have with me<br>
 A fair and youthful damsel, here at hand,<br>
 Who, I am well content, his mate shall be,<br>
 And do in all things, as he shall command.<br>
 But she that is most fair to the most strong,<br>
 Meseemeth, in all justice should belong.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 “Shut out all night, the moody Clodion strayed,<br>
 Puffing and pacing round his lofty tower,<br>
 As if that prince the sentinel had played<br>
 On them, that slept at ease in lordly bower:<br>
 Him, sorer far than wind and cold dismayed<br>
 That lovely lady’s loss in Tristram’s power:<br>
 But he, with pity touched, upon the morrow,<br>
 Rendered her back, and so relieved his sorrow.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 “Because, he said, and made it plain appear,<br>
 Such as he found her, he returned the fair;<br>
 And though for his discourtesy whilere,<br>
 Clodion had every scorn deserved to bear,<br>
 He was content with having made the peer<br>
 Outwatch the weary night in open air.<br>
 Accepting not that cavalier’s excuse,<br>
 Who would have thrown on Love his castle’s use.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 “For Love should make a churlish nature kind,<br>
 And not transform to rude a gentle breast.<br>
 When Tristram hence was gone, not long behind<br>
 Remained the enamoured prince who changed his rest:<br>
 But first he to a cavalier consigned<br>
 The tower; whereof that baron he possest,<br>
 On pact, that he and his in the domain<br>
 Henceforth this usage ever should maintain;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 “That cavalier of greater heart and power<br>
 Should in this hall be harboured without fail:<br>
 They that less worthy were should void the tower,<br>
 And seek another inn, by hill or dale.<br>
 In fine, that law was fixt, which to this hour<br>
 Endures, as you have seen”; while so his tale<br>
 To Bradamant recounts that castle’s lord,<br>
 The sewer with savoury meats has heaped the board.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 In the great hall that plenteous board was laid,<br>
 (None fairer was in all the world beside)<br>
 Then came where those beauteous ladies stayed,<br>
 And them, with torches lit, did thither guide.<br>
 On entering, Bradamant the room surveyed,<br>
 And she, that other fair, on every side;<br>
 Who as they gaze about the gorgeous hall<br>
 Filled full of picture, mark each storied wall.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVI.</td><td>
 So beauteous are the figures, that instead<br>
 Of eating, on the painted walls they stare;<br>
 Albeit of meat they have no little need,<br>
 Who wearied sore with that day’s labour are.<br>
 With grief the sewer, with grief the cook takes heed,<br>
 How on the table cools the untasted fare.<br>
 Nay, there is one amid the crowd, who cries,<br>
 “First fill your bellies, and then feast your eyes.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVII.</td><td>
 The guests were placed, and now about to eat,<br>
 When suddenly bethought that castellain,<br>
 To house two damsels were a thing unmeet;<br>
 One lady must dislodge, and one remain;<br>
 The fairest stay, and she least fair retreat.<br>
 Where howls the wind, where beats the pattering rain.<br>
 Because they separate came, ’tis ordered so:<br>
 One lady must remain, one lady go.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVIII.</td><td>
 The lord some matrons of his household crew<br>
 Calls, with two elders, in such judgments wise;<br>
 He marks the dames, and bids them of the two<br>
 Declare which is most beauteous in their eyes;<br>
 And all, upon examination due,<br>
 Cry, Aymon’s daughter best deserves the prize,<br>
 And vouch as she in might those kings outweighed,<br>
 No less in beauty she surpassed the maid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIX.</td><td>
 The warder cries to that Islandic dame,<br>
 Who of her sentence has a shrewd suspicion,<br>
 “O lady, let it be no cause of blame,<br>
 That we observe our usage and condition;<br>
 To seek some other rest must be thine aim,<br>
 Since, by our universal band’s admission,<br>
 Though unadorned that martial maid be seen,<br>
 Thou canst not match her charms and lovely mien.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">C.</td><td>
 As in a moment’s time a cloud obscure<br>
 Steams from the bottom of some marshy dale,<br>
 Which the sun’s visage, late so bright and pure,<br>
 Mantles all over with its dingy veil;<br>
 So that poor damsel, sentenced to endure,<br>
 Without, the pelting shower and blustering gale,<br>
 Is seen to change her cheer, and is no more<br>
 The fair and mirthful maid she was before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CI.</td><td>
 The maid turns pale, and all her colour flies,<br>
 Who dreads so stern a sentence to obey:<br>
 But generous Bradamant, in prudent guise,<br>
 Who could not bear to see her turned away,<br>
 Cried to that baron, “Partial and unwise<br>
 Your judgment seems, as well all judgments may,<br>
 Wherein the losing party has not room<br>
 To plead before the judge pronounces doom.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CII.</td><td>
 “I, who this cause take on me to defend,<br>
 Say (whether fairer or less fair I be)<br>
 I came not as a woman, nor intend<br>
 That now mine actions shall be womanly.<br>
 But, saving I undress, who shall pretend<br>
 To say I am or am not such as she?<br>
 Neither should aught be said but what we know,<br>
 And least of all what works another woe.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIII.</td><td>
 “Many, as well as I, long tresses wear,<br>
 Yet are not therefore women; if, as guest,<br>
 I have admittance gained to your repair,<br>
 Like woman or like man, is manifest:<br>
 Then why should I the name of woman bear,<br>
 That in my actions stand a man confest?<br>
 ’Tis ruled that woman should a woman chase;<br>
 Nor that a knight a woman should displace.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIV.</td><td>
 “Grant we (what I confess not howsoe’er)<br>
 That you the woman in my visage read;<br>
 But that in beauty I am not her peer:<br>
 Not therefore, deem I, of my valour’s meed<br>
 Ye would deprive me, though in beauteous cheer<br>
 The palm I to that damsel should concede<br>
 ’Twere hard, before I yield to her in charms,<br>
 That I should forfeit what I won in arms.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CV.</td><td>
 “And if it be your usage, that the dame<br>
 Who yields in beauty, from your tower must wend,<br>
 Here to remain I my design to proclaim,<br>
 Should my resolve have good or evil game,<br>
 Hence I infer, unequal were the game,<br>
 If she and I in beauty should contend:<br>
 For if such strife ’twixt her and me ensues,<br>
 Nought can the damsel gain, and much may lose;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVI.</td><td>
 “And save the gain and loss well balanced be<br>
 In every match, the contest is unfair.<br>
 So that by right, no less than courtesy,<br>
 May she a shelter claim in you repair.<br>
 But are there any here that disagree,<br>
 And to impugn my equal sentence dare,<br>
 Behold my prompt, at such gainsayer’s will,<br>
 To prove my judgment right, his judgment ill!”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVII.</td><td>
 Bradamant—grieved that maid of gentle kind<br>
 Should from that castle wrongfully be sped,<br>
 To bide the raging of the rain and wind,<br>
 Where sheltering house was none, nor even shed—<br>
 With reasons good, in wary speech combined,<br>
 Persuades that lord; but mostly what she said<br>
 On ending silences the knight; and he<br>
 Allows the justice of that damsel’s plea.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVIII.</td><td>
 As when hot summer sun the soil has rived,<br>
 And most the thirsty plant of moisture drains,<br>
 The weak and wasting flower, well nigh deprived<br>
 Of that quick sap which circled in its veins,<br>
 Sucks in the welcome rain, and is revived;<br>
 So, when bold Bradamant so well maintains<br>
 The courier maid’s defence, her beauteous cheer<br>
 And mirth revive, and brighten as whilere.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIX.</td><td>
 At length the supper, which had long been dight,<br>
 Nor yet was touched, enjoys each hungry guest;<br>
 Nor any further news of errant knight<br>
 Them, seated at the festive board, molest;<br>
 All, saving Bradamant, enjoy, whose sprite,<br>
 As wont, is still afflicted and opprest.<br>
 For that suspicious fear, that doubt unjust,<br>
 Which racked her bosom, marred the damsel’s gust.</td></tr>

 The supper done—brought sooner to a close<br>
 Haply from their desire to feast their eyes—<br>
 First of the set, Duke Aymon’s daughter rose,<br>
 And next the courier maid is seen to rise.<br>
 With that the warder signs to one, that goes<br>
 And many torches fires in nimble wise;<br>
 Whose light on storied wall and ceiling fell.<br>
 What followed shall another canto tell.</td></tr>
