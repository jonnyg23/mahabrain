 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 30</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 Great feats achieve Orlando by the way.<br>
 The Tartar king is by Rogero slain:<br>
 For whom fair Bradamant, his spouse, does stay,<br>
 But Fate forbade, that he who wounded lay<br>
 To her his plighted promise should maintain.<br>
 He after boldly with the brethren made,<br>
 Their lord Rinaldo in his need to aid.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 When Reason, giving way to heat of blood,<br>
 Herself from hasty choler ill defends,<br>
 And, hurried on by blind and furious mood,<br>
 We with the tongue or hand molest our friends,<br>
 Though the offence is, after, wept and rued,<br>
 The penance which we pay is poor amends.<br>
 Alas! I sorrow and lament in vain<br>
 For what I said in other angry strain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 But like sick man am I, who, sore bested,<br>
 Suffering with patience many and many a day,<br>
 When against pain he can no more make head,<br>
 Yields to his rage, and curses; pain give way,<br>
 And with it the impetuous wrath is fled,<br>
 Which moved his ready tongue such ill to say;<br>
 And he is left his willful rage to rue,<br>
 But cannot that which he has done undo.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 Well hope I, from your sovereign courtesy,<br>
 Your pardon, since I crave it, ladies bright;<br>
 You will excuse, if moved by madness, I<br>
 Rave in my passion; let your censure light<br>
 On foe, who treats me so despiteously,<br>
 I could not be reduced to worser plight;<br>
 Who prompts what sore repents me: Heaven above<br>
 Knows how she wrongs me, knows how well I love.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 No less beside myself than Brava’s peer<br>
 And I, nor less my pardon should obtain;<br>
 He, who by mead or mountain, far or near,<br>
 Had scowered large portion of the land of Spain,<br>
 Dragging that jennet in his wild career,<br>
 Dead as she was, behind him by the rein;<br>
 But, where a river joined the sea, parforce<br>
 Abandoned on the bank her mangled corse.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 And he, who could like any otter swim,<br>
 Leapt in and rose upon the further side.<br>
 Behold! a mounted shepherd at the brim<br>
 Arrived, his horse to water in the tide;<br>
 Nor when he saw Orlando coming, him<br>
 Eschewed, whom naked and alone he spied.<br>
 —“My jennet for thy hackney were I fain<br>
 To barter,” cried the madman to the swain:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 “Her will I show thee, if thou wilt; who dead<br>
 Upon the river’s other margin fell;<br>
 At leisure may’st thou have her cured,” (he said)<br>
 “And of no other fault have I to tell.<br>
 Give me thy hackney, with some boot instead:<br>
 Prythee, dismount thee, for he likes me well.”<br>
 The peasant, laughing, answered not a word,<br>
 But left the fool and pricked towards the ford.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 “Hearest thou not? hola! I want thy steed,”<br>
 (Cried Roland) and advanced with wrathful cheer.<br>
 A solid staff and knotted, for his need,<br>
 That shepherd had, wherewith he smote the peer;<br>
 Whose violence and ire all bounds exceed,<br>
 Who seems withal to wax more fierce than e’er:<br>
 A cuff he levels at that rustic’s head,<br>
 And splits the solid bone, and lays him dead.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 Then leaping on his horse, by different way<br>
 The country scowers, to make more spoil and wrack:<br>
 That palfrey never more tastes corn or hay;<br>
 So that few days exhaust the famished hack.<br>
 But not afoot does fierce Orlando stray,<br>
 Who will not, while he lives, conveyance lack.<br>
 As many as he finds, so many steeds<br>
 —Their masters slain—he presses for his needs.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 He came at last to Malaga, and here<br>
 Did mightier scathe than he had done elsewhere;<br>
 For now—besides that the infuriate peer<br>
 Of all its people left the country bare,<br>
 Nor (such the ravage) could another year<br>
 The desperate havoc of the fool repair—<br>
 So many houses burnt he, or cast down,<br>
 Sacked was a third of that unhappy town.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 Departing thence, insane Orlando flees<br>
 To Zizera, a seaward town, whose site<br>
 Is in Gibraltar’s bay, or (if you please)<br>
 Say Gibletar’s; for either way ’tis hight;<br>
 Here, loosening from the land, a boat he sees<br>
 Filled with a party, and for pleasure dight:<br>
 Which, for their solace, to the morning gale,<br>
 Upon that summer sea, had spread their sail.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 “Hoah! the boat! put back!” the count ’gan cry,<br>
 Who was in mind to go aboard their barge:<br>
 But vainly on their ears his clamours die:<br>
 For of such freight none willingly take charge.<br>
 As swiftly as a swallow cleaves the sky,<br>
 Furrowing the foamy wave the boat goes large.<br>
 Orlando urges on, with straightening knee,<br>
 And whip and spur, his horse towards the sea.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 He plunged into the waves, at last, parforce;<br>
 For vainly would he shun the waters green.<br>
 Bathed are knees, paunch, and croup, till of that horse<br>
 Scarcely the head above the wave is seen:<br>
 Let him not hope to measure back his course,<br>
 While smitten with the whip his ears between.<br>
 Woe worth him! he must founder by the way,<br>
 Or into Africa his load convey.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 Nor poops nor prows does Roland more descry,<br>
 For all have launched their shallops, which are wide<br>
 Of that dry shore; while from his level eye<br>
 Their hulls the tall and shifting surges hide.<br>
 He spurs his horse amid the billows high,<br>
 Wholly resolved to reach the farther side.<br>
 The courser ends his swim and life in fine,<br>
 Drained of his strength, and drenched brimfull of brine.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 He sinks, and would with him draw down his load;<br>
 But that himself the madman’s arms upbear:<br>
 With sinewy arms and either palm he rowed,<br>
 And puffed and blew the brine before; the air<br>
 Breathed softly, and the water gently flowed;<br>
 And well was needed weather more than fair:<br>
 For if the waters yet a little rise,<br>
 Whelmed by the waxing tide Orlando dies.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 But Fortune, that of madmen is the guide,<br>
 Him from the water drew near Ceuta’s shore,<br>
 Upon that beach, and of those walls as wide<br>
 As twice an archer’s hand could shoot at score.<br>
 For many days along the bank he hied,<br>
 At hazard, ever westward hurrying sore,<br>
 Until he came where on the sea-beat strand<br>
 Encamped a host of blacks, a countless band.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 Leave we the paladin at will to stray!<br>
 To speak of him occasion will come round.<br>
 —Sir, what befel the lady of Catày,<br>
 Who scaped, in time, from him of wit unsound,<br>
 And afterwards, upon her homeward way,<br>
 Was with good bark and better weather bound;<br>
 And how she made Medoro, India’s king;<br>
 Perchance some voice in happier verse may sing.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 To say so many things I am intent,<br>
 I mean not to pursue the cavalier.<br>
 To Mandricardo my fair argument<br>
 It now behoves me, in his turn, to veer<br>
 He happily enjoyed, his rival spent,<br>
 The beauty, left in Europe without peer,<br>
 Since fair Angelica from hence had wended,<br>
 And virtuous Isabel to heaven ascended.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 King Mandricardo, proud that in his right<br>
 His lady had adjudged the amorous suit,<br>
 Enjoys not her award with full delight;<br>
 Since others with him other points dispute.<br>
 By young Rogero claimed, that eagle white<br>
 Of one disastrous quarrel is the root;<br>
 Another moves the king of Sericana<br>
 Against the Tartar king, for Durindana.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 Agramant and Marsilius strive in vain,<br>
 With labour sore, this tangle to undo;<br>
 Nor only cannot they persuade the twain<br>
 In peace and concord to unite anew,<br>
 But cannot make the valiant Child refrain<br>
 From claiming Hector’s buckler as his due;<br>
 Nor yet Gradasso move the sword to lend,<br>
 ‘Till this, or till that, quarrel have an end.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 Rogero brooks not that in other fight<br>
 His shield be braced, nor will Gradasso bear<br>
 That save against himself the Tartar knight<br>
 Should wield the sword Orlando used to wear<br>
 “See we, in fine, on whom the chance will light<br>
 (Cries Agramant) and further words forbear.<br>
 How Fortune rules the matter let us see,<br>
 And choose him that of her shall chosen be.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 “And—would ye do what most would me delight,<br>
 And be an obligation evermore—<br>
 You shall by casting lots decide your right:<br>
 Premising, he whose lot is drawn before<br>
 The other, shall upon two quarrels fight:<br>
 So he who wins, on his companion’s score<br>
 Shall win as well as on his own; and who<br>
 Loses the battle lose alike for two.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 “Between Rogero and Gradasso, we<br>
 Deem there is little difference, rather none;<br>
 And wot whichever shall elected be.<br>
 In arms will make his martial prowess known,<br>
 As for the rest, let doubtful victory<br>
 Descend on him whom Heaven is pleased to own!<br>
 Upon the vanquished knight no blame shall fall,<br>
 But we to Fortune will impute it all.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 Rogero and Gradasso, at this say<br>
 Of Agramant, stood silent, and agreed,<br>
 That he whose lot first issued, the assay<br>
 Should undertake for both in listed mead.<br>
 Thus in two scrolls, inscribed in the same way,<br>
 Their names are writ as destined to succeed.<br>
 These afterwards are cast into an urn,<br>
 Which much they shake and topsy turvy turn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 A seely boy then dipt his hand and drew<br>
 A billet from the vase, and if befel,<br>
 Thereon Rogero’s name the assistants knew;<br>
 —Gradasso’s left behind—I cannot tell<br>
 How joyed renowned Rogero at the view,<br>
 And can as little say what sorrow fell<br>
 Upon Gradasso, on the other side;<br>
 But he parforce his fortune must abide.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 Gradasso every thought and every deed<br>
 Employs, Rogero to instruct and aid,<br>
 That in the strife his champion may succeed;<br>
 And teaches every sleight he has assaid:<br>
 —How best to manage sword and shield at need—<br>
 —What strokes are feints, and what with vantage made—<br>
 And when he should tempt Fortune, when eschew—<br>
 Reminds him, one by one, in long review.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 After the drawing lots and king’s award,<br>
 What of the day remained the champions spent<br>
 As wont, in giving tokens of regard,<br>
 To this or to that other warrior sent.<br>
 The people, greedy for the fight, toward<br>
 The field is gone, and many not content<br>
 With wending thither ere the dawn of light,<br>
 Upon the place of combat watch all night.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 The foolish rabble anxiously attends<br>
 Those goodly champions’ contest for the prize,<br>
 A crowd which neither sees nor comprehends<br>
 Other than that which is before its eyes.<br>
 But they who know what boots and what offends,<br>
 —Marsilius and Sobrino, and the wise—<br>
 Censure the fight, and monarch that affords<br>
 A field of combat to those martial lords.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 Nor what a heavy loss he would sustain<br>
 (Cease they to royal Agramant to read)<br>
 Were Mandricardo or Rogero slain;<br>
 A thing by cruel Destiny decreed.<br>
 Since they, to combat against Charlemagne,<br>
 Of one of these alone have greater need<br>
 Than of ten thousand more, amid which crew<br>
 They scarce would find one champion good and true.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 Agramant recognized this truth; but thought<br>
 That ill his royal word could be repealed;<br>
 Yet Mandricardo and the Child besought<br>
 That they the right, conferred by him, would yield:<br>
 More; that the question was a thing of nought,<br>
 Nor worthy to be tried in martial field;<br>
 And prayed them—would they not obey his hest<br>
 At least somewhile, to let their quarrel rest.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 Five or six months would they the strife delay,<br>
 Or more or less, till Charles defeated were,<br>
 And stript of mantle, crown, and royal sway.<br>
 But each, though he would willingly forbear,<br>
 And much desired his sovereign to obey,<br>
 Stood out against the Moorish monarch’s prayer:<br>
 Since either deemed he would be foully shent<br>
 Who to this treaty first should yield consent.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 But more than king, than all, who sought in vain<br>
 To soften Agrican’s infuriate son,<br>
 The beauteous daughter of King Stordilane<br>
 Lamented, besought him, woe-begone,<br>
 Besought him he would do what all would fain<br>
 Behold by the relenting warrior done;<br>
 —Lamenting her, as through the cavalier,<br>
 For ever kept in agony and fear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 “Alas! and what (exclaims she) can I find<br>
 Which may avail to minister repose,<br>
 If aye, by this or that desire inclined,<br>
 You don your harness to affront new foes?<br>
 What boots it to restore my harassed mind<br>
 That I behold one fearful quarrel’s close,<br>
 Against one champion moved for love of me,<br>
 If one as fierce already kindled be?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 “Woe worth me! I was proud, with little right,<br>
 So good a king, so stout a cavalier<br>
 For he should in the fierce and dangerous fight<br>
 Peril his life, who now, I see to clear,<br>
 Upon a ground of strife so passing light,<br>
 With the same risk prepares to couch the spear.<br>
 You, more than love for me, to strife impels<br>
 The natural rage, wherewith your bosom swells.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 “But if the love you force yourself to show,<br>
 Be in good earnest, that which you profess,<br>
 By this I pray you, by that chastening woe<br>
 Which does my spirit, does my heart oppress,<br>
 Be not concerned, because the bird of snow<br>
 Rogero, pictured on his shield, possess.<br>
 I know not wherefore you should joy or grieve<br>
 That he the blazoned buckler bear or leave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 “Much evil may ensue and little gain<br>
 Out of the battle you to wage prepare;<br>
 Small guerdon will be bought with mickle pain<br>
 If from Rogero you his eagle bear;<br>
 But if your fortune shifts on listed plain,<br>
 She whom you hold not captive by her hair,<br>
 You cause an evil with such mischief fraught,<br>
 My heart is broken at the simple thought.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 “If of small value life to you appear,<br>
 And you esteem a painted bird more high,<br>
 At least for my life’s sake esteem yours dear;<br>
 For one without the other shall not die.<br>
 With you to die excites in me no fear;<br>
 With you, prepared for life or death am I:<br>
 Yet would I fain not die so ill content,<br>
 As I should die if you before me went.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 Accompanying words with tears and sighs,<br>
 In such, or such like speech she him did pray,<br>
 Throughout that livelong night, in piteous wise,<br>
 Hoping her lover’s anger to allay;<br>
 And Mandricardo, sucking from her eyes<br>
 Those sweet tears, glittering in their humid ray,<br>
 And that sweet moan, from lips more deeply dyed<br>
 Than crimson rose, himself in tears, replied.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 “Alack! my dearest life! take thou no dread,<br>
 Alack! for love of Heaven! of thing so light:<br>
 For if (to my sole harm) with banners spread,<br>
 Their following of the Frank or paynim rite<br>
 King Agramant and Charles united led,<br>
 This need not cause you matter for affright.<br>
 What poor account you make of me is clear<br>
 If this one, sole, Rogero breeds such fear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 “And yet should you remember how alone<br>
 (Nor had I scimetar or sword in hand)<br>
 Of knights, with a spear’s truncheon overthrown,<br>
 I singly cleared the field, an armed band.<br>
 Though to his shame and sorrow this he own,<br>
 Gradasso tells to them who make demand,<br>
 He was my prisoner in the Syrian tower:<br>
 Yet other than Rogero’s is his power.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 “Not King Gradasso will the truth deny:<br>
 Sacripant knows it and your Isolier:<br>
 I say King Sacripant of Circassy,<br>
 And Aquilant, and Gryphon, famous peer;<br>
 With hundreds—yea and more—from far and nigh<br>
 Made prisoners at that fearful pass whilere,<br>
 Baptized or Infidel; and all by me<br>
 From prison on the selfsame day set free.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 “And even yet they marvel evermore<br>
 At the great feat which I performed that day;<br>
 Greater than if the squadrons of the Moor<br>
 And Frank united I had held at bay;<br>
 And shall Rogero, new to martial lore,<br>
 Me, onto to one, with scathe or scorn appay?<br>
 And me shall now this young Rogero scare,<br>
 When Hector’s sword and Hector’s arms I wear?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 “Ah! as I might have won you from my foe,<br>
 Why did I not for you in arms contend?<br>
 I so had them my valour shown, I know,<br>
 You would have well foreseen Rogero’s end.<br>
 For heaven’s sake dry your tears, nor by such woe<br>
 —An evil omen for my arms—offend;<br>
 And learn, ’tis Honour pricks me to the field,<br>
 And not an argent bird and blazoned shield.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 So said he; and with reasons passing good<br>
 To him that dame replied, with saddest face;<br>
 Nor only would have changed his sullen mood,<br>
 But would have moved a pillar from its place.<br>
 She would the champion quickly have subdued,<br>
 Though she was gowned, he locked in iron case;<br>
 And make him satisfy the Moorish lord,<br>
 If Agramant spake further of accord;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 And had; but that Aurora—on his way<br>
 Ushering aye the sun—no sooner stirred,<br>
 Than young Rogero, anxious to display<br>
 That rightfully he bore Jove’s beauteous bird,<br>
 To cut the quarrel short, and lest delay<br>
 Be further interposed, in act or word,<br>
 Where round the palisade the people close,<br>
 Appears in armour and his bugle blows.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 When that loud sound is by the Tartar heard,<br>
 Which the proud warrior to the strife defies,<br>
 No more of treaty will he hear a word:<br>
 From bed upspringing, “Arms,” the monarch cries,<br>
 And shows a visage with such fury stirred,<br>
 Doralice dares no longer peace advise,<br>
 Nor speak of treaty or of truce anew;<br>
 And now parforce the battle must ensue.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 The Tartar arms himself in haste; with pain<br>
 The wonted service of his squires he tarries:<br>
 This done, he springs upon the steed amain,<br>
 Erewhile the champion’s who defended Paris;<br>
 And him with speed towards the listed plain,<br>
 Fixt for that fierce assay, the courser carries.<br>
 Even then the king and barons thither made,<br>
 So that the strife was little time delaid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 Put on and laced the shining helmets were,<br>
 And given to either champion was the spear:<br>
 Quickly the trumpet’s blast was heard in air,<br>
 Whose signal blanched a thousand cheeks with fear.<br>
 Levelled those cavaliers their lances bear,<br>
 Spurring their warlike steeds to the career,<br>
 And, in mid champaign, meet with such a shock,<br>
 That Earth appears to rive and Heaven to rock.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 From this side and from that, the eagle flew,<br>
 Which Jove in air was wonted to sustain;<br>
 So hurtled, but with plumes of different hue,<br>
 Those others often on Thessalian plain.<br>
 The beamy lances, rested by the two,<br>
 Well warranted the warriors’ might and main,<br>
 And worse than that encounter had withstood:<br>
 So towers resist the wind, so rocks the flood.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 As Turpin truly writes, into the sky<br>
 Upwent the splinters, broke in the career;<br>
 For two or three fell flaming from on high,<br>
 Which had ascended to the starry sphere.<br>
 The knights unsheathed their faulchions from the thigh,<br>
 And, like those who were little moved by fear,<br>
 For new encounter wheeled, and, man to man,<br>
 Pointing at one another’s vizor ran.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 They, pointing at the vizors’ sight, attacked,<br>
 Nor with their faulchions at the steeds took aim,<br>
 Each other to unhorse, unseemly act!<br>
 Since in that quarrel they are nought to blame.<br>
 Those err, nor know the usage, why by pact<br>
 Deem they were bound their horses not to maim:<br>
 Without pact made, ’twas reckoned a misdeed,<br>
 And an eternal blot to smite a steed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 They level at the vizor, which is double,<br>
 And yet resists such mighty blows with pain.<br>
 The champions evermore their strokes redouble<br>
 Faster than pattering hail, which mars the grain,<br>
 And bruises branch and leaf, and stalk and stubble,<br>
 And cheats the hopes of the expecting swain.<br>
 To you is known the force of either brand,<br>
 And known the force of either warrior’s hand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 But yet no stroke well worthy of their might<br>
 Those peers have dealt, so cautious are the twain.<br>
 The Tartar’s faulchion was the first to bite,<br>
 By which was good Rogero well nigh slain.<br>
 By one of those fell blows which either knight<br>
 So well could plant, his shield was cleft in twain;<br>
 Beneath, his cuirass opened to the stroke,<br>
 And to the quick the cruel weapon broke.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 The assistants’ hearts were frozen at the blow,<br>
 So did Rogero’s danger them appal,<br>
 On whom the many’s favor, well they know,<br>
 And wishes rest, if not of one and all.<br>
 And then (had Fortune ordered matters so,<br>
 As the most part desired they should befall)<br>
 Taken had been the Tartar king or slain;<br>
 So had that blow offended all the train.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
 I think that blow was by some angel stayed,<br>
 To save Rogero from the mischief near:<br>
 Yet at the king (nor answer he delayed)<br>
 He dealt a stroke more terrible than e’er.<br>
 As Mandricardo’s head he aims his blade,<br>
 But such the fury of the cavalier,<br>
 And such his haste, he less my blame deserves,<br>
 If slanting from the mark his faulchion swerves.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 Had Balisarda smote him full, though crowned<br>
 With Hector’s helm, the enchantment had been vain.<br>
 So reels the Tartar, by that stroke astound,<br>
 He from the bristle-hand lets go the rein:<br>
 Thrice with his head he threats to smite the ground,<br>
 While his unguided courser scowers the plain;<br>
 That Brigliadoro, whom by name you know,<br>
 Yet, for his change of master, full of woe.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 Never raged trampled serpent, never so<br>
 Raged wounded lion, as in fell despite<br>
 Raged Mandricardo, rallying from that blow,<br>
 Which had deprived of sense the astonied knight;<br>
 And as his pride and fury waxes, grow<br>
 As much, yea more, his valour and his might.<br>
 He at Rogero makes his courser vault,<br>
 With sword uplifted high for the assault.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 Poised in his stirrups stood the Tartar lord,<br>
 And aiming at his foeman’s casque, believed<br>
 He with the stroke of his descending sword<br>
 Rogero to the bosom should have cleaved;<br>
 But from that youth, yet quicker in his ward,<br>
 A wound beneath his arm the king received,<br>
 Which made wide daylight in the stubborn mail,<br>
 That clothed the better armpit with its scale.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 Rogero drawing Balisarda back,<br>
 Out sprang the tepid blood of crimson stain;<br>
 Hence Mandricardo’s arm did vigour lack,<br>
 And with less dint descended Durindane:<br>
 Yet on the croup the stripling tumbled back,<br>
 Closing his eyelids, through excess of pain;<br>
 And memorable aye had been that blow,<br>
 Had a worse helmet clothed the warrior’s brow.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 For this he pauses not, but spurs amain,<br>
 And Mandricardo smites in the right side.<br>
 Here little boots the texture of the chain,<br>
 And the well wealded metal’s temper tried,<br>
 Against that sword, which never falls in vain,<br>
 Which was enchanted to no end beside,<br>
 But that against it nothing should avail,<br>
 Enchanted corselet or enchanted mail.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 Whate’er that sword takes-in it shears outright,<br>
 And in the Tartar’s side inflicts a wound:<br>
 He curses Heaven and raves in such despite,<br>
 Less horribly the boisterous billows sound.<br>
 He now prepares to put forth all his might:<br>
 The shield, with argent bird and azure ground,<br>
 He hurls, with rage transported, from his hand,<br>
 And grasps with right and left his trenchant brand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
 “Marry,” (Rogero cried,) “it needs no more<br>
 To prove your title to that ensign vain,<br>
 Which now you cast away, and cleft before;<br>
 Nor can you more your right in it maintain.”<br>
 So saying, he parforce must prove how sore<br>
 The danger and the dint of Durindane;<br>
 Which smites his front, and with such weight withal,<br>
 A mountain lighter than that sword would fall.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 If cleft his vizor through the midst; ’twas well<br>
 That from the sight diverged the trenchant blade,<br>
 Which on the saddle’s plated pommel fell;<br>
 Nor yet its double steel the faulchion stayed:<br>
 It reached his armour (like soft wax, the shell<br>
 Oped, and the skirts wherewith ’twas overlaid)<br>
 And trenched upon his thigh a grievous wound;<br>
 So that ’twas long ere he again waxed sound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIII.</td><td>
 The spouting blood of either cavalier<br>
 Their arms had crimsoned in a double drain:<br>
 Hence diversly the people guessed, which peer<br>
 Would have the better of the warlike twain:<br>
 But soon Rogero made the matter clear<br>
 With that keen sword, so many a champion’s bane:<br>
 With this he at that part in fury past<br>
 Whence Mandricardo had his buckler cast.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIV.</td><td>
 He the left side of his good cuirass gored,<br>
 And found a passage to the heart below;<br>
 Which a full palm above the flank he bored;<br>
 So that parforce the Tartar must forego<br>
 His every title to the famous sword,<br>
 The blazoned buckler, and its bird of snow,<br>
 And yield, together with these seeds of strife,<br>
 —Dearer than sword and shield—his precious life.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXV.</td><td>
 Not unavenged the unhappy monarch dies;<br>
 For in the very moment he is smit,<br>
 The sword—for little period his—he plies,<br>
 And good Rogero’s vizor would have split.<br>
 But that he stopt the stroke in wary wise,<br>
 And broke its force and vigour ere it lit;<br>
 Its force and vigour broke: for he, below<br>
 The better arm, first smote his Tartar foe.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVI.</td><td>
 Smit was the Child by Mandricardo’s hand,<br>
 At the same moment he that monarch slew:<br>
 He, albeit thick, divides an iron band<br>
 And good steel cap beneath it; inches two,<br>
 Lies buried in the head the trenchant brand,<br>
 The solid bone and sinew severed through.<br>
 Astound Rogero fell, on earth reversed,<br>
 And from his head a stream of life-blood burst.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVII.</td><td>
 Rogero was the first who went to ground,<br>
 And so much longer did the king delay,<br>
 Nigh every one of those who waited round<br>
 Weened he the prize and vaunt had borne away.<br>
 So, erred his Doralice, that oft was drowned<br>
 In tears, and often clad in smiles that day:<br>
 She thanked her God, with hands to Heaven extended,<br>
 That in such wise the fearful fight had ended.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVIII.</td><td>
 But when by tokens manifest appear<br>
 The live man living and the dead man slain,<br>
 The favourers of those knights, with change of cheer,<br>
 Some weep and some rejoice, an altered train.<br>
 King, lord, and every worthiest cavalier<br>
 Crowd round Rogero, who has risen with pain.<br>
 Him to embrace and gratulate they wend,<br>
 And do him grace and honour without end.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIX.</td><td>
 Each with Rogero is rejoiced, and feels<br>
 That which he utters in his heart; among<br>
 The crowd the Sericane alone conceals<br>
 Other than what he vouches with his tongue.<br>
 He pleasure in his countenance reveals,<br>
 With envy at the conquest inly stung;<br>
 And—were his destiny or chance to blame—<br>
 Curses whiche’er produced Rogero’s name.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXX.</td><td>
 What of Rogero’s favour can be said?<br>
 What of caresses, many, true, and kind,<br>
 From Agramant? that not without his aid<br>
 Would have unrolled his ensigns the wind;<br>
 Who had to move from Africk been afraid,<br>
 Nor would have trusted in his host combined.<br>
 He, now King Mandricardo is no more,<br>
 Esteems him the united world before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 Nor to Rogero lean the men alone;<br>
 To him incline as well the female train,<br>
 Who for the land of France had left their own,<br>
 Amid the troops of Africk or of Spain;<br>
 And Doralice, herself, although she moan,<br>
 And for her lover, cold and pale, complain,<br>
 Save by the griding curb of shame represt,<br>
 Her voice, perchance, had added to the rest.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 I say perchance, nor warrant it I dare,<br>
 Albeit the thing may easily be true;<br>
 For such his manners, such his merits are,<br>
 So beauteous is Rogero’s form to view,<br>
 She (from experience we are well aware)<br>
 So prone to follow whatsoe’er is new,<br>
 That not to play the widow’s lovelorn part,<br>
 She on Rogero well might set her heart.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 Though he did well alive, what could be done<br>
 With Mandricardo, after he was dead?<br>
 ’Tis fitting she provide herself with one<br>
 That her, by night or day, may bravely stead.<br>
 Meanwhile to young Rogero’s succour run<br>
 The king’s physician in his art best read;<br>
 Who, having seen the fruits of that fell strife,<br>
 Already has ensured Rogero’s life.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 Agramant bids them diligently lay<br>
 The wounded warrior in his tent, and there<br>
 Is evermore beside him, night and day;<br>
 Him with such love he watches, with such care:<br>
 To his bed the Tartar’s arms and buckler gay,<br>
 So bade the Moorish king, suspended were;<br>
 Suspended all, save trenchant Durindana,<br>
 Relinquished to the King of Sericana.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 With Mandricardo’s arms, his other weed<br>
 Was to Rogero given, and given with these<br>
 Was warlike Brigliador, whom on the mead<br>
 Orlando left, distraught with his disease.<br>
 To Agramant Rogero gave the steed,<br>
 Well knowing how that goodly gift would please.<br>
 No more of this: parforce my strain returns<br>
 To her that vainly for Rogero burns.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 Bradamant’s torment have I to recount,<br>
 While for the courier damsel she did stay:<br>
 With tidings of her love to Alban’s Mount,<br>
 To her Hippalca measured back her way:<br>
 She of Frontino first and Rodomont,<br>
 And next of good Rogero had to say;<br>
 How to the fount anew he had addrest<br>
 His way, with Richardetto and the rest;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 And how the Child, in rescue of the steed,<br>
 Had gone with her to find the paynim rude;<br>
 And weened to have chastized his foul misdeed,<br>
 That from a woman took Frontino good.<br>
 And how the youth’s design did ill succeed,<br>
 Because the king had other way pursued.<br>
 The reason too why to Mount Alban’s hold<br>
 Rogero had not come, at full she told;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 And fully she to Bradamant exprest<br>
 What to excuse himself Rogero said:<br>
 She after drew the letter from her breast,<br>
 Wherewith entrusted she had thither sped:<br>
 With visage which more care than hope confest,<br>
 The paper Bradamant received and read;<br>
 Which, but that she expected to have seen<br>
 Rogero’s self, more welcome would have been.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIX.</td><td>
 To find herself with written scroll appaid<br>
 In good Rogero’s place, whom she attends,<br>
 Marred her fair visage; which such fear pourtrayed,<br>
 Despite and sorrow as her bosom rends.<br>
 Ten times the page she kisses, while the maid<br>
 As oft to him who writes her heart commends:<br>
 The tears alone which trickle from her eyes<br>
 Keep it from kindling at her burning sighs.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXX.</td><td>
 Four times, nay six, she that epistle read,<br>
 And willed moreover that as many more<br>
 The message by that damsel should be said,<br>
 Who word and letter to Mount Alban bore.<br>
 This while unceasing tears the lady shed,<br>
 Nor, I believe, would ever have given o’er,<br>
 Save by the hope consoled, that she anew<br>
 Should briefly her beloved Rogero view.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXI.</td><td>
 Rogero’s word was pledged for his return<br>
 When fifteen days or twenty were gone by:<br>
 So had he after to Hippalca sworn,<br>
 Bidding her boldly on his faith rely.<br>
 “From accidents that chance at every turn”<br>
 (Cried Bradamant) “what warranty have I,<br>
 Alas!—and such are commonest in war—<br>
 That none the knight’s return for ever bar?<br>
 
    ’     LXXXII<br>
 “Alas! alas! Rogero, that above<br>
 Myself hast evermore been prized by me,<br>
 Who would have thought thou more than me could’st love<br>
 Any, and most thy mortal enemy?<br>
 And harm’st where thou should’st help; nor do I see<br>
 If thou as worthy praise or blame regard<br>
 Such tardiness to punish and reward.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIII.</td><td>
 “I know not if thou knowest—the stones know—<br>
 How by Troyano was thy father slain;<br>
 And yet Troyano’s son, against his foe,<br>
 Thou would’st defend, and keep from harm or stain<br>
 Such vengeance upon him do’st thou bestow?<br>
 And do his vengers, as their meed obtain,<br>
 That I, descended of his stock, should be<br>
 The martyr of the mortal cruelty?”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 To her Rogero, in his absence, said<br>
 The lady these sad words, and more beside,<br>
 Lamenting aye; while her attendant maid<br>
 Nor once alone, but often, certified<br>
 The stripling would observe his faith, and prayed<br>
 Her—who could do no better—to abide<br>
 The Child’s arrival till the time came round<br>
 When he by promise to return was bound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 The comfort that Hippalca’s words convey,<br>
 And Hope, companion of the loving train,<br>
 Bradamant’s fear and sorrow so allay,<br>
 That she enjoys some respite from her pain:<br>
 This moves her in Mount Alban’s keep to stay;<br>
 Nor ever thence that lady stirred again<br>
 Until the day, that day the youthful knight<br>
 Had fixt, who ill observed his promise plight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 But in that he his promise ill maintained,<br>
 No blame upon Rogero should be cast;<br>
 Him one or other cause so long detained,<br>
 The appointed time parforce he overpast:<br>
 On a sick bed, long time, he, sorely pained,<br>
 Was laid, wherein a month or more he past<br>
 In doubt of death; so deeply him had gored<br>
 Erewhile in fight the Tartar monarch’s sword.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 Him on the day prefixed the maid attended,<br>
 Nor other tidings of the youth had read,<br>
 But those he through Hippalca had commended,<br>
 And that which after Richardetto said;<br>
 Who told how him Rogero had defended,<br>
 And freed the captive pair to prison led.<br>
 The tidings, overjoyed, she hears repeat;<br>
 Yet blended with some bitter is the sweet.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 For she had heard as well in that discourse,<br>
 For might and beauty voiced, Marphisa’s praise;<br>
 Heard, how Rogero thither bends his course,<br>
 Together with that lady, as he says,<br>
 Where in weak post and with unequal force<br>
 King Agramant the Christian army stays.<br>
 Such fair companionship the lady lauds,<br>
 But neither likes that union nor applauds.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 Nor light suspicion has she of that queen:<br>
 For, were Marphisa beauteous, as was said,<br>
 And they together till that time had been,<br>
 ’T were marvel but Rogero loved the maid:<br>
 Yet would she not believe; but hung between<br>
 Her hopes and fears, and in Mount Alban stayed;<br>
 And close and anxious there, until the day<br>
 Which was to bring her joy or sorrow, lay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 This while Mount Alban’s prince and castellain,<br>
 Rinaldo, first of that fair brotherhood,<br>
 —I say in honour, not in age, for twain<br>
 In right of birth before the warrior stood,<br>
 Who—as the sun illumes the starry train—<br>
 Had by his deeds ennobled Aymon’s blood,<br>
 One day at noon, with none beside a page<br>
 To serve him, reached that famous fortilage.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 Hither had good Rinaldo now repaired;<br>
 Because returning Paris ward again,<br>
 From Brava, (whither had he often fared,<br>
 As said, to seek Angelica in vain)<br>
 He of that pair those evil news had heard.<br>
 His Malagigi and his Viviane,<br>
 How they were to Maganza to be sent;<br>
 And hence to Agrismont his way had bent.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 There, hearing of the safety of that pair,<br>
 And of their enemies’ defeat and fall;<br>
 And how Rogero and Marphisa were<br>
 The authors of their ruin; and how all<br>
 His valiant brethren and his cousins are<br>
 Returned, and harboured in Mount Alban’s hall,<br>
 Until he there embrace the friendly throng<br>
 Each hour appears to him a twelvemonth long.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 His course to Mont Albano had he ta’en;<br>
 And, there embracing wife and children dear,<br>
 Mother and brethren and the cousins twain,<br>
 (They who were captives to their foe whilere)<br>
 A parent swallow seems, amid that train,<br>
 Which, with full beak, its fasting youth doth cheer.<br>
 With them a day or more the warrior stayed,<br>
 Then issued forth and others thence conveyed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 Guichard, Duke Aymon’s eldest born, and they,<br>
 Richard, Alardo, and Richardet’ combined,<br>
 Vivian and Malagigi, wend their way<br>
 In arms, the martial paladin behind.<br>
 Bradamant, waiting the appointed day,<br>
 Which she, in her desire, too slow opined,<br>
 Feigned herself ailing to the brethren true,<br>
 Nor would she join in arms the banded crew;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 And, saying that she ailed, most truly said;<br>
 Yet ’twas not corporal pain or fever sore,<br>
 It was Desire that on her spirit preyed,<br>
 Diseased with Love’s disastrous fit: no more<br>
 Rinaldo in Mount Alban’s castle stayed:<br>
 With him his kinsman’s flower the warrior bore.<br>
 How he for Paris journeyed, and how well<br>
 He succoured Charles, shall other canto tell.</td></tr>
