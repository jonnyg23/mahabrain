 <h3 align="CENTER">47.</h3>
 <p>47. 1. Without going outside his door, one understands (all that takes place) under the sky; without looking out from his window, one sees the Tâo of Heaven. The farther that one goes out (from himself), the less he knows.</p>
 <p>2. Therefore the sages got their knowledge without travelling; gave their (right) names to things without seeing them; and accomplished their ends without any purpose of doing so.</p>
 <p><a name="img_08906"></a>
 <img src="img/08906.jpg">, 'Surveying what is Far-off.' The chapter is a lesson to men to judge of things according to their internal conviction of similar things in their own experience. Short as the chapter is, it is somewhat mystical. The phrase, 'The Tâo' or way of Heaven, occurs in it for the first time; and it is difficult to lay down its precise meaning. Lâo-dze would seem to teach that man is a microcosm; and that, if</p>
 <p><a name="page_90"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 90</font></a></p>
 <p>he understand the movements of his own mind, he can understand the movements of all other minds. There are various readings, of which it is not necessary to speak.</p>
 <p>I have translated par. 2 in the past tense, and perhaps the first should also be translated so. Most of it is found in Han Ying, preceded by 'formerly' or 'anciently.'</p>
