 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">THE BOY AND THE MANTLE<br>  from <i> RELIQUES OF ANCIENT ENGLISH POETRY</i> </h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">by</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">BISHOP THOMAS PERCY</h2>  </center>  <br>  In the third day of May,<br>  To Carleile did come<br>  A kind curteous child,<br>  That cold much of wisdome.<br>  <br>  A kirtle and a mantle<br>  This child had uppon,<br>  With brouches and ringes<br>  Full richelye bedone.<br>  <br>  He had a sute of silke<br>  About his middle drawne;<br>  Without he cold of curtesye,<br>  He thought itt much shame.<br>  <br>  "God speed thee, King Arthur,<br>  Sitting at thy meate:<br>  And the goodly Queene Guénever<br>  I cannott her forgett.<br>  <br>  "I tell you, lords, in this hall,<br>  I hett you all to heede,<br>  Except you be the more surer,<br>  Is you for to dread."<br>  <br>  He plucked out of his poterner,<br>  And longer wold not dwell;<br>  He pulled forth a pretty mantle,<br>  Betweene two nut-shells.<br>  <br>  "Have thou here, King Arthur,<br>  Have thou heere of mee;<br>  Give itt to thy comely queene,<br>  Shapen as itt is alreadye.<br>  <br>  "Itt shall never become that wiffe,<br>  That hath once done amisse:-"<br>  Then every knight in the kings court<br>  Began to care for his.<br>  <br>  Forth came dame Guénever;<br>  To the mantle shee her hied;<br>  The ladye shee was newfangle,<br>  But yett shee was affrayd.<br>  <br>  When shee had taken the mantle,<br>  She stoode as shee had beene madd:<br>  It was from the top to the toe<br>  As sheeres had itt shread.<br>  <br>  One while was it gule,<br>  Another while was itt greene;<br>  Another while was it wadded;<br>  Ill itt did her beseeme.<br>  <br>  Another while was it blacke,<br>  And bore the worst hue:<br>  "By my troth," quoth King Arthur,<br>  "I thinke thou be not true."<br>  <br>  Shee threw downe the mantle,<br>  That bright was of blee;<br>  Fast, with a rudd redd,<br>  To her chamber can shee flee.<br>  <br>  She curst the weaver and the walker<br>  That clothe that had wrought,<br>  And bade a vengeance on his crowne<br>  That hither hath itt brought.<br>  <br>  "I had rather be in a wood,<br>  Under a greene tree,<br>  Then in King Arthurs court<br>  Shamed for to bee."<br>  <br>  Kay called forth his ladye,<br>  And bade her come neere;<br>  Saies, "Madam, and thou be guiltye,<br>  I pray thee hold thee there."<br>  <br>  Forth came his ladye,<br>  Shortlye and anon;<br>  Boldlye to the mantle<br>  Then is shee gone.<br>  <br>  When she had tane the mantle,<br>  And cast it her about,<br>  Then was shee bare<br>  Before all the rout.<br>  <br>  Then every knight,<br>  That was in the kings court,<br>  Talked, laughed, and showted<br>  Full oft att that sport.<br>  <br>  She threw downe the mantle,<br>  That bright was of blee;<br>  Fast, with a red rudd,<br>  To her chamber can shee flee.<br>  <br>  Forth came an old knight<br>  Pattering ore a creede,<br>  And he proferred to this little boy<br>  Twenty markes to his meede,<br>  <br>  And all the time of the Christmasse,<br>  Willinglye to ffeede;<br>  For why this mantle might<br>  Doe his wiffe some need.<br>  <br>  When she had tane the mantle,<br>  Of cloth that was made,<br>  Shee had no more left on her,<br>  But a tassell and a threed:<br>  Then every knight in the kings court<br>  Bade evill might shee speed.<br>  <br>  Shee threw downe the mantle,<br>  That bright was of blee;<br>  And fast, with a redd rudd,<br>  To her chamber can shee flee.<br>  <br>  Craddocke called forth his ladye,<br>  And bade her come in;<br>  Saith, "Winne this mantle, ladye,<br>  With a litle dinne.<br>  <br>  "Winne this mantle, ladye,<br>  And it shal be thine,<br>  If thou never did amisse<br>  Since thou wast mine."<br>  <br>  Forth came Craddockes ladye,<br>  Shortlye and anon;<br>  But boldlye to the mantle<br>  Then is shee gone.<br>  <br>  When she had tane the mantle,<br>  And cast it her about,<br>  Upp att her great toe<br>  It began to crinkle and crowt:<br>  Shee said, "Bowe downe, mantle,<br>  And shame me not for nought.<br>  <br>  "Once I did amisse,<br>  I tell you certainlye,<br>  When I kist Craddockes mouth<br>  Under a greene tree;<br>  When I kist Craddockes mouth<br>  Before he marryed mee."<br>  <br>  When shee had her shreeven, <br>  And her sines shee had tolde,<br>  The mantle stoode about her<br>  Right as shee wold,<br>  <br>  Seemelye of coulour,<br>  Glittering like gold:<br>  Then every knight in Arthurs court<br>  Did her behold.<br>  <br>  Then spake dame Guénever<br>  To Arthur our king;<br>  "She hath tane yonder mantle<br>  Not with right, but with wronge.<br>  <br>  "See you not yonder woman,<br>  That maketh her self soe cleane?<br>  I have seene tane out of her bedd<br>  Of men fiveteene;<br>  <br>  "Priests, clarkes, and wedded men<br>  From her, bedeene:<br>  Yett shee taketh the mantle,<br>  And maketh her self cleane."<br>  <br>  Then spake the little boy,<br>  That kept the mantle in hold;<br>  Sayes, "King, chasten thy wiffe,<br>  Of her words shee is to bold:<br>  <br>  "Shee is a bitch and a witch,<br>  And a whore bold:<br>  King, in thine owne hall<br>  Thou art a cuckold."<br>  <br>  The little boy stoode<br>  Looking out a dore;<br>  And there as he was lookinge<br>  He was ware of a wyld bore.<br>  <br>  He was ware of a wyld bore,<br>  Wold have werryed a man:<br>  He pulld forth a wood kniffe,<br>  Fast thither that he ran:<br>  He brought in the bores head,<br>  And quitted him like a man.<br>  <br>  He brought in the bores head,<br>  And was wonderous bold:<br>  He said there was never a cuckolds kniffe<br>  Carve itt that cold.<br>  <br>  Some rubbed their knives<br>  Uppon a whetstone:<br>  Some threw them under the table,<br>  And said they had none.<br>  <br>  King Arthur and the child<br>  Stood looking upon them;<br>  All their knives edges<br>  Turned backe againe.<br>  <br>  Craddocke had a little knive<br>  Of iron and of steele;<br>  He britled the bores head<br>  Wonderous weele,<br>  That every knight in the kings court<br>  Had a morssell.<br>  <br>  The little boy had a horne,<br>  Of red gold that ronge:<br>  He said there was "noe cuckolde<br>  Shall drinke of my horne,<br>  But he shold it sheede,<br>  Either behind or beforne."<br>  <br>  Some shedd on their shoulder,<br>  And some on their knee;<br>  He that cold not hitt his mouthe,<br>  Put it in his eye:<br>  And he that was a cuckold<br>  Every man might him see.<br>  <br>  Craddocke wan the horne,<br>  And the bores head:<br>  His ladie wan the mantle<br>  Unto her meede.<br>  Everye such a lovely ladye<br>        God send her well to speede.<br>
