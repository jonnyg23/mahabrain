 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 45</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 Young Leo doth from death Rogero free;<br>
 For him Rogero Bradamant hath won,<br>
 Making that maid appear less strong to be,<br>
 Disguised in fight like Leo; and, that done,<br>
 Straight in despite would slay himself; so he<br>
 By sorrow, so by anguish is foredone.<br>
 To hinder Leo of his destined wife<br>
 Marphisa works, and kindles mighty strife.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 By how much higher we see poor mortal go<br>
 On Fortune’s wheel, which runs a restless round,<br>
 We so much sooner see his head below<br>
 His heels; and he is prostrate on the ground.<br>
 The Lydian, Syracusan, Samian show<br>
 This truth, and more whose names I shall not sound;<br>
 All into deepest dolour in one day<br>
 Hurled headlong from the height of sovereign sway.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 By how much more deprest on the other side,<br>
 By how much more the wretch is downwards hurled,<br>
 He so much sooner mounts, where he shall ride,<br>
 If the revolving wheel again be twirled.<br>
 Some on the murderous block have well-nigh died,<br>
 That on the following day have ruled the world.<br>
 Ventidius, Servius, Marius this have shown<br>
 In ancient days; King Lewis in our own;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 King Lewis, stepfather of my duke’s son;<br>
 Who, when his host at Santalbino fled,<br>
 Left in his clutch by whom that field was won,<br>
 Was nigh remaining shorter by the head.<br>
 Nor long before the great Corvinus run<br>
 A yet more fearful peril, worse bested:<br>
 Both throned, when overblown was their mischance,<br>
 One king of Hungary, one king of France.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 ’Tis plain to sight, through instances that fill<br>
 The page of ancient and of modern story,<br>
 That ill succeeds to good, and good to ill;<br>
 That glory ends in shame, and shame in glory;<br>
 And that man should not trust, deluded still,<br>
 In riches, realm, or field of battle, gory<br>
 With hostile blood, nor yet despair, for spurns<br>
 Of Fortune; since her wheel for ever turns.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 Through that fair victory, when overthrown<br>
 Were Leo and his royal sire, the knight<br>
 Who won that battle to such trust is grown,<br>
 In his good fortune and his peerless might,<br>
 He, without following, without aid, alone<br>
 (So is he prompted by his daring sprite)<br>
 Thinks, mid a thousand squadrons in array,<br>
 —Footmen and horsemen—sire and son to slay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 But she, that wills no trust shall e’er be placed<br>
 In her by man, to him doth shortly show,<br>
 How wight by her is raised, and how abased;<br>
 How soon she is a friend, how soon a foe;<br>
 She makes him know Rogero, that in haste<br>
 Is gone to work that warrior shame and woe;<br>
 The cavalier, which in that battle dread<br>
 With much ado had from his faulchion fled.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 He to Ungiardo hastens to declare<br>
 The Child who put the imperial host to flight,<br>
 Whose carnage many years will not repair,<br>
 Here past the day and was to pass the night;<br>
 And saith, that Fortune, taken by the hair,<br>
 Without more trouble, and without more fight,<br>
 Will, if he prisons him, the Bulgars bring<br>
 Beneath the yoke and lordship of his king.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 Ungiardo from the crowd, which had pursued<br>
 Thither their flight from the ensanguined plain,<br>
 For, troop by troop, a countless multitude<br>
 (Arrived, because not all the bridge could gain)<br>
 Knew what a cruel slaughter had ensued:<br>
 For there the moiety of the Greeks was slain;<br>
 And knew that by a cavalier alone<br>
 One host was saved, and one was overthrown;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 And that undriven he should have made his way<br>
 Into the net, and of his own accord,<br>
 Wondered, and showed his pleasure, at the say<br>
 In visage, gesture, and in joyful word.<br>
 He waited till Rogero sleeping lay;<br>
 Then softly sent his guard to take that lord;<br>
 And made the valiant Child, who had no dread<br>
 Of such a danger, prisoner in his bed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 By his own shield accused, that witness true,<br>
 The Child is captive in Novogorood,<br>
 To Ungiardo, worst among the cruel, who<br>
 Marvellous mirth to have that prisoner shewed.<br>
 And what, since he was naked, could he do,<br>
 Bound, while his eyes were yet by slumber glued?<br>
 A courier, who the news should quickly bear,<br>
 Ungiardo bids to Constantine repair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 Constantine on that night with all his host,<br>
 Raising his camp, from Save’s green shore had gone:<br>
 With this in Beleticche he takes post,<br>
 Androphilus’, his sister’s husband’s town,<br>
 Father of him, whose arms in their first joust<br>
 (As if of wax had been his habergeon)<br>
 Had pierced and carved the puissant cavalier,<br>
 Now by Ungiardo pent in dungeon drear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 Here from attack the emperor makes assure<br>
 The city walls and gates on every side;<br>
 Lest, from the Bulgar squadrons ill secure,<br>
 Having so good a warrior for their guide,<br>
 His broken Grecians worse than fear endure;<br>
 Deeming the rest would by his hand have died.<br>
 Now he is taken, these breed no alarms;<br>
 Nor would he fear the banded world in arms.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 The emperor, swimming in a summer sea,<br>
 Knows not for very pleasure what to do:<br>
 “Truly the Bulgars may be said to be<br>
 Vanquished,” he cries, with bold and cheerful brow.<br>
 As he would feel assured of victory,<br>
 That had of either arm deprived his foe;<br>
 So the emperor was assured, and so rejoiced,<br>
 When good Rogero’s fate the warrior voiced.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 No less occasion has the emperor’s son<br>
 For joying; for besides that he anew<br>
 Trusts to acquire Belgrade, and tower and town<br>
 Throughout the Bulgars’ country to subdue,<br>
 He would by favours make the knight his own,<br>
 And hopes to rank him in his warlike crew:<br>
 Nor need he envy, guarded by his blade,<br>
 King Charles’, Orlando’s, or Rinaldo’s aid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 Theodora was by other thoughts possest,<br>
 Whose son was killed by young Rogero’s spear;<br>
 Which through his shoulders, entering at his breast,<br>
 Issued a palm’s breadth in the stripling’s rear;<br>
 Constantine’s sister she, by grief opprest,<br>
 Fell down before him; and with many a tear<br>
 That dropt into her bosom, while she sued,<br>
 His heart with pity softened and subdued.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 “I still before these feet will bow my knee,<br>
 Save on this felon, good my lord,” (she cried)<br>
 “Who killed my son, to venge me thou agree,<br>
 Now that we have him in our hold; beside<br>
 That he thy nephew was, thou seest how thee<br>
 He loved; thou seest what feats upon thy side<br>
 That warrior wrought; thou seest if thou wilt blot<br>
 Thine own good name, if thou avenge him not.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 “Thou seest how righteous Heaven by pity stirred<br>
 From the wide champaign, red with Grecian gore,<br>
 Bears that fell man; and like a reckless bird<br>
 Into the fowler’s net hath made him soar;<br>
 That for short season, for revenge deferred,<br>
 My son may mourn upon the Stygian shore.<br>
 Give me, my lord, I pray, this cruel foe,<br>
 That by his torment I may soothe my woe.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 So well she mourns; and in such moving wise<br>
 And efficacious doth she make lament;<br>
 (Nor from before the emperor will arise,<br>
 Though he three times and four the dame has hent,<br>
 And to uplift by word and action tries)<br>
 That he is forced her wishes to content;<br>
 And thus, according to her prayer, commands<br>
 The Child to be delivered to her hands;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 And, not therein his orders to delay,<br>
 They take the warrior of the unicorn<br>
 To cruel Theodora; but one day<br>
 Of respite has the knight: to have him torn<br>
 In quarters, yet alive; to rend and slay<br>
 Her prisoners publicly with shame and scorn,<br>
 Seems a poor pain; and he must undergo<br>
 Other unwonted and unmeasured woe.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 At the commandment of that woman dread,<br>
 Chains on his neck and hands and feet they don;<br>
 And put him in a dungeon-cell, where thread<br>
 Of light was never by Apollo thrown:<br>
 He has a scanty mess of mouldy bread;<br>
 And sometimes is he left two days with none;<br>
 And one that doth the place of jailer fill<br>
 Is prompter than herself to work him ill.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 Oh! if Duke Aymon’s daughter brave and fair,<br>
 Of if Marphisa of exalted mind<br>
 Had heard Rogero’s sad estate declare,<br>
 And how he in this guise in prison pined,<br>
 To his rescue either would have made repair,<br>
 And would have flung the fear of death behind:<br>
 Nor had bold Bradamant, intent to aid,<br>
 Respect to Beatrice or Aymon paid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 Meanwhile King Charlemagne upon his side,<br>
 Heeding his promise made in solemn sort,<br>
 That none should have the damsel for his bride,<br>
 That of her prowess in the field fell short;<br>
 Not only had his sovereign pleasure cried<br>
 With sound of trumpet in his royal court,<br>
 But in each city subject to his crown.<br>
 Hence quickly through the world the bruit was blown.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 Such the condition which he bids proclaim:<br>
 He that would with Duke Aymon’s daughter wed<br>
 Must with the sword contend against that dame<br>
 From the suns rise until he seeks his bed;<br>
 And if he for that time maintains the game,<br>
 And is not overcome, without more said,<br>
 The lady is adjudged to have lost the stake;<br>
 Nor him for husband can refuse to take.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 The choice of arms must be by her foregone,<br>
 No matter who may claim it in the course:<br>
 And by the damsel this may well be done,<br>
 Good at all arms alike, on foot or horse.<br>
 Aymon, who cannot strive against the crown,<br>
 —Cannot and will not—yields at length parforce.<br>
 He much the matter sifts, and in the end<br>
 Resolves to court with Bradamant to wend.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 Though for the daughter choler and disdain<br>
 The mother nursed, yet that she honour due<br>
 Might have, she garments, dyed in different grain,<br>
 Had wrought for her, of various form and hue.<br>
 Bradamant for the court of Charlemagne<br>
 Departs, and finding not her love, to her view<br>
 His noble court appears like that no more,<br>
 Which had appeared to her so fair before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 As he that hath beheld a garden, bright<br>
 With flowers and leaves in April or in May,<br>
 And next beholds it, when the sun his light<br>
 Hath sloped toward the north, and shortened day,<br>
 Finds it a desert horrid to the sight;<br>
 So, now that her Rogero is away,<br>
 To Bradamant, who thither made resort,<br>
 No longer what it was appeared that court.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 What is become of him she doth not dare<br>
 Demand, lest more suspicion thence be bred;<br>
 But listens still, and searches here and there;<br>
 That this by some, unquestioned, may be said;<br>
 Knows he is gone, but has no notion where<br>
 The warrior, when he went, his steps had sped;<br>
 Because, departing thence, he spake no word<br>
 Save to the squire who journeyed with his lord.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 Oh! how she sighs! how fears the gentle maid,<br>
 Hearing Rogero, as it were, was flown!<br>
 Oh! how above all other terrors, weighed<br>
 The fear, that to forget her he was gone!<br>
 That, seeing Aymon still his wish gainsayed,<br>
 And that to wed the damsel hope was none,<br>
 He fled, perchance, so hoping to be loosed<br>
 From toils wherein he by her love was noosed;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 And that with further end the youthful lord<br>
 Her from his heart more speedily to chase,<br>
 Will rove from realm to realm, till one afford<br>
 Some dame, that may his former love efface;<br>
 Even, as the proverb says, that in a board<br>
 One nail drives out another from its place.<br>
 A second thought succeeds, and paints the youth<br>
 Arraigned of fickleness, as full of truth;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 And her reproves for having lent an ear<br>
 To a suspicion so unjust and blind;<br>
 And so, this thought absolves the cavalier;<br>
 And that accuses; and both audience find;<br>
 And now this way, now that, she seemed to veer;<br>
 Nor this, nor that—irresolute of mind—<br>
 Preferred: yet still to what gave most delight<br>
 Most promptly leaned, and loathed its opposite;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 And thinking, ever and anon, anew<br>
 On that so oft repeated by the knight,<br>
 As for grave sin, remorse and sorrow grew<br>
 That she had nursed suspicion and affright;<br>
 And she, as her Rogero were in view,<br>
 Would blame herself, and would her bosom smite;<br>
 And say: “I see ’twas ill such thoughts to nurse,<br>
 But he, the cause, is even cause of worse.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 “Love is the cause; that in my heart inlaid<br>
 Thy form, so graceful and so fair to see;<br>
 And so thy darling and thy wit pourtrayed,<br>
 And worth, of all so bruited, that to me<br>
 It seems impossible that wife or maid,<br>
 Blest with thy sight, should not be fired by thee;<br>
 And that she should not all her art apply<br>
 To unbind, and fasten thee with other tie.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 “Ah! wellaway! if in my thought Love so<br>
 Thy thought, as thy fair visage, had designed,<br>
 This—am I well assured—in open show,<br>
 As I unseen believe it, should I find;<br>
 And be so quit of Jealousy, that foe<br>
 Would not still harass my suspicious mind;<br>
 And, where she is by me repulsed with pain,<br>
 Not quelled and routed would she be, but slain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 “I am like miser, so intent on gear,<br>
 And who hath this so buried in his heart,<br>
 That he, for hoarded treasure still in fear,<br>
 Cannot live gladly from his wealth apart.<br>
 Since I Rogero neither see nor hear,<br>
 More puissant far than Hope, O Fear! thou art;<br>
 To thee, though false and idle I give way;<br>
 And cannot choose but yield myself thy prey.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 “But I, Rogero, shall no sooner spy<br>
 The light of thy glad countenance appear,<br>
 Against mine every credence, from mine eye<br>
 Concealed (and woe is me), I know not where,—<br>
 Oh! how true Hope false Fear shall from on high<br>
 Depose withal, and to the bottom bear!<br>
 Ah! turn to me, Rogero! turn again,<br>
 And comfort Hope, whom Fear hath almost slain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 “As when the sun withdraws his glittering head,<br>
 The shadows lengthen, causing vain affright;<br>
 And as the shadows, when he leaves his bed,<br>
 Vanish, and reassure the timid wight:<br>
 Without Rogero so I suffer dread;<br>
 Dread lasts not, if Rogero is in sight.<br>
 Return to me, return, Rogero, lest<br>
 My hope by fear should wholly be opprest.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 “As every spark is in the night alive,<br>
 And suddenly extinguished when ’tis morn;<br>
 When me my sun doth of his rays deprive,<br>
 Against me felon Fear uplifts his horn:<br>
 But they the shades of night no sooner drive,<br>
 Than Fears are past and gone, and Hopes return.<br>
 Return, alas! return, O radiance dear!<br>
 And drive from me that foul, consuming Fear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 “If the sun turn from us and shorten day,<br>
 Earth all its beauties from the sight doth hide;<br>
 The wild winds howl, and snows and ice convey;<br>
 Bird sings not; nor is leaf or flower espied.<br>
 So, whensoever thou thy gladsome ray,<br>
 O my fair sun, from me dost turn aside,<br>
 A thousand, and all evil, dreads, make drear<br>
 Winter within me many times a year.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 “Return, my sun, return! and springtide sweet,<br>
 Which evermore I long to see, bring back;<br>
 Dislodge the snows and ice with genial hear;<br>
 And clear my mind, so clouded o’er and black.”<br>
 As Philomel, or Progne, with the meat<br>
 Returning, which her famished younglings lack,<br>
 Mourns o’er an empty nest, or as the dove<br>
 Laments himself at having lost is love;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 The unhappy Bradamant laments her so,<br>
 Fearing the Child is reft from her and gone;<br>
 While often tears her visage overflow:<br>
 But she, as best she can, conceals her moan.<br>
 Oh! how—oh! how much worse would be her woe,<br>
 If what she knew not to the maid were known!<br>
 That, prisoned and with pain and pine consumed,<br>
 Her consort to a cruel death was doomed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 The cruelty which by that beldam ill<br>
 Was practised on the prisoned cavalier,<br>
 And who prepared the wretched Child to kill,<br>
 By torture new and pains unused whilere,<br>
 While so Rogero pined, the gracious will<br>
 Of Heaven conveyed to gentle Leo’s ear;<br>
 And put into his heart the means to aid,<br>
 And not to let such worth be overlaid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 The courteous Leo that Rogero loved,<br>
 Not that the Grecian knew howe’er that he<br>
 Rogero was, but by that valour moved<br>
 Which sole and superhuman seemed to be,<br>
 Thought much, and mused, and planned, how it behoved<br>
 —And found at last a way—to set him free;<br>
 So that his cruel aunt should have no right<br>
 To grieve or say he did her a despite.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 In secret, Leo with the man that bore<br>
 The prison-keys a parley had, and said,<br>
 He wished to see that cavalier, before<br>
 Upon the wretch was done a doom so dread.<br>
 When it was night, one, faithful found of yore,<br>
 Bold, strong, and good in brawl, he thither led;<br>
 And—by the silent warder taught that none<br>
 Must know ’twas Leo—was the door undone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 Leo, escorted by none else beside,<br>
 Was led by the compliant castellain,<br>
 With his companion, to the tower, where stied<br>
 Was he, reserved for nature’s latest pain.<br>
 There round the neck of their unwary guide,<br>
 Who turns his back the wicket to unchain,<br>
 A slip-knot Leo and his follower cast;<br>
 And, throttled by the noose, he breathes his last.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 —The trap upraised, by rope from thence suspended<br>
 For such a need—the Grecian cavalier,<br>
 With lighted flambeau in his hand, descended,<br>
 Where, straitly bound, and without sun to cheer,<br>
 Rogero lay, upon a grate extended,<br>
 Less than a palm’s breadth of the water clear:<br>
 To kill him in a month, or briefer space,<br>
 Nothing was needed but that deadly place.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 Lovingly Leo clipt the Child, and, “Me,<br>
 O cavalier! thy matchless valour,” cried,<br>
 “Hath in indissoluble bands to thee,<br>
 In willing and eternal service, tried;<br>
 And wills thy good to mine preferred should be,<br>
 And I for thine my safety set aside,<br>
 And weigh thy friendship more than sire, and all<br>
 Whom I throughout the world my kindred call.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 “I Leo am, that thou what fits mayst know,<br>
 Come to thy succour, the Greek emperor’s son:<br>
 If ever Constantine, my father, trow<br>
 That I have aided thee, I danger run<br>
 To be exiled, or aye with troubled brow<br>
 Regarded for the deed that I have done;<br>
 For thee he hates because of those thy blade<br>
 Put to the rout and slaughtered near Belgrade.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 He his discourse with more beside pursues,<br>
 That might from death to life the Child recall;<br>
 And all this while Rogero’s hands doth loose.<br>
 “Infinite thanks I owe you,” cries the thrall,<br>
 “And I the life you gave me, for your use<br>
 Will ever render back, upon your call;<br>
 And still, at all your need, I for your sake,<br>
 And at all times, that life will promptly stake.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 Rogero is rescued; and the gaoler slain<br>
 Is left in that dark dungeon in his place;<br>
 Nor is Rogero known, nor are the twain:<br>
 Leo the warrior, free from bondage base,<br>
 Brings home, and there in safety to remain<br>
 Persuades, in secret, four or six days’ space:<br>
 Meanwhile for him will he retrieve the gear<br>
 And courser, by Ungiardo reft whilere.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 Open the gaol is found at dawn of light,<br>
 The gaoler strangled, and Rogero gone.<br>
 Some think that these or those had helped his flight:<br>
 All talk; and yet the truth is guessed by none.<br>
 Well may they think by any other wight<br>
 Rather than Leo had the deed been done;<br>
 For many deemed he had cause to have repaid<br>
 The Child with scathe, and none to give him aid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 So wildered by such kindness, so immersed<br>
 In wonder, is the rescued cavalier,<br>
 So from those thoughts is he estranged, that erst<br>
 So many weary miles had made him steer,<br>
 His second thoughts confronting with his first,<br>
 Nor these like those, nor those like these appear.<br>
 He first with hatred, rage, and venom burned;<br>
 With pity and with love then wholly yearned.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 Much muses he by night and much by day;<br>
 —Nor cares for ought, nor ought desires beside—<br>
 By equal or more courtesy to pay<br>
 The mighty debt that him to Leo tied.<br>
 Be his life long or short, or what it may,<br>
 Albeit to Leo’s service all applied,<br>
 Dies he a thousand deaths, he can do nought,<br>
 But more will be deserved, Rogero thought.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 Thither meanwhile had tidings been conveyed<br>
 Of Charles’ decree: that who in nuptial tye<br>
 Would yoke with Bradamant, with trenchant blade<br>
 Or lance must with the maid his prowess try.<br>
 These news the Grecian prince so ill appaid,<br>
 His cheek was seen to blanch with sickly dye;<br>
 Because, as one that measured well his might,<br>
 He knew he was no match for her in fight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
 Communing with himself, he can supply<br>
 (He sees) the valour wanting with his wit;<br>
 And the strange knight with his own ensignry,<br>
 Whose name is yet unknown to him, will fit:<br>
 Him he against Frank champion, far and nigh,<br>
 Believes he may for force and daring pit;<br>
 And if the knight to that emprize agree,<br>
 Vanquished and taken Bradamant will be.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 But two things must he do; must, first, dispose<br>
 That cavalier to undertake the emprize;<br>
 Then send afield the champion, whom he chose,<br>
 In mode, that none suspect the youth’s disguise:<br>
 To him the matter Leo doth disclose;<br>
 And after prays in efficacious wise,<br>
 That he the combat with the maid will claim,<br>
 Under false colours and in other’s name.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 Much weighs the Grecian’s eloquence; but more<br>
 Than eloquence with good Rogero weighed<br>
 The mighty obligation which he bore;<br>
 That debt which cannot ever be repaid.<br>
 So, albeit it appeared a hardship sore<br>
 And thing well-nigh impossible, he said,<br>
 With blither face than heart, that Leo’s will<br>
 In all that he commands he would fulfil.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 Albeit no sooner he the intent exprest,<br>
 Than with sore grief Rogero’s heart was shent;<br>
 Which, night and day, and ever, doth molest,<br>
 Ever afflict him, evermore torment:<br>
 And though he sees his death is manifest,<br>
 Never will he confess he doth repent:<br>
 Rather than not with Leo’s prayer comply,<br>
 A thousand deaths, not one, the Child will die.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 Right sure he is to die; if he forego<br>
 The lady, he foregoes his life no less.<br>
 His heart will break through his distress and woe,<br>
 Or, breaking not with woe and with distress,<br>
 He will, himself, the bands of life undo,<br>
 And of its clay the spirit dispossess.<br>
 For all things can he better bear than one;<br>
 Than see that gentle damsel not his own.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 To die is he disposed; but how to die<br>
 Cannot as yet the sorrowing lord decide:<br>
 Sometimes he thinks his prowess to belie,<br>
 And offer to her sword his naked side:<br>
 For never death can come more happily<br>
 Than if her hand the fatal faulchion guide:<br>
 Then sees, except he wins the martial maid<br>
 For that Greek prince, the debt remains unpaid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 For he with Bradamant, as with a foe,<br>
 Promised to do, not feign, a fight in mail,<br>
 And not to make of arms a seeming show;<br>
 So that his sword should Leo ill avail.<br>
 Then by his word will he abide; and though<br>
 His breast now these now other thoughts assail,<br>
 All from his bosom chased the generous youth,<br>
 Save that which moved him to maintain his truth.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
 With the emperor’s licence, armour to prepare,<br>
 And steeds meanwhile had wrought his youthful son;<br>
 Who with such goodly following as might square<br>
 With his degree, upon his way was gone:<br>
 With him Rogero rides, through Leo’s care,<br>
 Equipt with horse and arms, that were his own.<br>
 Day after day the squadron pricks; nor tarries<br>
 Until arrived in France; arrived at Paris.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 Leo will enter not the town; but nigh<br>
 Pitches his broad pavilions on the plain;<br>
 And his arrival by an embassy<br>
 Makes known that day to royal Charlemagne.<br>
 Well pleased is he; and visits testify<br>
 And many gifts the monarch’s courteous vein.<br>
 His journey’s cause the Grecian prince displayed,<br>
 And to dispatch his suit the sovereign prayed:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIII.</td><td>
 To send afield the damsel, who denied<br>
 Ever to take in wedlock any lord<br>
 Weaker than her: for she should be his bride,<br>
 Or he would perish by the lady’s sword.<br>
 Charles undertook for this; and, on her side,<br>
 The following day upon the listed sward<br>
 Before the walls, in haste, enclosed that night,<br>
 Appeared the martial maid, equipt for fight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIV.</td><td>
 Rogero past the night before the day<br>
 Wherein by him the battle should be done,<br>
 Like that which felon spends, condemning to pay<br>
 Life’s forfeit with the next succeeding sun:<br>
 He made his choice to combat in the fray<br>
 All armed; because he would discovery shun:<br>
 Nor barded steed he backed, nor lance he shook;<br>
 Nor other weapon than his faulchion took.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXV.</td><td>
 No lance he took: yet was it not through fear<br>
 Of that which Argalìa whilom swayed;<br>
 Astolpho’s next; then hers, that in career<br>
 Her foemen ever upon earth had laid:<br>
 Because none weened such force was in the spear,<br>
 Nor that it was by necromancy made;<br>
 Excepting royal Galaphron alone;<br>
 Who had it forged, and gave it to his son.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVI.</td><td>
 Nay, bold Astolpho, and the lady who<br>
 Afterwards bore it, deemed that not to spell,<br>
 But simply to their proper force, was due<br>
 The praise that they in knightly joust excel;<br>
 And with whatever spear they fought, those two<br>
 Believed that they should have performed as well.<br>
 What only makes that knight the joust forego<br>
 Is that he would not his Frontino show.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVII.</td><td>
 For easily that steed of generous kind<br>
 She might have known, if him she had espied;<br>
 Whom in Montalban, long to her consigned,<br>
 The gentle damsel had been wont to ride.<br>
 Rogero, that but schemes, but hath in mind<br>
 How he from Brandamant himself shall hide,<br>
 Neither Frontino nor yet other thing.<br>
 Whereby he may be known, afield will bring.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVIII.</td><td>
 With a new sword will he the maid await;<br>
 For well he knew against the enchanted blade<br>
 As soft as paste would prove all mail and plate;<br>
 For never any steel its fury stayed;<br>
 And heavily with hammer, to rebate<br>
 Its edge, as well he on this faulchion layed.<br>
 So armed, Rogero in the lists appeared,<br>
 When the first dawn of day the horizon cheered.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIX.</td><td>
 To look like Leo, o’er his breast is spread<br>
 The surcoat that the prince is wont to wear;<br>
 And the gold eagle with its double head<br>
 He blazoned on the crimson shield doth bear;<br>
 And (what the Child’s disguisement well may stead)<br>
 Of equal size and stature are the pair.<br>
 In the other’s form presents himself the one;<br>
 That other lets himself be seen of none.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXX.</td><td>
 Dordona’s martial maid is of a vein<br>
 Right different from the gentle youth’s, who sore<br>
 Hammers and blunts the faulchion’s tempered grain,<br>
 Lest it his opposite should cleave or bore.<br>
 She whets her steel, and into it would fain<br>
 Enter, that stripling to the quick to gore:<br>
 Yea, would such fury to her strokes impart,<br>
 That each should go directly to his heart.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 As on the start the generous barb in spied,<br>
 When he the signal full of fire attends;<br>
 And paws now here now there; and opens wide<br>
 His nostrils, and his pointed ears extends;<br>
 So the bold damsel, to the lists defied,<br>
 Who knows not with Rogero she contends,<br>
 Seemed to have fire within her veins, nor found<br>
 Resting-place, waiting for the trumpet’s sound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 As sometimes after thunder sudden wind<br>
 Turns the sea upside down; and far and nigh<br>
 Dim clouds of dust the cheerful daylight blind,<br>
 Raised in a thought from earth, and whirled heaven-high;<br>
 Scud beasts and herd together with the hind;<br>
 And into hail and rain dissolves the sky;<br>
 So she upon the signal bared her brand,<br>
 And fell on her Rogero, sword in hand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 But well-built wall, strong tower, or aged oak,<br>
 No more are moved by blasts that round them rave,<br>
 No more by furious sea is moved the rock,<br>
 Smote day and night by the tempestuous wave,<br>
 Than in those arms, secure from hostile stroke,<br>
 Which erst to Trojan Hector Vulcan gave,<br>
 Moved was he by that ire and hatred rank<br>
 Which stormed about his head, and breast, and flank.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 Now aims that martial maid a trenchant blow,<br>
 And now gives point; and wholly is intent<br>
 ’Twixt plate and plate to reach her hated foe;<br>
 So that her stifled fury she may vent:<br>
 Now on this side, now that, now high, now low<br>
 She strikes, and circles him, on mischief bent;<br>
 And evermore she rages and repines;<br>
 As balked of every purpose she designs.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 As he that layeth siege to well-walled town,<br>
 And flanked about with solid bulwarks, still<br>
 Renews the assault; now fain would batter down<br>
 Gateway or tower; now gaping fosse would fill;<br>
 Yet vainly toils (for entrance is there none)<br>
 And wastes his host, aye frustrate of his will;<br>
 So sorely toils and strives without avail<br>
 The damsel, nor can open plate or mail.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 Sparks now his shield, now helm, now cuirass scatter,<br>
 While straight and back strokes, aimed now low, now high,<br>
 Which good Rogero’s head and bosom batter,<br>
 And arms, by thousands and by thousands fly<br>
 Faster than on the sounding farm-roof patter<br>
 Hailstones descending from a troubled sky.<br>
 Rogero, at his ward, with dexterous care,<br>
 Defends himself, and ne’er offends the fair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 Now stopt, now circled, now retired the knight,<br>
 And oft his hand his foot accompanied;<br>
 And lifted shield, and shifted sword in fight,<br>
 Where shifting he the hostile hand espied.<br>
 Either he smote her not, or—die he smite—<br>
 Smote, where he deemed least evil would betide.<br>
 The lady, ere the westering sun descend,<br>
 Desires to bring that duel to an end.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 Of the edict she remembered her, and knew<br>
 Her peril, save the foe was quickly sped:<br>
 For if she took not in one day nor slew<br>
 Her claimant, she was taken; and his head<br>
 Phœbus was now about to hide from view,<br>
 Nigh Hercules’ pillars, in his watery bed,<br>
 When first she ’gan misdoubt her power to cope<br>
 With the strong foe, and to abandon hope.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIX.</td><td>
 By how much more hope fails the damsel, so<br>
 Much more her anger waxes; she her blows<br>
 Redoubling, yet the harness of her foe<br>
 Will break, which through that day unbroken shows;<br>
 As he, that at his daily drudgery slow,<br>
 Sees night on his unfinished labour close,<br>
 Hurries and toils and moils without avail,<br>
 Till wearied strength and light together fail.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXX.</td><td>
 Didst thou, O miserable damsel, trow<br>
 Whom thou wouldst kill, if in that cavalier<br>
 Matched against thee thou didst Rogero know,<br>
 On whom depend thy very life-threads, ere<br>
 Thou killed him thou wouldst kill thyself; for thou,<br>
 I know, dost hold him than thyself more dear;<br>
 And when he for Rogero shall be known,<br>
 I know these very strokes thou wilt bemoan.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXI.</td><td>
 King Charles and peers him sheathed in plate and shell<br>
 Deem not Rogero, but the emperor’s son;<br>
 And viewing in that combat fierce and fell<br>
 Such force and quickness by the stripling shown;<br>
 And, without e’er offending her, how well<br>
 That knight defends himself, now change their tone;<br>
 Esteem both well assorted; and declare<br>
 The champions worthy of each other are.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXII.</td><td>
 When Phœbus wholly under water goes,<br>
 Charlemagne bids the warring pair divide;<br>
 And Bradamant (nor boots it to oppose)<br>
 Allots to youthful Leo as a bride.<br>
 Not there Rogero tarried to repose;<br>
 Nor loosed his armour, nor his helm untied:<br>
 On a small hackney, hurrying sore, he went<br>
 Where Leo him awaited in his tent.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIII.</td><td>
 Twice in fraternal guise and oftener threw<br>
 Leo his arms about the cavalier;<br>
 And next his helmet from his head withdrew,<br>
 And kiss’d him on both cheeks with loving cheer.<br>
 “I would,” he cried, “that thou wouldst ever do<br>
 By me what pleaseth thee; for thou wilt ne’er<br>
 Weary my love: at any call I lend<br>
 To thee myself and state; these friendly spend;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 “Nor see I recompense, which can repay<br>
 The mighty obligation that I owe;<br>
 Though of the garland I should disarray<br>
 My brows, and upon thee that gift bestow.”<br>
 Rogero, on whom his sorrows press and prey,<br>
 Who loathes his life, immersed in that deep woe,<br>
 Little replies; the ensigns he had worn<br>
 Returns, and takes again his unicorn;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 And showing himself spiritless and spent,<br>
 From thence as quickly as he could withdrew,<br>
 And from young Leo’s to his lodgings went;<br>
 When it was midnight, armed himself anew,<br>
 Saddled his horse, and sallied from his tent;<br>
 (He takes no leave, and none his going view;)<br>
 And his Frontino to that road addrest,<br>
 Which seemed to please the goodly courser best.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 Now by straight way and now by crooked wound<br>
 Frontino, now by wood and wide champaign;<br>
 And all night with his rider paced that round,<br>
 Who never ceased a moment to complain:<br>
 He called on Death, and therein comfort found;<br>
 Since broke by him alone is stubborn pain;<br>
 Nor saw, save Death, what other power could close<br>
 The account of his insufferable woes.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 “Whereof should I complain,” he said, “wo is me!<br>
 So of my every good at once forlorn?<br>
 Ah! if I will not bear this injury<br>
 Without revenge, against whom shall I turn?<br>
 For I, besides myself, none other see<br>
 That hath inflicted on me scathe and scorn.<br>
 Then I to take revenge for all the harm<br>
 Done to myself, against myself must arm.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 “Yet was but to myself this injury done,<br>
 Myself to spare (because this touched but me)<br>
 I haply could, yet hardly could, be won;<br>
 Nay, I will say outright, I could not be.<br>
 Less can I be, since not to me alone,<br>
 But Bradamant, is done this injury;<br>
 Even if I could consent myself to spare,<br>
 It fits me not unvenged to leave that fair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 “Then I the damsel will avenge, and die,<br>
 (Nor this disturbs me) whatsoe’er betide;<br>
 For, bating death, I know not aught, whereby<br>
 Defence against my grief can be supplied.<br>
 But I lament myself alone, that I<br>
 Before offending her, should not have died.<br>
 O happier Fortune! had I breathed my last<br>
 In Theodora’s dungeon prisoned fast!</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 “Though she had slain, had tortured me before<br>
 She slew, as prompted by her cruelty,<br>
 At least the hope would have remained in store<br>
 That I by Bradamant should pitied be:<br>
 But when she knows that I loved Leo more<br>
 Than her, that, of my own accord and free,<br>
 Myself of her, I for his good, deprive,<br>
 Dead will she rightly hate me or alive.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 These words he said and many more, with sigh<br>
 And heavy sob withal accompanied,<br>
 And, when another sun illumed the sky,<br>
 Mid strange and gloomy woods himself espied;<br>
 And, for he desperate was and bent to die,<br>
 And he, as best he could, his death would hide;<br>
 This place to him seemed far removed from view,<br>
 And fitted for the deed that he would do.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 He entered into that dark woodland, where<br>
 He thickest trees and most entangled spied:<br>
 But first Frontino was the warrior’s care,<br>
 Whom he unharnessed wholly, and untied.<br>
 “O my Frontino, if thy merits rare<br>
 I could reward, thou little cause” (he cried)<br>
 “Shouldst have to envy him, so highly graced,<br>
 Who soared to heaven, and mid the stars was placed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 “Nor Cillarus, nor Arion, was whilere<br>
 Worthier than thee, nor merited more praise;<br>
 Nor any other steed, whose name we hear<br>
 Sounded in Grecian or in Latin lays.<br>
 Was any such in other points thy peer,<br>
 None of them, well I know, the vaunt can raise;<br>
 That such high honour and such courtesy<br>
 Were upon him bestowed, as were on thee.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 “Since to the gentlest maid, of fairest dye,<br>
 And boldest that hath been, or evermore<br>
 Will be, thou wast so dear, she used to tie<br>
 Thy trappings, and to thee thy forage bore:<br>
 Dear wast thou to my lady-love: Ah! why<br>
 Call I her mine, since she is mine no more?<br>
 If I have given her to another lord,<br>
 Why turn I not upon myself this sword?”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 If him these thoughts so harass and torment,<br>
 That bird and beast are softened by his cries;<br>
 (For, saving these, none hears the sad lament,<br>
 Nor sees the flood that trickles form his eyes)<br>
 You are not to believe that more content<br>
 The Lady Bradamant in Paris lies;<br>
 Who can no longer her delay excuse,<br>
 Nor Leo for her wedded lord refuse.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVI.</td><td>
 Ere she herself to any consort tie,<br>
 Beside her own Rogero, she will fain<br>
 Do what so can be done; her word belie;<br>
 Anger friends, kindred, court, and Charlemagne;<br>
 And if she nothing else can do, will die,<br>
 By poison or her own good faulchion slain:<br>
 For not to live appears far lesser woe,<br>
 Than, living, her Rogero to forego.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVII.</td><td>
 “Rogero mine, ah! wonder gone” (she cried)<br>
 “Art thou; and canst thou so far distant be,<br>
 Thou heardest not this royal edict cried,<br>
 A thing concealed from none, expecting thee?<br>
 Faster than thee would none have hither hied,<br>
 I wot, hadst thou known this; ah! wretched me!<br>
 How can I e’er in future think of aught,<br>
 Saving the worst that can by me be thought?</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVIII.</td><td>
 “How can it be, Rogero, thou alone<br>
 Hast read not what by all the world is read?<br>
 If thou hast read it not, nor hither flown,<br>
 How canst thou but a prisoner be, or dead?<br>
 But well I wot, that if the truth were known,<br>
 This Leo will for thee some snare have spread:<br>
 The traitor will have barred thy way, intent<br>
 Thou shouldst not him by better speed prevent.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIX.</td><td>
 “From Charles I gained the promise, that to none<br>
 Less puissant than myself should I be given;<br>
 In the reliance thou wouldst be that one,<br>
 With whom I should in arms have vainly striven.<br>
 None I esteemed, excepting thee alone:<br>
 But well my rashness is rebuked by Heaven:<br>
 Since I by one am taken in this wise<br>
 Unfamed through life for any fair emprize.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">C.</td><td>
 “If I am held as taken, since the knight<br>
 I had not force to take nor yet to slay;<br>
 A thing that is not, in my judgment, right;<br>
 Nor I to Charles’s sentence will give way,<br>
 I know that I shall be esteemed as light,<br>
 If what I lately said, I now unsay;<br>
 But of those many ladies that have past<br>
 For light, I am not, I, the first or last.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CI.</td><td>
 “Enough I to my lover faith maintain,<br>
 And, firmer than a rock, am still found true!<br>
 And far herein surpass the female train,<br>
 That were in olden days, or are in new!<br>
 Nor, if they me as fickle shall arraign,<br>
 Care I, so good from fickleness ensue;<br>
 Though I am lighter than a leaf be said,<br>
 So I be forced not with that Greek no wed.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CII.</td><td>
 These things and more beside the damsel bright<br>
 (’Twixt which oft sobs and tears were interposed),<br>
 Ceased not to utter through the livelong night<br>
 Which upon that unhappy day had closed.<br>
 But, when within Cimmeria’s caverned height<br>
 Nocturnus with his troops of shades reposed,<br>
 Heaven, which eternally had willed the maid<br>
 Should be Rogero’s consort, brought him aid:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIII.</td><td>
 This moves the haught Marphisa, when ’tis morn,<br>
 To appear before the king; to whom that maid<br>
 Saith, to the Child, her brother, mighty scorn<br>
 Was done; nor should he be so ill appaid,<br>
 That from him should his plighted wife be torn;<br>
 And nought thereof unto the warrior said;<br>
 And on whoever lists she will in strife<br>
 Prove Bradamant to be Rogero’s wife;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIV.</td><td>
 And this, before all others, will prove true<br>
 On her, if to deny it she will dare;<br>
 For she had to Rogero, in her view,<br>
 Spoken those words, which they that marry swear;<br>
 And with all ceremony wont and due<br>
 So was the contract sealed between the pair,<br>
 They were no longer free; nor could forsake<br>
 The one the other, other spouse to take.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CV.</td><td>
 Whether Marphisa true or falsely spake,<br>
 I well believe that, rather with intent<br>
 Young Leo’s purpose, right or wrong, to break,<br>
 Than tell the truth, she speaks; and with consent<br>
 Of Bradamant doth that avowal make:<br>
 For to exclude the hated Leo bent,<br>
 And of Rogero to be repossest,<br>
 This she believes her shortest way and best.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVI.</td><td>
 Sorely by this disturbed, King Charlemagne<br>
 Bade Bradamant be called, and to her told<br>
 That which the proud Marphisa would maintain;<br>
 And Aymon present in the press behold!<br>
 —Bradamant drops her head, nor treats as vain,<br>
 Nor vouches what avows that virgin bold,<br>
 In such confusion, they may well believe<br>
 That fierce Marphisa speaks not to deceive.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVII.</td><td>
 Joy good Orlando and joy Rinaldo show,<br>
 Who view in valorous Marphisa’s plea<br>
 A cause the alliance shall no further go,<br>
 Which sealed already Leo deemed to be;<br>
 And yet, in spite of stubborn Aymon’s no,<br>
 Bradamant shall Rogero’s consort be;<br>
 And they may, without strife, without despite<br>
 Done to Duke Aymon’s, give her to the knight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CVIII.</td><td>
 For if such words have pass’d between the twain,<br>
 Fast is the knot and cannot be untied;<br>
 They what they vowed more fairly will obtain,<br>
 And without further strife are these affied.<br>
 “This is a plot, a plot devised in vain;<br>
 And ye deceive yourselves (Duke Aymon cried)<br>
 For, were the story true which ye have feigned,<br>
 Believe not therefore that your cause is gained.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIX.</td><td>
 “For granting what I will not yet allow,<br>
 And what I to believe as yet demur;<br>
 That weakly to Rogero so her vow<br>
 Was plighted, as Rogero’s was to her;<br>
 Where was the contract made, and when and how?<br>
 More clearly this to me must ye aver.<br>
 Either it was not so, I am advised;<br>
 Or was before Rogero was baptized.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CX.</td><td>
 “But if it were before the youthful knight<br>
 A Christian was, I will not heed it, I;<br>
 For ’twixt a faithful and a paynim wight,<br>
 I deem that nought avails the marriage-tie.<br>
 For this not vainly in the doubtful fight<br>
 Should Constantine’s fair son have risked to die;<br>
 Nor Charlemagne for this, our sovereign lord<br>
 Will forfeit, I believe, his plighted word.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXI.</td><td>
 “What now you say you should before have said,<br>
 While yet the matter was unbroke, and ere<br>
 Charles at my daughter’s prayer that edict made<br>
 Which has drawn Leo to the combat here.”<br>
 Orlando and Rinaldo were gainsayed<br>
 So before royal Charles by Clermont’s peer;<br>
 And equal Charlemagne heard either side,<br>
 But neither would for this nor that decide.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXII.</td><td>
 As in the southern or the northern breeze<br>
 The greenwood murmurs; and as on the shore,<br>
 When Aeolus with the god that rules the seas<br>
 Is wroth, the hoarse and hollow breakers roar,<br>
 So a loud rumour of this strife, that flees<br>
 Through France, and spreads and circles evermore,<br>
 Affords such matter to rehearse and hear,<br>
 That nought beside is bruised far or near.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXIII.</td><td>
 These with Rogero, those with Leo side;<br>
 But the most numerous are Rogero’s friends,<br>
 Who against Aymon, ten to one, divide.<br>
 Good Charlemagne to neither party bends;<br>
 But wills that cause shall be by justice tried,<br>
 And to his parliament the matter sends.<br>
 Marphisa, now the bridal was deferred,<br>
 Appeared anew, and other question stirred;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXIV.</td><td>
 And said, “In that anther cannot have<br>
 Bradamant, while my brother is alive,<br>
 Let Leo, if the gentle maid he crave,<br>
 His foe in listed fight of life deprive;<br>
 And he, that sends the other to his grave,<br>
 Freed from his rival, with the lady wive.”<br>
 Forthwith this challenge, as erewhile the rest,<br>
 To Leo was declared at Charles’ behest.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXV.</td><td>
 Leo who if he had the cavalier<br>
 Of the unicorn, believed he from his foe<br>
 Was safe; and thought no peril would appear<br>
 Too hard a feat for him; and knew not how<br>
 Thence into solitary woods and drear<br>
 That warrior had been hurried by his woe;<br>
 Him gone for little time and for disport<br>
 Believed, and took his line in evil sort.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXVI.</td><td>
 This shortly Leo was condemned to rue:<br>
 For he, on whom too fondly he relied,<br>
 Nor on that day nor on the following two<br>
 Appeared, nor news of him were signified;<br>
 And combat with Rogero was, he knew,<br>
 Unsafe, unless that knight was on his side:<br>
 So sent, to eschew the threatened scathe and scorn,<br>
 To seek the warrior of the unicorn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CXVII.</td><td>
 Through city, and through hamlet, and through town,<br>
 He sends to seek Rogero, far and near:<br>
 And not content with this, himself is gone<br>
 In person, on his steed, to find the peer.<br>
 But of the missing warrior tidings none<br>
 Nor he nor any of the Court would hear<br>
 But for Melissa: I for other verse<br>
 Reserve myself, her doings to rehearse.</td></tr>
