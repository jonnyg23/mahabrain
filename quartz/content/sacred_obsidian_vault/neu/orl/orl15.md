 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 15</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 Round about Paris every where are spread<br>
 The assailing hosts of Africa and Spain.<br>
 Astolpho home by Logistilla sped,<br>
 Binds first Caligorantes with his chain;<br>
 Next from Orrilo’s trunk divides the head;<br>
 With whom Sir Aquilant had warred in vain,<br>
 And Gryphon bold: next Sansonet discerns,<br>
 Ill tidings of his lady Gryphon learns.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 Though Conquest fruit of skill or fortune be,<br>
 To conquer always is a glorious thing.<br>
 ’Tis true, indeed, a bloody victory<br>
 Is to a chief less honour wont to bring;<br>
 And that fair field is famed eternally,<br>
 And he who wins it merits worshipping,<br>
 Who, saving from all harm his own, without<br>
 Loss to his followers, puts the foe to rout.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 You, sir, earned worthy praise, when you o’erbore<br>
 The lion of such might by sea, and so<br>
 Did by him, where he guarded either shore<br>
 From Francolino to the mouth of Po,<br>
 That I, though yet again I heard him roar,<br>
 If you were present, should my fear forego.<br>
 How fields are fitly won was then made plain;<br>
 For we were rescued, and your foemen slain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 This was the Paynim little skilled to do,<br>
 Who was but daring to his proper loss;<br>
 And to the moat impelled his meiny, who<br>
 One and all perished in the burning fosse.<br>
 The mighty gulf had not contained the crew,<br>
 But that, devouring those who sought to cross,<br>
 Them into dust the flame reduced, that room<br>
 Might be for all within the crowded tomb.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 Of twenty thousand warriors thither sent,<br>
 Died nineteen thousand in the fiery pit;<br>
 Who to the fosse descended, ill content;<br>
 But so their leader willed, of little wit:<br>
 Extinguished amid such a blaze, and spent<br>
 By the devouring flame the Christians lit.<br>
 And Rodomont, occasion of their woes,<br>
 Exempted from the mighty mischief goes:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 For he to the inner bank, by foes possest,<br>
 Across the ditch had vaulted wonderously:<br>
 Had he within it been, among the rest,<br>
 It sure had been his last assault. His eye<br>
 He turns, and when the wild-fires, which infest<br>
 The infernal vale, he sees ascend so high,<br>
 And hears his people’s moan and dying screams,<br>
 With imprecations dread he Heaven blasphemes.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 This while a band King Agramant had brought,<br>
 To make a fierce assault upon a gate:<br>
 For while the cruel battle here was fought,<br>
 Wherein so many sufferers met their fate,<br>
 This haply unprovided had he thought<br>
 With fitting guard. Upon the monarch wait<br>
 King Bambirago, ’mid his knights of price,<br>
 And Baliverso, sink of every vice.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 And Corineus of Mulga, Prusion,<br>
 The wealthy monarch of the blessed isles;<br>
 Malabuferzo, he who fills the throne<br>
 Of Fez, where a perpetual summer smiles;<br>
 And other noble lords, and many a one<br>
 Well-armed and tried; and others ’mid their files,<br>
 Naked, and base, whose hearts in martial fields<br>
 Had found no shelter from a thousand shields.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 But all things counter to the hopes ensue<br>
 Of Agramant upon his side; within,<br>
 In person, girded by a gallant crew,<br>
 Is Charlemagne, with many a paladin:<br>
 Ogier the Duke, King Salamon, the two<br>
 Guidos are seen, and either Angelin;<br>
 Bavaria’s duke, and Ganelon are here,<br>
 Avino, Avolio, Otho, and Berlinghier.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 And of inferior count withal, a horde<br>
 Of Lombards, French, and Germans, without end;<br>
 Who, every one, in presence of his lord,<br>
 To rank among the valiantest contend,<br>
 This will I in another place record;<br>
 Who here a mighty duke perforce attend,<br>
 Who signs to me from far, and prays that I<br>
 Will not omit him in my history.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 ’Tis time that I should measure back my way<br>
 Thither, where I Astolpho left of yore;<br>
 Who, in long exile, loathing more to stay,<br>
 Burnt with desire to tread his native shore;<br>
 As hopes to him had given the sober fay,<br>
 Who quelled Alcina by her better lore,<br>
 She with all care would send the warrior back<br>
 By the securest and the freest track.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 And thus by her a barque is fitted out;<br>
 —A better galley never ploughed the sea;<br>
 And Logistilla wills, for aye in doubt<br>
 Of hinderance from Alcina’s treachery,<br>
 That good Andronica, with squadron stout,<br>
 And chaste Sophrosina, with him shall be,<br>
 Till to the Arabian Sea, beneath their care,<br>
 Or to the Persian Gulf he safe repair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 By Scyth and Indian she prefers the peer<br>
 Should coast, and by the Nabataean reign;<br>
 Content he, after such a round, should veer<br>
 For Persian gulf, or Erithraean main,<br>
 Rather than for that Boreal palace steer,<br>
 Where angry winds aye vex the rude domain:<br>
 So ill, at seasons, favoured by the sun,<br>
 That there, for months together, light is none.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 Next, when she all in readiness espied,<br>
 Her license to depart the prudent fay<br>
 Accorded to the duke, first fortified<br>
 With counsel as to things too long to say;<br>
 And that he might no more by charms be stayed<br>
 In place from whence he could not wend his way,<br>
 Him with a useful book and fair purveyed,<br>
 And ever for her love to wear it prayed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 How man should guard himself from magic cheats<br>
 The book instructed, which the fay bestowed;<br>
 At the end or the beginning, where it treats<br>
 Of such, an index and appendix showed.<br>
 Another gift, which in its goodly feats<br>
 All other gifts excelled, to her he owed;<br>
 This was a horn, which made whatever wight<br>
 Should hear its clang betake himself to flight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 I say, the horn is of such horrid sound,<br>
 That, wheresoe’er ’tis heard, all fly for fear;<br>
 Nor in the world is one of heart so sound<br>
 That would not fly, should he the bugle hear.<br>
 Wind, thunder, and the shock which rives the ground,<br>
 Come not, in aught, the hideous clangour near.<br>
 With thanks did the good Englishman receive<br>
 The gift, and of the fairy took his leave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 Quitting the port and smoother waves, they stand<br>
 To sea, with favouring wind which blows astern;<br>
 And (coasting) round the rich and populous land<br>
 Of odoriferous Ind the vessels turn,<br>
 Opening a thousand isles on either hand,<br>
 Scattered about that sea, till they discern<br>
 The land of Thomas; here the pilot veers<br>
 His ready tiller, and more northward steers.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 Astolpho, furrowing that ocean hoar,<br>
 Marks, as he coasts, the wealthy land at ease.<br>
 Ganges amid the whitening waters roar,<br>
 Nigh skirting now the golden Chersonese;<br>
 Taprobana with Cori next, and sees<br>
 The frith which chafes against its double shore;<br>
 Makes distant Cochin, and with favouring wind<br>
 Issues beyond the boundaries of Ind.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 Scouring at large broad ocean, with a guide<br>
 So faithful and secure, the cavalier<br>
 Questions Andronica, if from that side<br>
 Named from the westering sun, of this our sphere,<br>
 Bark, which with oars or canvas stemmed the tide,<br>
 On eastern sea was wonted to appear;<br>
 —And could a wight, who loosed from Indian strand,<br>
 Reach France or Britain, without touching land.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 Andronica to England’s duke replies:<br>
 “Know that this earth is girt about with seas,<br>
 And all to one another yield supplies,<br>
 Whether the circling waters boil or freeze:<br>
 But, since the Aethiops’ land before us lies,<br>
 Extending southward many long degrees.<br>
 Across his waters, some one has supposed<br>
 A barrier here to Neptune interposed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 “Hence bark from this Levant of Ind is none<br>
 Which weighs, to shape her course for Europe’s shore;<br>
 Nor navigates from Europe any one,<br>
 Our Oriental regions to explore;<br>
 Fain to retrace alike the course begun<br>
 By the mid land, extending wide before:<br>
 Weening (its limits of such length appear)<br>
 That it must join another hemisphere.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 “But in the course of circling years I view<br>
 From farthest lands which catch the western ray,<br>
 New Argonauts put forth, and Tiphys new<br>
 Opening, till now an undiscovered way.<br>
 Others I see coast Afric, and pursue<br>
 So far the negroes’ burning shore, that they<br>
 Pass the far sign, from whence, on his return,<br>
 The sun moves hither, leaving Capricorn;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 “And find the limit of this length of land,<br>
 Which makes a single sea appear as two;<br>
 Who, scouring in their frigates every strand,<br>
 Pass Ind and Arab isles, or Persian through:<br>
 Others I see who leave, on either hand,<br>
 The banks, which stout Alcides cleft in two,<br>
 And in the manner of the circling sun,<br>
 To seek new lands and new creations run.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 “The imperial flags and holy cross I know,<br>
 Fixed on the verdant shore; see some upon<br>
 The shattered barks keep guard, and others go<br>
 A-field, by whom new countries will be won;<br>
 Ten chase a thousand of the flying foe,<br>
 Realms beyond Ind subdued by Arragon;<br>
 And see all, wheresoe’er the warriors wend,<br>
 To the fifth Charles’ triumphant captains bend.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 “That this way should be hidden was God’s will<br>
 Of old, and ere ’twas known long time should run;<br>
 Nor will he suffer its discovery, till<br>
 The sixth and seventh century be done.<br>
 And he delays his purpose to fulfil,<br>
 In that he would subject the world to one,<br>
 The justest and most fraught with prudent lore<br>
 Or emperors, since Augustus, or before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 “Of Arragon and Austria’s blood I see<br>
 On the left bank of Rhine a monarch bred;<br>
 No sovereign is so famed in history,<br>
 Of all whose goodly deeds are heard or read.<br>
 Astraea reinthroned by him will be,—<br>
 Rather restored to life, long seeming dead;<br>
 And Virtues with her into exile sent,<br>
 By him shall be recalled from banishment.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 “For such desert, Heaven’s bounty not alone<br>
 Designs he should the imperial garland bear,—<br>
 Augustus’, Trajan’s, Mark’s, Severus’, crown;<br>
 But that of every farthest land should wear,<br>
 Which here and there extends, as yet unknown,<br>
 Yielding no passage to the sun and year;<br>
 And wills that in his time Christ’s scattered sheep<br>
 Should be one flock, beneath one Shepherd’s keep.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 “And that this be accomplished with more ease,<br>
 Writ in the skies from all eternity,<br>
 Captains, invincible by lands and seas,<br>
 Shall heavenly Providence to him supply.<br>
 I mark Hernando Cortez bring, ’mid these,<br>
 New cities under Caesar’s dynasty,<br>
 And kingdoms in the Orient so remote,<br>
 That we of these in India have no note.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 “With Prospero Colonna, puissant peer,<br>
 A marquis of Pescara I behold;—<br>
 A youth of Guasto next, who render dear<br>
 Hesperia to the flower-de-luce of gold;<br>
 I see prepared to enter the career<br>
 This third, who shall the laurel win and hold;<br>
 As a good horse before the rest will dart,<br>
 And first attain the goal, though last to start.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 “I see such faith, such valour in the deeds<br>
 Of young Alphonso (such his name) confest,<br>
 He in his unripe age,—nor he exceeds<br>
 His sixth and twentieth year,—at Caesar’s hest,<br>
 (A mighty trust) the imperial army leads:<br>
 Saving which, Caesar not alone the rest<br>
 Of his fair empire saves, but may the world<br>
 Reduce, with ensigns by this chief unfurled.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 “As with these captains, where the way by land<br>
 Is free, he spreads the ancient empire’s sway,<br>
 So on the sea, which severs Europe’s strand<br>
 From Afric, open to the southern day,<br>
 When with good Doria linked in friendly band,<br>
 Victorious he shall prove in every fray.<br>
 This is that Andrew Doria who will sweep<br>
 From pirates, on all sides, your midland deep.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 “Pompey, though he chased rovers everywhere,<br>
 Was not his peer; for ill the thievish brood<br>
 Vanquished by him, in puissance, could compare<br>
 With the most mighty realm that ever stood.<br>
 But Doria singly will of the corsair<br>
 With his own forces purge the briny flood:<br>
 So that I see each continent and isle<br>
 Quake at his name, from Calpe to the Nile.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 “Beneath the faith, beneath the warrantry<br>
 Of the redoubted chief, of whom I say,<br>
 I see Charles enter fertile Italy,<br>
 To which this captain clears the monarch’s way;<br>
 But on his country, not himself, that fee<br>
 Shall he bestow, which is his labour’s pay;<br>
 And beg her freedom, where himself perchance<br>
 Another would to sovereign rule advance.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 “The pious love he bears his native land<br>
 Honours him more than any battle’s gain<br>
 Which Julius ever won on Afric’s strand,<br>
 Or in thine isle, France, Thessaly, or Spain.<br>
 Nor great Octavius does more praise command,<br>
 Nor Anthony who jousted for the reign,<br>
 With equal arms: in that the wrong outweighs<br>
 —Done to their native land—their every praise.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 “Let these, and every other wight who tries<br>
 To subject a free country, blush for shame,<br>
 Nor dare in face of man to lift his eyes,<br>
 Where he hears Andrew Doria’s honoured name!<br>
 To him I see Charles other meed supplies;<br>
 For he beside his leaders’ common claim,<br>
 Bestows upon the chief the sumptuous state,<br>
 Whence Norman bands their power in Puglia date.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 “Not only to this captain courtesy<br>
 Shall Charles display, still liberal of his store;<br>
 But to all those who for the empery<br>
 In his emprizes have not spared their gore.<br>
 Him to bestow a town,—a realm—I see,<br>
 Upon a faithful friend, rejoicing more,<br>
 And on all such as have good service done,<br>
 Than in new kingdom and new empire won.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 Thus of the victories, by land and main,<br>
 Which, when long course of years shall be complete,<br>
 Charles’ worthy captains for their lord will gain,<br>
 Andronica did with Astolpho treat.<br>
 This while, now loosening, tightening now, the rein<br>
 On the eastern winds, which blow upon their feet,<br>
 Making this serve or that, her comrade stands;<br>
 While the blasts rise or sink as she commands.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 This while they saw, as for their port they made,<br>
 How wide the Persian sea extends to sight;<br>
 Whence in few days the squadron was conveyed<br>
 Nigh the famed gulf from ancient Magi hight;<br>
 Here they found harbourage; and here were stayed<br>
 Their wandering barks, which stern to shore were dight.<br>
 Secure from danger from Alcina’s wrath,<br>
 The duke by land continued hence his path.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 He pricks through many a field and forest blind,<br>
 By many a vale and many a mountain gray;<br>
 Where robbers, now before and now behind,<br>
 Oft threat the peer by night or open day;<br>
 Lion and dragon oft of poisonous kind,<br>
 And other savage monsters cross his way:<br>
 But he no sooner has his bugle wound,<br>
 Than these are scared and scattered by the sound.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 Through Araby the blest he fares, where grow<br>
 Thickets of myrrh, and gums odorous ooze,<br>
 Where the sole phoenix makes her nest, although<br>
 The world is all before her where to choose;<br>
 And to the avenging sea which whelmed the foe<br>
 Of Israel, his way the duke pursues;<br>
 In which King Pharaoh and his host were lost:<br>
 From whence he to the land of heroes crost.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 Astolpho along Trajan’s channel goes,<br>
 Upon that horse which has no earthly peer,<br>
 And moves so lightly, that the soft sand shows<br>
 No token of the passing cavalier;<br>
 Who prints not grass, prints not the driven snows,<br>
 —Who dry-shod would the briny billows clear,<br>
 And strains so nimbly in the course, he wind<br>
 And thunderbolt and arrow leaves behind:—</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 Erst Argalìa’s courser, which was born<br>
 From a close union of the wind and flame,<br>
 And, nourished not by hay or heartening corn,<br>
 Fed on pure air, and Rabican his name.<br>
 His way the bearer of the magic horn<br>
 Following, where Nile received that river, came;<br>
 But ere he at its outlet could arrive,<br>
 Towards him saw a pinnace swiftly drive.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 A hermit in the poop the bark did guide<br>
 With snowy beard descending to mid breast;<br>
 Who when from far the Paladin be spied,<br>
 Him to ascend his ready pinnace prest.<br>
 “My son, unless thou loathest life, (he cried)<br>
 And wouldst that Death to-day thy course arrest,<br>
 Content thee in my bark to cross the water;<br>
 For yonder path conducts thee straight to slaughter.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 “Within six miles, no further, shalt thou light<br>
 (Pursued the hermit) on the bloody seat,<br>
 Where dwells a giant, horrible to sight,<br>
 Exceeding every stature by eight feet.<br>
 From him wayfaring man or errant knight<br>
 Would vainly hope with life to make retreat;<br>
 For some the felon quarters, some he flays,<br>
 And some he swallows quick, and some he slays.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 “He, ’mid the cruel horrors he intends,<br>
 Takes pleasure in a net, by cunning hands<br>
 Contrived, which near his mansion he extends;<br>
 So well concealed beneath the crumbling sands,<br>
 That whoso uninstructed thither wends,<br>
 Nought of the subtle mischief understands;<br>
 And so the giant scares him with his cries,<br>
 That he within the toils in terror flies;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 “Whom with loud laughter, to his seat hard by<br>
 He drags along, enveloped in his snare;<br>
 And knight and damsel views with equal eye,<br>
 And for his prisoners’ worth has little care.<br>
 Then, having sucked their brains and life-blood dry,<br>
 Casts forth their bones upon the desert lair;<br>
 And round about his griesly palace pins,<br>
 For horrid ornament, their bloody skins.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 “Take this,—my son, oh! take this other way,<br>
 Which thee will to the sea in safety guide.”<br>
 “I thank thee, holy father, for thy say,<br>
 (To him the fearless cavalier replied)<br>
 But cannot peril against honour weigh,<br>
 Far dearer than my life. To the other side<br>
 Me vainly dost thou move to pass the wave;<br>
 Rather for this I seek the giant’s cave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 “I with dishonour life to flight may owe;<br>
 But worse than death loath thus to save my head.<br>
 The worst that can befall me if I go,<br>
 Is I my blood shall with the others shed:<br>
 But if on me such mercy God bestow,<br>
 That I remain alive, the giant dead,<br>
 Secure for thousands shall I make the ways;<br>
 So that the greater good the risque o’erpays.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 “I peril but the single life of one<br>
 Against safety of the countless rest.”<br>
 —“Go then in peace,” (the other said) “my son,<br>
 And to thy succour, form among the blest,<br>
 May God dispatch the Archangel Michael down.”<br>
 —And him, with that, the simple hermit blest.<br>
 Astolpho pricks along Nile’s rosy strand,<br>
 More in his horn confiding than his brand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 Between the mighty river and the fen,<br>
 A path upon the sandy shore doth lie,<br>
 Barred by the giant’s solitary den<br>
 Cut off from converse with humanity.<br>
 About it heads and naked limbs of men<br>
 Were fixed, the victims of his cruelty.<br>
 Window or battlements was not, whence strung<br>
 Might not be seen some wretched prisoner hung.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 As in hill-farm or castle, fenced with moat,<br>
 The hunter, mindful what his dangers were,<br>
 Aye fastens on his door the shaggy coat<br>
 And horrid paws and monstrous head of bear;<br>
 So showed the giant those of greatest note,<br>
 Who, thither brought, had perished in his snare.<br>
 The bones of countless others wide were spread,<br>
 And every ditch with human blood was red.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 Caligorant was standing at the gate<br>
 (For so was the despiteous monster hight);<br>
 Who decked his house with corpses, as for state<br>
 Some theirs with cloth of gold and scarlet dight.<br>
 He scarce contained himself for joy, so great<br>
 His pleasure, when the duke appeared in sight;<br>
 For ’twas two months complete, a third was near,<br>
 Since by that road had past a cavalier.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 Towards the marish, where green rushes grow,<br>
 He hastes, intending from that covert blind<br>
 To double on his unsuspecting foe,<br>
 And issue on the cavalier behind:<br>
 For him to drive into the net, below<br>
 The sand, the griesly giant had designed;<br>
 As others trapt he had been wont to see,<br>
 Brought thither by their evil destiny.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 When him the wary paladin espied,<br>
 He stopt his courser, not without great heed,<br>
 Lest he into the covert snare might tide,<br>
 Forewarned of this by the good hermit’s rede.<br>
 Here to his horn for succour he applied,<br>
 Nor failed its wonted virtue in this need:<br>
 It smote the giant’s heart with such affright,<br>
 That he turned back, and homeward fled outright.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
 Astolpho blew, still watchful of surprise,<br>
 Weening to see the engine sprung: fast flew<br>
 The giant,—as if heart as well as eyes<br>
 The thief had lost,—nor whitherward he knew:<br>
 Such is his fear, he kens not as he flies,<br>
 How is own covert mischief to eschew:<br>
 He runs into the net, which closing round,<br>
 Hampers the wretch, and drags him to the ground.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 Astolpho, who beholds his bulky prey<br>
 Fall bodily, drives thither at full speed,<br>
 Secure himself, and, bent—to make him pay<br>
 The price of slaughtered thousands—quits his steed.<br>
 Yet after, deems a helpless wight to slay<br>
 No valour were, but rather foul misdeed:<br>
 For him, arms, neck, and feet, so closely tied,<br>
 He could not shake himself, the warrior spied.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 With subtle thread of steel had Vulcan wrought<br>
 The net of old, and with such cunning pain,<br>
 He, who to break its weakest mesh had sought,<br>
 Would have bestowed his time and toil in vain.<br>
 It was with this he Mars and Venus caught,<br>
 Who, hands and feet, were fettered by the chain:<br>
 Nor did the jealous husband weave the thread<br>
 For aught, but to surprise that pair in bed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 Mercury from the smith conveyed the prize,<br>
 Wanting to take young Chloris in the snare;<br>
 Sweet Chloris, who behind Aurora flies,<br>
 At rise of sun, through fields of liquid air,<br>
 And from her gathered garment, through the skies,<br>
 Scatters the violet, rose, and lily fair.<br>
 He for this nymph his toils so deftly set,<br>
 One day, in air he took her with the net.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 The nymph (it seems) was taken as she flew,<br>
 Where the great Aethiop river meets the brine:<br>
 The net was treasured in Canopus, through<br>
 Successive ages, in Anubis’ shrine.<br>
 After three thousand years, Caligorant drew<br>
 The sacred relict from the palace divine:<br>
 Whence with the net the impious thief returned,<br>
 Who robbed the temple and the city burned,</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 He fixed it here, beneath the sandy plain,<br>
 In mode, that all the travellers whom he chased<br>
 Ran into it, and the engine was with pain<br>
 Touched, ere it arms, and feet, and neck embraced.<br>
 From this the good Astolpho took a chain,<br>
 And with the gyve his hands behind him laced:<br>
 His arms and breast he swaddled in such guise,<br>
 He could not loose himself; then let him rise.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 After, his other knots unfastening,<br>
 (For he was turned more gentle than a maid)<br>
 Astolpho, as a show, the thief would bring,<br>
 By city, borough-town, and farm conveyed;<br>
 The net as well; than which no quainter thing<br>
 Was ever by the file and hammer made.<br>
 On him, like sumpter-nag he laid the load,<br>
 In triumph led, behind him, on his road.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
 Him helm and shield he gives alike to bear,<br>
 As to a valet; hence proceeds the peer,<br>
 Gladdening the fearful pilgrim every where,<br>
 Who joys to think, henceforth his way is clear.<br>
 So far an end does bold Astolpho fare,<br>
 He is to Memphis’ tombs already near,—<br>
 Memphis renowned for pyramids; in sight,<br>
 He marks the populous Cairo opposite.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 Ran all the people in tumultuous tide,<br>
 To see him drag the unmeasured wight along.<br>
 “How can it be,” (each to his fellow cried)<br>
 “That one so weak could master one so strong?”<br>
 Scarce can Astolpho put the press aside,<br>
 So close from every part their numbers throng;<br>
 While all admire him as a cavalier<br>
 Of mighty worth, and make him goodly cheer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIII.</td><td>
 Then Cairo was not such, as common cry<br>
 Pronounces in our age that costly seat;<br>
 —That eighteen thousand districts ill supply<br>
 Lodging to those who in her markets meet;<br>
 —And though the houses are three stories high,<br>
 Numbers are forced to sleep in the open street;<br>
 And that the soldan has a palace there<br>
 Of wonderous size, and passing rich and fair;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIV.</td><td>
 And therein (Christian renegadoes all)<br>
 Keeps fifteen thousand vassals, for his needs,<br>
 Beneath one roof supplied with bower and stall,<br>
 Themselves, and wives, and families, and steeds.<br>
 The duke desired to see the river’s fall,<br>
 And how far Nile into the sea proceeds.<br>
 At Damietta; where wayfaring wight,<br>
 He heard, was prisoner made or slain outright.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXV.</td><td>
 For at Nile’s outlet there, beside his bed,<br>
 A sturdy thief was sheltered in a tower,<br>
 Alike the native’s and the stranger’s dread,<br>
 Wont even to Cairo’s gate the road to scower.<br>
 Him no one could resist, and, it was said,<br>
 That man to slay the felon had no power.<br>
 A hundred thousand wounds he had in strife<br>
 Received, yet none could ever take his life.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVI.</td><td>
 To see if he could break the thread which tied<br>
 The felon’s life, upon his way the knight<br>
 Set forward, and to Damietta hied,<br>
 To find Orrilo, so the thief was hight;<br>
 Thence to the river’s outlet past, and spied<br>
 The sturdy castle on the margin dight;<br>
 Harboured in which the enchanted demon lay,<br>
 The fruit of a hobgoblin and a fay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVII.</td><td>
 He here Orrilo and two knights in mail<br>
 Found at fierce strife: the two ill held their own<br>
 Against him; so Orrilo did assail<br>
 The warlike pair, although himself alone;<br>
 And how much either might in arms avail,<br>
 Fame through the universal world had blown.<br>
 Of Oliviero’s seed was either plant;<br>
 Gryphon the white, and sable Aquilant.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXVIII.</td><td>
 The necromancer had this while (to say<br>
 The truth) with vantage on his side, begun<br>
 The fight, who brought a monster to the fray,<br>
 Found only in those parts, and wont to won<br>
 Ashore or under water, and to prey,<br>
 For food, on human bodies; feeding on<br>
 Poor mariners and travelling men, who fare,<br>
 Of the impending danger, unaware.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIX.</td><td>
 The monster, slaughtered by the brethren two,<br>
 Upon the sand beside the haven lies;<br>
 And hence no wrong they to Orrilo do,<br>
 Assailing him together in this guise.<br>
 Him they dismembered often and not slew:<br>
 Now he,—because dismembered,—ever dies;<br>
 For he replaces leg or hand like wax,<br>
 Which the good faulchion from his body hacks.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXX.</td><td>
 Gryphon and Aquilant by turns divide,<br>
 Now to the teeth, now breast, the enchanted wight.<br>
 The fruitless blow Orrilo does deride,<br>
 While the two baffled warriors rage for spite.<br>
 Let him who falling silver has espied<br>
 (Which mercury by alchymists is hight)<br>
 Scatter, and reunite each broken member,<br>
 Hearing my tale, what he has seen remember.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 If the thief’s head be severed by the pair,<br>
 He lights and staggers till he finds it; now<br>
 Uptaken by the nose or by the hair,<br>
 And fastened to the neck, I know not how.<br>
 This sometimes Gryphon takes, and whirled through air,<br>
 Whelms in the stream; but bootless is the throw:<br>
 For like a fish can fierce Orrilo swim;<br>
 And safely, with the head, regains the brim.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 Two ladies, meetly clad in fair array,<br>
 One damsel was in black and one in white,<br>
 And who had been the occasion of that fray,<br>
 Stood by to gaze upon the cruel fight:<br>
 Either of these was a benignant fay,<br>
 Whose care had nourished one and the other knight,<br>
 Oliver’s children; when the babes forlorn<br>
 They from the claws of two huge birds had torn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 Since, from Gismonda they had these conveyed,<br>
 Borne to a distance from their native sky.<br>
 But more to say were needless, since displaid<br>
 To the whole world has been their history.<br>
 Though the author has the father’s name mis-said;<br>
 One for another (how I know not, I)<br>
 Mistaking. Now this fearful strife the pair<br>
 Of warriors waged at both the ladies’ prayer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 Though it was noon in the happy islands, day<br>
 Had vanished in this clime, displaced by night;<br>
 And, underneath the moon’s uncertain ray,<br>
 And ill-discerned, were all things hid from sight;<br>
 When to the fort Orrilo took his way.<br>
 Since both the sable sister and the white<br>
 Were pleased the furious battle to defer,<br>
 Till a new sun should in the horizon stir.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 The duke, who by their ensigns, and yet more<br>
 Had by the sight of many a vigorous blow,<br>
 Gryphon and Aquilant long time before<br>
 Agnized, to greet the brethren was not slow:<br>
 And they, who in the peer, victorious o’er<br>
 The giant, whom he led a captive, know<br>
 The BARON OF THE PARD, (so styled at court)<br>
 Him to salute, with no less love resort.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 The ladies to repose the warriors led<br>
 To a fair palace near, their sumptuous seat:<br>
 Thence issuing courtly squire and damsel sped,<br>
 Them with lit torches in mid-way to meet.<br>
 Their goodly steeds they quit, there well bested,<br>
 Put off their arms, and in a garden sweet<br>
 Discern the ready supper duly laid<br>
 Fast by, where a refreshing fountain played.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 Here they bid bind the giant on the green,<br>
 Fast-tethered by a strong and weighty chain<br>
 To a tough oak, whose ancient trunk they ween<br>
 May well be proof against a single strain;<br>
 With that, by ten good serjeants overseen,<br>
 Lest he by night get loose, and so the train<br>
 Assault and haply harm; while careless they<br>
 Without a guard and unsuspecting lay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 At the abundant and most sumptuous board,<br>
 With costly viands (its least pleasure) fraught,<br>
 The longest topic for discourse afford<br>
 Orrilo’s prowess, and the marvel wrought;<br>
 For head or arm dissevered by the sword,<br>
 They (who upon the recent wonder thought)<br>
 Might think a dream to see him re-unite,<br>
 And but return more furious to the fight.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIX.</td><td>
 Astolpho in his book had found exprest<br>
 (That which prescribed a remedy for spell)<br>
 How he who of one hair deprived the pest<br>
 Only could him in battle hope to quell:<br>
 But this plucked out or sheared, he from his breast<br>
 Parforce the felon’s spirit would expell.<br>
 So says the volume; but instructs not where,<br>
 ’Mid locks so thickly set, to find the hair.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXX.</td><td>
 The duke no less with hope of conquest glows<br>
 Than if the palm he has already won;<br>
 As he that hopes with small expense of blows<br>
 To pluck the hair, the wizard-wight undone.<br>
 Hence does he to the youthful pair propose<br>
 The burden of that enterprize upon<br>
 Himself to take: Orrilo will he slay,<br>
 If the two brethren nought the intent gainsay,</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXI.</td><td>
 But willingly to him these yield the emprize,<br>
 Assured his toil will be bestowed in vain;<br>
 And now a new Aurora climbs the skies,<br>
 And from his walls Orrilo on the plain<br>
 Drops,—and the strife begins—Orrilo plies<br>
 The mace, the duke the sword; he ’mid a rain<br>
 Of strokes would from the body at one blow<br>
 Divorce the spirit of the enchanted foe:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXII.</td><td>
 Together with the mace he lops the fist;<br>
 And now this arm, now the other falls to ground;<br>
 Sometimes he cleaves the corslet’s iron twist,<br>
 And piecemeal shares and maims the felon round.<br>
 Orrilo re-unites the portions missed,<br>
 Found on the champagne, and again is sound:<br>
 And, though into a hundred fragments hewed,<br>
 Astolpho sees him, in a thought, renewed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIII.</td><td>
 After a thousand blows, Astolpho sped<br>
 One stroke, above the shoulders and below<br>
 The chin, which lopt away both helm and head:<br>
 Nor lights the duke less swiftly than his foe.<br>
 Then grasps the hair defiled with gore and red,<br>
 Springs in a moment on his horse, and lo!<br>
 Up-stream with it along Nile’s margin hies,<br>
 So that the thief cannot retake the prize.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 That fool, who had not marked the warrior’s feat,<br>
 Was searching in the dust to find his head;<br>
 But when he heard the charger in retreat,<br>
 Who through the forest with the plunder fled,<br>
 Leapt quickly into his own courser’s seat,<br>
 And in pursuit of bold Astolpho sped.<br>
 Fain had Orrilo shouted “Hola! stay!”<br>
 But that the duke had borne his mouth away:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 Yet pleased Astolpho had not in like guise<br>
 Borne off his heels, pursues with flowing rein.<br>
 Him Rabican, who marvellously flies,<br>
 Distances by a mighty length of plain.<br>
 This while the wizard’s head Astolpho eyes<br>
 From poll to front, above the eyebrows twain,<br>
 Searching, in haste, if he the hair can see<br>
 Which makes Orrilo’s immortality.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 Amid innumerable locks, no hair<br>
 Straiter or crisper than the rest was seen.<br>
 How then should good Astolpho, in his care<br>
 To slay the thief, so many choose between?<br>
 “To cut them all (he said) it better were.”<br>
 And since he scissors lacked and razor keen,<br>
 He wanting these, resorted to his glaive,<br>
 Which cut so well, it might be said to shave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 And, holding, by the nose, the severed head,<br>
 Close-sheared it all, behind and eke before.<br>
 He found, among the rest, the fatal thread.<br>
 Then pale became the visage, changing sore,<br>
 Turned up its eyes, and signals sore and dread<br>
 Of the last agony of nature wore;<br>
 And the headless body seated in the sell,<br>
 Shuddered its last, and from the courser fell.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 The duke returns where he the champions two<br>
 And dames had left, the trophy in his hand,<br>
 Which manifests of death the tokens true;<br>
 And shows the distant body on the sand.<br>
 I know not if they this with pleasure view,<br>
 Though him they welcome with demeanour bland:<br>
 For the intercepted victory might pain<br>
 Perchance inflict upon the envying twain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 Nor do I think that either gentle fay<br>
 With pleasure could that battle’s issue see:<br>
 Since those kind dames, because they would delay<br>
 The doleful fate which shortly was to be<br>
 In France the brethren’s lot, had in that fray<br>
 With fierce Orrilo matched the warriors free;<br>
 And so to occupy the pair had cast,<br>
 Till the sad influence of the skies were past.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 When to the castellan was certified<br>
 In Damietta, that the thief was dead,<br>
 He loosed a carrier pigeon, having tied<br>
 Beneath her wing a letter by a thread.<br>
 She went to Cairo; and, to scatter wide<br>
 The news, another from that town was sped<br>
 (Such is the usage there); so, Egypt through,<br>
 In a few hours the joyful tidings flew.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 As he had brought the adventure to an end,<br>
 The duke now sought the noble youths to stir,<br>
 (Though of themselves that way their wishes tend,<br>
 Nor they to whet that purpose need the spur)<br>
 That they the Church from outrage to defend,<br>
 And rights of Charles, the Roman Emperor,<br>
 Would cease to war upon that Eastern strand,<br>
 And would seek honour in their native land.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 Gryphon and Aquilant thus bid adieu,<br>
 One and the other, to his lady fair;<br>
 Who, though it sorely troubled them, ill knew<br>
 How to resist the wishes of the pair.<br>
 The duke, together with the warlike two,<br>
 Turns to the right, resolved to worship, where<br>
 God erst incarnate dwelt, the holy places,<br>
 Ere he to cherished France his way retraces.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 The warriors to the left-hand might incline,<br>
 As plainer and more full of pleasant cheer,<br>
 Where still along the sea extends their line;<br>
 But take the right-hand path, abrupt and drear;<br>
 Since the chief city of all Palestine,<br>
 By six days’ journey, is, through this, more near.<br>
 Water there is along this rugged track,<br>
 And grass; all other needful matters lack.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 So that, before they enter on their road,<br>
 All that is needful they collect, and lay<br>
 Upon the giant’s back the bulky load,<br>
 Who could a tower upon his neck convey.<br>
 The Holy Land a mountain-summit showed,<br>
 At finishing their rough and salvage way;<br>
 Where HEAVENLY LOVE a willing offering stood,<br>
 And washed away our errors with his blood.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 They, at the entrance of the city, view<br>
 A gentle stripling; and in him the three<br>
 Agnize Sir Sansonet of Mecca, who<br>
 Was, in youth’s flower, for sovereign chivalry,<br>
 For sovereign goodness, famed the country through,<br>
 And wise beyond his years: from paganry<br>
 Converted by Orlando to the truth,<br>
 Who had, with his own hands, baptized the youth.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVI.</td><td>
 Designing there a fortilage, in front<br>
 Of Egypt’s caliph they the warrior found;<br>
 And with a wall two miles in length, the mount<br>
 Of Calvary intending to surround.<br>
 Received with such a countenance, as is wont<br>
 To be of inward love the surest ground,<br>
 Them he conducted to his royal home,<br>
 And, with all comfort, harboured in the dome.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVII.</td><td>
 As deputy, the sainted land he swayed,<br>
 Conferred on him by Charlemagne, in trust,<br>
 To him the English duke a present made<br>
 Of that so sturdy and unmeasured beast,<br>
 That it ten draught horse burdens had conveyed;<br>
 So monstrous was the giant, and next gave<br>
 The net, in which he took the unwieldy slave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVIII.</td><td>
 In quittance, Sansonet, his sword to bear,<br>
 Gave a rich girdle to Astolpho bold,<br>
 And spurs for either heel, a costly pair,<br>
 With bucklers and with rowels made of gold;<br>
 Which (’twas believed) the warrior’s relicts were,<br>
 Who freed the damsel from that dragon old;<br>
 Spoils, which Sir Sansonet, with many more,<br>
 From Joppa, when he took the city, bore</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIX.</td><td>
 Cleansed of their errors in a monastery,<br>
 From whence the odour of good works upwent,<br>
 They of Christ’s passion every mystery<br>
 Contemplating, through all the churches went;<br>
 Which now, to our eternal infamy,<br>
 Foul Moor usurp; what time on strife intent,<br>
 All Europe rings with arms and martial deeds,<br>
 And war is everywhere but where it needs.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">C.</td><td>
 While grace the warlike three devoutly sought,<br>
 Intent on pardon and on pious lore,<br>
 A Grecian pilgrim, known to Gryphon, brought<br>
 Tidings, which ill the afflicted champion bore,<br>
 From his long-cherished vow and former thought,<br>
 Too foreign, too remote; and these so sore<br>
 Inflamed his troubled breast, and bred such care,<br>
 They wholly turned aside his mind from prayer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CI.</td><td>
 For his misfortune, one of lovely feature<br>
 Sir Gryphon worshipped, Origilla hight.<br>
 Of fairer visage and of better stature,<br>
 Not one among a thousand meets the sight:<br>
 But faithless, and of such an evil nature,<br>
 That thou mightst town and city search outright,<br>
 And continent and island, far and near,<br>
 Yet, never, as I think, wouldst find her peer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CII.</td><td>
 In Constantine’s imperial city, burned<br>
 With a fierce fever, he had left the fair;<br>
 And hoped to find her, to that place returned,<br>
 Lovelier than ever; and enjoy her there.<br>
 But she to Antioch (as the warrior learned)<br>
 Had with another leman made repair;<br>
 Thinking, while such fresh youth was yet her own,<br>
 ’Twere not a thing to brook—to sleep alone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIII.</td><td>
 Sir Gryphon, from the time he heard the news<br>
 Had evermore bemoaned him, day or night:<br>
 Whatever pleasure other wight pursues<br>
 Seems but the more to vex his troubled sprite.<br>
 Let each reflect, who to his mischief woos,<br>
 How keenly tempered are Love’s darts of might,<br>
 And, heavier than all ills, the torment fell,<br>
 In that he was ashamed his grief to tell.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CIV.</td><td>
 This: for that Aquilant had oft before<br>
 Reproved him for the passion which he nursed,<br>
 And sought to banish her from his heart’s core;<br>
 —Her, who of all bad women is the worst,<br>
 He still had censured, in his wiser lore,<br>
 If by his brother Aquilant accurst,<br>
 Her Gryphon, in his partial love, excuses,<br>
 For mostly self-conceit our sense abuses.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">CV.</td><td>
 It therefore is his purpose, without say<br>
 To Aquilant, alone to take the quest<br>
 As far as Antioch, and bear her away,<br>
 Who had borne off his heart-core from his breast:<br>
 To find him, who had made the dame his prey,<br>
 And take such vengeance of him, ere he rest,<br>
 As shall for aye be told. My next will tell<br>
 How he effected this, and what befell.</td></tr>
