 </p><h1 align="center">CANTO 25</h1>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="center"><small>ARGUMENT.</small></td></tr><tr><td><small><i>
 Rogero Richardetto from the pains<br>
 Of fire preserves, doomed by Marsilius dead:<br>
 He to Rogero afterwards explains<br>
 Fully the cause while he to death was led.<br>
 Them mournful Aldigier next entertains,<br>
 And with them the ensuing morning sped,<br>
 Vivian and Malagigi to set free;<br>
 To Bertolagi sold for hire and fee.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <table align="center" cellpadding="10">
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">I.</td><td>
 Oh! mighty springs of war in youthful breast,<br>
 Impetuous force of love, and thirst of praise!<br>
 Nor yet which most avails is known aright:<br>
 For each by turns its opposite outweighs.<br>
 Within the bosom here of either knight,<br>
 Honour, be sure, and duty strongly sways:<br>
 For the amorous strife between them is delayed,<br>
 Till to the Moorish camp they furnish aid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">II.</td><td>
 Yet love sways more; for, save that the command<br>
 Was laid upon them by their lady gay,<br>
 Neither would in that battle sheathe the brand,<br>
 Till he was crowned with the victorious bay;<br>
 And Agramant might vainly with his band,<br>
 For either knight’s expected succour, stay.<br>
 Then Love is not of evil nature still;<br>
 —He can at times do good, if often ill.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">III.</td><td>
 ’Twas now, suspending all their hostile rage,<br>
 One and the other paynim cavalier,<br>
 The Moorish host from siege to disengage,<br>
 For Paris, with the gentle lady, steer;<br>
 And with them goes as well that dwarfish page,<br>
 Who tracked the footsteps of the Tartar peer,<br>
 Till he had brought the warrior front to front,<br>
 In presence with the jealous Rodomont.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IV.</td><td>
 They at a mead arrived, where, in disport,<br>
 Knights were reposing by a stream, one pair<br>
 Disarmed, another casqued in martial sort;<br>
 And with them was a dame of visage fair.<br>
 Of these in other place I shall report,<br>
 Not now; for first Rogero is my care,<br>
 That good Rogero, who, as I have shown,<br>
 Into a well the magic shield had thrown.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">V.</td><td>
 He from that well a mile is hardly gone<br>
 Ere he a courier sees arrive at speed,<br>
 Of those dispatched by King Troyano’s son<br>
 To knights whom he awaited in his need;<br>
 From him Rogero hears that so foredone<br>
 By Charles are those who hold the paynim creed,<br>
 They will, save quickly succoured in the strife,<br>
 As quickly forfeit liberty and life.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VI.</td><td>
 Rogero stood awhile in pensive case,<br>
 Whom many warring thoughts at once opprest;<br>
 But neither fitted was the time nor place<br>
 To make his choice, or judge what promised best.<br>
 The courier he dismist, and turned his face<br>
 Whither he with the damsel was addrest;<br>
 Whom aye the Child so hurried on her way,<br>
 He left her not a moment for delay.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VII.</td><td>
 Pursuing thence their ancient road again,<br>
 They reached a city, with the westering sun;<br>
 Which, in the midst of France, from Charlemagne<br>
 Marsilius had in that long warfare won:<br>
 Nor them to interrupt or to detain,<br>
 At drawbridge or at gate, was any one:<br>
 Though in the fosse, and round the palisade,<br>
 Stood many men, and piles of arms were laid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">VIII.</td><td>
 Because the troop about that fortress see<br>
 Accompanying him, the well-known dame,<br>
 They to Rogero leave the passage free,<br>
 Nor even question him from whence he came.<br>
 Reaching the square, of evil company<br>
 He finds it full, and bright with ruddy flame;<br>
 And, in the midst, is manifest to view<br>
 The youth condemned, with face of pallid hue.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">IX.</td><td>
 As on the stripling’s face he turns his eyes,<br>
 Which hangs declined and wet with frequent tear,<br>
 Rogero thinks he Bradamant descries;<br>
 So much the youth resembles her in cheer:<br>
 More sure the more intently he espies<br>
 Her face and shape: when thus the cavalier:<br>
 “Or this is Bradamant, or I no more<br>
 Am the Rogero which I was before.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">X.</td><td>
 “She hath adventured with too daring will,<br>
 In rescue of the youth condemned to die;<br>
 And, for the enterprise had ended ill,<br>
 Hath there been taken, as I see. Ah! why<br>
 Was she so hot her purpose to fulfil,<br>
 That she must hither unattended hie!<br>
 —But I thank Heaven, that hither have I made:<br>
 Since I am yet in time to lend her aid.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XI.</td><td>
 He drew his falchion without more delay,<br>
 (His lance was broken at the other town),<br>
 And, though the unarmed people making way,<br>
 Wounding flank, paunch, and bosom, bore them down.<br>
 He whirled his weapon, and, amid the array,<br>
 Smote some across the gullet, cheek, or crown.<br>
 Screaming, the dissipated rabble fled;<br>
 The most with cloven limbs or broken head.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XII.</td><td>
 As while at feed, in full security,<br>
 A troop of fowl along the marish wend,<br>
 If suddenly a falcon from the sky<br>
 Swoop mid the crowd, and one surprise and rend,<br>
 The rest dispersing, leave their mate to die,<br>
 And only to their own escape attend;<br>
 So scattering hadst thou seen the frighted throng,<br>
 When young Rogero pricked that crowd among.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIII.</td><td>
 Rogero smites the head from six or four,<br>
 Who in escaping from the field are slow.<br>
 He to the breast divides as many more,<br>
 And countless to the eyes and teeth below.<br>
 I grant no helmets on their heads they wore,<br>
 But there were shining iron caps enow;<br>
 And, if fine helmets did their temples press,<br>
 His sword would cut as deep, or little less.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIV.</td><td>
 Such good Rogero’s force and valour are,<br>
 As never now-a-days in warrior dwell;<br>
 Nor yet in rampant lion, nor in bear,<br>
 Nor (whether home or foreign) beast more fell.<br>
 Haply with him the earthquake might compare,<br>
 Or haply the great devil—not he of hell—<br>
 But he who is my lord’s, who moves in fire,<br>
 And parts heaven, earth, and ocean in his ire.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XV.</td><td>
 At every stroke he never less o’erthrew<br>
 Than one, and oftener two, upon the plain;<br>
 And four, at once, and even five he slew;<br>
 So that a hundred in a thought were slain.<br>
 The sword Rogero from his girdle drew<br>
 As knife cuts curd, divides their plate and chain.<br>
 Falerina in Orgagna’s garden made,<br>
 To deal Orlando death, that cruel blade.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVI.</td><td>
 But to have forged that falchion sorely rued,<br>
 Who saw her garden wasted by the brand.<br>
 What wreck, what ruin then must have ensued,<br>
 From this when wielded by such warrior’s hand?<br>
 If e’er Rogero force, e’er fury shewed,<br>
 If e’er his mighty valour well was scanned,<br>
 ’Twas here; ’twas here employed; ’twas here displayed;<br>
 In the desire to give his lady aid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVII.</td><td>
 As hare from hound unslipt, that helpless train<br>
 Defends itself against the cavalier.<br>
 Many lay dead upon the cumbered plain,<br>
 And numberless were they who fled in fear.<br>
 Meanwhile the damsel had unloosed the chain<br>
 From the youth’s hands, and him in martial gear<br>
 Was hastening, with what speed she might, to deck,<br>
 With sword in hand and shield about his neck.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XVIII.</td><td>
 He, who was angered sore, as best he cou’d,<br>
 Sought to avenge him of that evil crew;<br>
 And gave such signal proofs of hardihood,<br>
 As stamped him for a warrior good and true.<br>
 The sun already in the western flood<br>
 Had dipt his gilded wheels, what time the two,<br>
 Valiant Rogero and his young compeer,<br>
 Victorious issued, of the city clear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XIX.</td><td>
 When now Rogero and the stranger knight,<br>
 Clear of the city-gates, the champaigne reach,<br>
 The youth repays, with praises infinite,<br>
 Rogero in kind mode and cunning speech,<br>
 Who him, although unknown, had sought to right,<br>
 At risk of life, and prays his name to teach<br>
 That he may know to whom his thanks he owed<br>
 For such a mighty benefit bestowed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XX.</td><td>
 “The visage of Bradamant I see,<br>
 The beauteous features and the beauteous cheer.”<br>
 Rogero said; “and yet the suavity<br>
 I of her well-known accents do not hear:<br>
 Nor such return of thanks appears to be<br>
 In place towards her faithful cavalier.<br>
 And if in very sooth it is the same,<br>
 How has the maid so soon forgot my name?”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXI.</td><td>
 In wary wise, intent the truth to find,<br>
 Rogero said, “You have I seen elsewhere;<br>
 And have again, and yet again, divined,<br>
 Yet know I not, nor can remember where.<br>
 Say it, yourself, if it returns to mind,<br>
 And, I beseech, your name as well declare:<br>
 Which I would gladly hear, in the desire<br>
 To know whom I have rescued from the fire.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXII.</td><td>
 “—Me, it is possible you may have seen,<br>
 I know not when nor where (the youth replied);<br>
 For I too range the world, in armour sheen,<br>
 Seeking adventure strange on every side;<br>
 Or haply it a sister may have been,<br>
 Who to her waist the knightly sword has tied;<br>
 Born with me at a birth; so like to view,<br>
 The family discerns not who is who.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIII.</td><td>
 “You not first, second, or even fourth will be,<br>
 Who have in this their error had to learn;<br>
 Nor father, brother, nor even mother me<br>
 From her (such our resemblance) can discern.<br>
 ’Tis true, this hair, which short and loose you see,<br>
 In many guise, and hers, with many a turn,<br>
 And in long tresses wound about her brow,<br>
 Wide difference made between us two till now.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIV.</td><td>
 “But since the day, that, wounded by a Moor<br>
 In the head (a story tedious to recite)<br>
 A holy man, to heal the damsel’s sore,<br>
 Cut short to the mid-ear her tresses bright,<br>
 Excepting sex and name, there is no more<br>
 One from the other to distinguish; hight<br>
 I Richardetto am, Bradamant she;<br>
 Rinaldo’s brother and his sister we.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXV.</td><td>
 “And to displease you were I not afraid,<br>
 You with a wonder would I entertain,<br>
 Which chanced from my resemblance to the maid;<br>
 Begun in pleasure, finishing in pain.”<br>
 He to whom nought more pleasing could be said,<br>
 And to whose ears there was no sweeter strain<br>
 That what in some sort on his lady ran,<br>
 Besought the stripling so, that he began.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVI.</td><td>
 “It so fell out, that as my sister through<br>
 The neighbouring wood pursued her path, a wound<br>
 Was dealt the damsel by a paynim crew,<br>
 Which her by chance without a helmet found.<br>
 And she was fain to trim the locks which grew<br>
 Clustering about the gash, to maker her sound<br>
 Of that ill cut which in her head she bore:<br>
 Hence, shorn, she wandered through the forest hoar.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVII.</td><td>
 “Ranging, she wandered to a shady font;<br>
 Where, worn and troubled, she, in weary wise,<br>
 Lit from her courser and disarmed her front,<br>
 And, couched upon the greenwood, closed her eyes.<br>
 A tale more pleasing than what I recount<br>
 In story there is none, I well surmise:<br>
 Thither repaired young Flordespine of Spain,<br>
 Who in that wood was hunting with her train.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXVIII.</td><td>
 “And, when she found my sister in the shade,<br>
 Covered, except her face, with martial gear,<br>
 —In place of spindle, furnished with the blade—<br>
 Believed that she beheld a cavalier:<br>
 The face and manly semblance she surveyed,<br>
 Till conquered was her heart: with courteous cheer<br>
 She wooed the maid to hunt with her, and past<br>
 With her alone into that hold at last.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXIX.</td><td>
 “When now she had her, fearless of surprise,<br>
 Safe in a solitary place, that dame,<br>
 By slow degrees, in words and amorous wise,<br>
 Showed her deep-wounded heart; with sighs of flame,<br>
 Breathed from her inmost breast, with burning eyes,<br>
 She spake her soul sick with desire; became<br>
 Now pale, now red; nor longer self-controlled,<br>
 Ravished a kiss, she waxed so passing bold.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXX.</td><td>
 “My sister was assured the huntress maid<br>
 Falsely conceited her a man to be;<br>
 Nor in that need could she afford her aid;<br>
 And found herself in sore perplexity.<br>
 ’ ’Tis better that I now dispel (she said)<br>
 The foolish thought she feeds, and that in me<br>
 The damsel should a gentle woman scan,<br>
 Rather than take me for a craven man.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXI.</td><td>
 “And she said well: for cravenhood it were<br>
 Befitting man of straw, not warrior true,<br>
 With whom so bright a lady deigned to pair,<br>
 So wonderous sweet and full of nectarous dew,<br>
 To clack like a poor cuckow to the fair,<br>
 Hanging his coward wing, when he should woo,<br>
 Shaping her speech to this in wary mode,<br>
 My sister that she was a damsel, showed;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXII.</td><td>
 “That, like Camilla and like Hyppolite,<br>
 Sought fame in battle-field, and near the sea,<br>
 In Afric, in Arzilla, saw the light;<br>
 To shield and spear enured from infancy.<br>
 A spark this quenched not; nor yet burned less bright<br>
 The enamoured damsel’s kindled phantasy.<br>
 Too tardy came the salve to ease the smart:<br>
 So deep had Love already driven his dart.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIII.</td><td>
 “Nor yet less fair to her my sister’s face<br>
 Appeared, less fair her ways, less fair her guise;<br>
 Nor yet the heart returned into its place,<br>
 Which joyed itself within those dear-loved eyes.<br>
 Flordespine deems the damsel’s iron case<br>
 To her desire some hope of ease supplies;<br>
 And when she thinks she is indeed a maid,<br>
 Laments and sobs, with mighty woe downweighed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIV.</td><td>
 “He who had marked her sorrow and lament,<br>
 That day, himself had sorrowed with the fair.<br>
 ‘What pains (she said) did ever wight torment,<br>
 So cruel, but that mine more cruel were?<br>
 I need not to accomplish my intent,<br>
 In other love, impure or pure, despair;<br>
 The rose I well might gather from the thorn:<br>
 My longing only is of hope forlorn.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXV.</td><td>
 “ ‘If ’twas thy pleasure, Love, to have me shent,<br>
 Because by glad estate thine anger stirred,<br>
 Thou with some torture might’st have been content<br>
 On other lovers used; but never word<br>
 Have I found written of a female bent<br>
 On love of female, mid mankind or herd.<br>
 Woman to woman’s beauty still is blind;<br>
 Nor ewe delights in ewe, nor hind in hind.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVI.</td><td>
 “ ’Tis only I, on earth, in air, or sea,<br>
 Who suffer at thy hands such cruel pain;<br>
 And this thou hast ordained, that I may be<br>
 The first and last example in thy reign.<br>
 Foully did Ninus’ wife and impiously<br>
 For her own son a passion entertain;<br>
 Loved was Pasiphae’s bull and Myrrha’s sire;<br>
 But mine is madder than their worst desire.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVII.</td><td>
 “ ‘Here female upon male had set her will;<br>
 Had hope; and, as I hear, was satisfied.<br>
 Pasiphae the wooden cow did fill:<br>
 Others, in other mode, their want supplied.<br>
 But, had he flown to me,—with all his skill,<br>
 Dan Daedalus had not the noose untied:<br>
 For one too diligent hath wreathed these strings;<br>
 Even Nature’s self, the puissantest of things.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXVIII.</td><td>
 “So grieves the maid, so goads herself and wears,<br>
 And shows no haste her sorrowing to forego;<br>
 Sometimes her face, sometimes her tresses tears,<br>
 And levels at herself the vengeful blow.<br>
 In pity, Bradamant the sorrow shares,<br>
 And is constrained to hear the tale of woe,<br>
 She studies to divert, with fruitless pain,<br>
 The strange and mad desire; but speaks in vain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XXXIX.</td><td>
 “She, who requires assistance, not support,<br>
 Still more laments herself, with grief opprest.<br>
 By this the waning day was growing short,<br>
 For the low sun was crimsoning the west;<br>
 A fitting hour for those to seek a port,<br>
 Who would not in the wood set up their rest.<br>
 When to this city, near her sylvan haunt,<br>
 Young Flordespine invited Bradament.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XL.</td><td>
 “My sister the request could ill deny;<br>
 And so they came together to the place,<br>
 Where, but for you, by that ill squadron I<br>
 Had been compelled the cruel flame to face:<br>
 There Flordespina made her family<br>
 Caress and do my sister no small grace;<br>
 And, having in a female robe arraid,<br>
 Past her on all beholders for a maid.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLI.</td><td>
 “Because perceiving vantage there was none<br>
 In the male cheer by which she was misled,<br>
 The damsel held it wise, reproach to shun,<br>
 Which might by any carping tongue be said.<br>
 And this the rather: that the ill, which one<br>
 Of the two garments in her mind had bred,<br>
 Now with the other which revealed the cheat,<br>
 She would assay to drive from her conceit.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLII.</td><td>
 “The ladies share one common bed that night,<br>
 Their bed the same, but different their repose.<br>
 One sleeps, one groans and weeps in piteous plight,<br>
 Because her wild desire more fiercely glows;<br>
 And on her wearied eyes should slumber light,<br>
 All is deceitful that brief slumber shows.<br>
 To her it seems, as if relenting Heaven<br>
 A better sex to Bradamant is given.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIII.</td><td>
 “As the sick man with burning thirst distrest,<br>
 If he should sleep,—ere he that wish fulfil,—<br>
 Aye in his troubled, interrupted rest,<br>
 Remembers him of every once-seen rill:<br>
 So is the damsel’s fancy still possest,<br>
 In sleep, with images which glad her will.<br>
 Then from the empty dreams which crowd her brain,<br>
 She wakes, and, waking, finds the vision vain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIV.</td><td>
 “What vows she vowed, how oft that night she prayed,<br>
 To all her gods and Mahound, in despair!<br>
 —That they, by open miracle, the maid<br>
 Would change, and give her other sex to wear.<br>
 But all the lady’s vows were ill appaid,<br>
 And haply Heaven as well might mock the prayer;<br>
 Night fades, and Phœbus raises from the main<br>
 His yellow head, and lights the world again.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLV.</td><td>
 “On issueing from their bed when day is broken,<br>
 The wretched Flordespina’s woes augment:<br>
 For of departing Bradamant had spoken,<br>
 Anxious to scape from that embarrassment.<br>
 The princess a prime jennet, as a token,<br>
 Forced on my parting sister, when she went;<br>
 And gilded housings, and a surcoat brave,<br>
 Which her own hand had richly broidered, gave.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVI.</td><td>
 “Her Flordespine accompanied some way,<br>
 Then, weeping, to her castle made return.<br>
 So fast my sister pricked, she reached that day<br>
 Mount Alban; we who for her absence mourn,<br>
 Mother and brother, greet the martial may,<br>
 And her arrival with much joy discern:<br>
 For hearing nought, we feared that she was dead,<br>
 And had remained in cruel doubt and dread.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVII.</td><td>
 “Unhelmed, we wondered at her hair, which passed<br>
 In braids about her brow, she whilom wore;<br>
 Nor less we wondered at the foreign cast<br>
 Of the embroidered surcoat which she wore:<br>
 And she to us rehearsed, from first to last,<br>
 The story I was telling you before;<br>
 How she was wounded in the wood, and how,<br>
 For cure, were shorn the tresses from her brow;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLVIII.</td><td>
 “And next how came on her, with labour spent,<br>
 —As by the stream she slept—that huntress bright;<br>
 And how, with all her false semblance well content,<br>
 She from the train withdrew her out of sight.<br>
 Nor left she any thing of her lament<br>
 Untold; which touched with pity every wight;<br>
 Told how the maid had harboured her, and all<br>
 Which past, till she revisited her Hall.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XLIX.</td><td>
 “Of Flordespine I knew: and I had seen<br>
 In Saragossa and in France the maid;<br>
 To whose bewitching eyes and lovely mien<br>
 My youthful appetite had often strayed:<br>
 Yet her I would not make my fancy’s queen;<br>
 For hopeless love is but a dream and shade:<br>
 Now I this proffered in such substance view,<br>
 Straitway the ancient flame breaks forth anew.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">L.</td><td>
 “Love, with this hope, constructs his subtle ties;<br>
 Who other threads for me would vainly weave.<br>
 ’Tis thus he took me, and explained the guise<br>
 In which I might the long-sought boon achieve.<br>
 Easy it were the damsel to surprise;<br>
 For as the likeness others could deceive,<br>
 Which I to Bradamant, my sister, bear,<br>
 This haply might as well the maid ensnare.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LI.</td><td>
 “Whether I speed or no, I hold it wise,<br>
 Aye to pursue whatever give delight.<br>
 I with no other of my plan devise,<br>
 Nor any seek to counsel me aright.<br>
 Well knowing where the suit of armour lies<br>
 My sister doffed, I thither go at night;<br>
 Her armour and her steed to boot I take,<br>
 Nor stand expecting until daylight break.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LII.</td><td>
 “I rode all night—Love served me as a guide—<br>
 To seek the home of beauteous Flordespine;<br>
 And there arrived, before in ocean’s tide<br>
 The western sun had hid his orbit sheen.<br>
 A happy man was he who fastest hied<br>
 To tell my coming to the youthful queen;<br>
 Expecting from that lady, for his pain,<br>
 Favour and goodly guerdon to obtain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIII.</td><td>
 “For Bradamant the guests mistake me all,<br>
 —As you yourself but now—so much the more,<br>
 That I have both the courser and the pall<br>
 With which she left them but the day before.<br>
 Flordespine comes at little interval,<br>
 With such festivity and courteous lore,<br>
 And with a face, so jocund and so gay,<br>
 She could not, for her life, more joy display.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIV.</td><td>
 “Her beauteous arms about my neck she throws,<br>
 And fondly clasping me, my mouth she kist.<br>
 If to my inmost heart the arrow goes,<br>
 Which Love directs, may well by you be wist.<br>
 She leads me to her chamber of repose<br>
 In haste, not suffers others to assist<br>
 In taking off my panoply of steel;<br>
 Disarming me herself from head to heel.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LV.</td><td>
 “Then, ordering from her store a costly vest,<br>
 She spread it, and—as I a woman were—<br>
 The lady me in that rich garment drest,<br>
 And in a golden net confined my hair.<br>
 I gravely moved my eye-balls, nor confest,<br>
 By gesture or by look, the sex I bear.<br>
 My voice, which might discover the deceit,<br>
 I tuned so well that none perceived the cheat.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVI.</td><td>
 “Next to the hall, where dame and cavalier<br>
 In crowds are gathered, we united go;<br>
 Who make to us such court and goodly cheer,<br>
 As men to queen or high-born lady show.<br>
 Here oft I laughed at some, with secret jeer,<br>
 Who, knowing not the sex concealed below<br>
 My flowing robe of feminine array,<br>
 Wooed me with wishful eyes in wanton way.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVII.</td><td>
 “When more advanced in now the festive night,<br>
 And the rich board—board plenteously purveyed<br>
 With what in season was most exquisite—<br>
 Has been some time removed, the royal maid<br>
 Expects not till I of myself recite<br>
 The cause, which thither me anew conveyed:<br>
 By her own courtesy and kindness led,<br>
 That lady prays me to partake her bed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LVIII.</td><td>
 “Damsels and dames withdrawn—with all the rest—<br>
 Pages and chamberlains, when now we lay,<br>
 One and the other, in our bed undrest,<br>
 With kindled torches, counterfeiting day;<br>
 ‘Marvel not, lady,’ (her I thus addrest,)<br>
 ‘That I return after such short delay;<br>
 For, haply, thou imagined, that again<br>
 Thou shouldst not see me until Heaven knows when.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LIX.</td><td>
 “ ‘The reason I departed from thy side,<br>
 And next of my return, explained shall be.<br>
 Could I unto thy fever have applied,<br>
 By longer sojourn here, a remedy,<br>
 I in thy service would have lived and died,<br>
 Nor would have been an hour away from thee:<br>
 But seeing how my stay increased thy woe,<br>
 I, who could do no better, fixed to go.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LX.</td><td>
 “ ‘Into the middle of a wood profound<br>
 By chance I from the beaten pathway strayed:<br>
 Where near me plaintive cries I hear resound,<br>
 As of a woman who intreated aid.<br>
 To a lake of crystal I pursue the sound,<br>
 And, there, amid the waves, a naked maid<br>
 Caught on the fish-hook of a Faun, survey,<br>
 Who would devour alive his helpless prey.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXI.</td><td>
 “ ‘Upon the losel, sword in hand, I ran,<br>
 And, for I could not aid in other wise,<br>
 Bereft of life that evil fisherman.<br>
 She in an instant to the water flies.<br>
 —‘Me hast thou helped not vainly,’ (she began)<br>
 And well shalt be rewarded—with what prize<br>
 Thou canst demand—for know I am a nymph,<br>
 And have my dwelling in this crystal lymph;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXII.</td><td>
 “ ‘And power is mine to work portentous ends;<br>
 Nature and Elements I force: thy prayer<br>
 Shape to the scope to which my strength extends,<br>
 And leave its satisfaction to my care.<br>
 Charmed by my song the moon from Heaven descends;<br>
 Fire can I freeze, and harden liquid air;<br>
 And I at times have stopt the sun, and stirred<br>
 This earth beneath me by a simple word.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIII.</td><td>
 “Treasure I covet not, nor yet aspire<br>
 O’er land or people to hold sovereign sway;<br>
 Nor greater strength nor valour would acquire,<br>
 Nor fame in every warfare bear away;<br>
 But only to accomplish thy desire,<br>
 Entreat the damsel she will show some way.<br>
 Nor one nor other method I forestall;<br>
 But to her choice refer me, all in all.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXIV.</td><td>
 “Scarce my demand was made, before mine eye<br>
 Beneath the lymph engulphed that lady viewed:<br>
 Nor answered she my prayer, but, for reply,<br>
 Me with the enchanted element bedewed;<br>
 Which has no sooner touched my face than I,<br>
 I know not how, am utterly transmewed:<br>
 I see, I feel—yet doubting what I scan—<br>
 Feel, I am changed from woman into man.<br>
 
         LXV - LXIX<br>
    (Stazas LXV - LXIX untranslated by Rose)</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXX.</td><td>
 “The thing remained concealed between us two;<br>
 So that our bliss endured some months; at last<br>
 We were espied; and, as I sorely rue,<br>
 The tidings to the Spanish monarch past.<br>
 Thou that whilere preserved’st me from the crew,<br>
 Which me into the flames designed to cast,<br>
 By this mayst fully comprehend the rest;<br>
 But God alone can read my sorrowing breast.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXI.</td><td>
 So Richardetto spake, and by his say<br>
 Made the dark path they trod less irksome be.<br>
 Up a small height this while their journey lay,<br>
 Girded with cliff and cavern, drear to see.<br>
 Bristling with rocks, a steep and narrow way<br>
 Was to that rugged hill the stubborn key;<br>
 A town, called Agrismonte, crowned the steep,<br>
 Which Aldigier of Clermont had in keep.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXII.</td><td>
 Bastard of Buovo, brother to the pair,<br>
 Sir Vivian and Sir Malagigi hight:<br>
 Who him Gerardo’s lawful son declare,<br>
 Are witnesses of little worth and light.<br>
 —This, as it may!—strong, valiant, wise, and ware,<br>
 Liberal, humane, and courteous was the knight;<br>
 And on the fortress of its absent lord,<br>
 By night and day, kept faithful watch and ward.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIII.</td><td>
 His cousin Richardetto, as behoved,<br>
 Was courteously received by Aldigier;<br>
 Who him as dearly as a brother loved,<br>
 And made Rogero for his sake good cheer;<br>
 But not with wonted welcome;—inly moved—<br>
 He even wore a visage sad and drear:<br>
 For he, that day, ill-tidings had received,<br>
 And hence in heart and face the warrior grieved.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIV.</td><td>
 To Richardetto he exclaims, instead<br>
 Of greeting: “Evil news are hither blown.<br>
 By a sure messenger, to-day I read<br>
 That faithless Bertolagi of Bayonne,<br>
 With barbarous Lanfusa has agreed,<br>
 And costly spoils makes over to that crone;<br>
 Who will consign to him the brethren twain,<br>
 Thy Malagigi and thy Viviane;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXV.</td><td>
 “These she, since Ferrau took them, aye has stayed<br>
 Imprisoned in a dark and evil cell;<br>
 Till the discourteous and foul pact was made<br>
 With that false Maganzese of whom I tell;<br>
 And them to-morrow, to a place conveyed<br>
 ’Twixt Bayonne and a town of his, will sell<br>
 To him, who will be present, to advance<br>
 The price of the most precious blood in France.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVI.</td><td>
 “One, at a gallop, even now, to report<br>
 Tidings to our Rinaldo of the wrong,<br>
 I sent; bur fear that he can ill resort<br>
 To him in time, the journey is so long.<br>
 Men have I not to sally from my fort;<br>
 And my power halts where my desire is strong.<br>
 The traitor will the knights, if rendered, slay;<br>
 Nor know I what to do nor what to say.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVII.</td><td>
 Sir Richardetto the ill news displease,<br>
 And (as they him) displease in equal wise<br>
 Rogero; who, when silent both he sees,<br>
 Nor able any counsel to devise,<br>
 Exclaims with mickle daring: “Be at ease;<br>
 I challenge for myself the whole emprize;<br>
 And, to set free your brethren, in my hand<br>
 More than a thousand shall avail this brand.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXVIII.</td><td>
 “I ask not men, I ask not aid; my spear<br>
 Is, I believe, sufficient to the feat.<br>
 I only ask of you a guide to steer<br>
 Me to the place where for the exchange they meet:<br>
 I even in this place will make you hear<br>
 Their cries, who for that evil bargain threat.”<br>
 He said; nor to one listener of the twain,<br>
 That had helped his actions, spake in vain.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXIX.</td><td>
 The other heard him not, or heard at most<br>
 As we great talkers hear, who little do:<br>
 But Richardetto took aside their host<br>
 And told how him he from the fire withdrew;<br>
 And how he was assured, beyond his boast,<br>
 He would in time and place his prowess shew.<br>
 ’Twas now that better audience than before<br>
 Aldigier lent, and set by him great store;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXX.</td><td>
 And at the feast, where Plenty for the three<br>
 Emptied her horn, him honoured as his lord.<br>
 Here they conclude they can the brethren free<br>
 Without more succour from their gaoler’s ward.<br>
 This while Sleep seized on lord and family,<br>
 Save young Rogero: no repose afford<br>
 To him the thoughts, which evermore molest,<br>
 And, rankling in his bosom, banish rest.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXI.</td><td>
 The siege of Agramant, to him that day<br>
 Told by the messenger, he has at heart.<br>
 He well discerns that every least delay<br>
 Will he dishonour. What a ceaseless smart<br>
 Will scorn inflict, what shame will him appay,<br>
 If he against his sovereign lord take part?<br>
 Oh! what foul cowardice, how foul a crime<br>
 His baptism will appear at such a time!</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXII.</td><td>
 That true religion had the stripling swayed<br>
 Men might at any other time conceive:<br>
 But now, when needed was the warrior’s aid<br>
 From siege the Moorish monarch to relieve,<br>
 That Fear and Baseness had more largely weighed,<br>
 In his designs, would every one believe,<br>
 That any preference of a better creed:<br>
 This thought makes good Rogero’s bosom bleed.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIII.</td><td>
 Nor less to quit his Queen, her leave unsought,<br>
 Did with Rogero’s other griefs combine:<br>
 Now this and now that care upon him wrought;<br>
 Which diversely his doubtful heart incline:<br>
 The unhappy lover fruitlessly had thought<br>
 To find her at the abode of Flordespine;<br>
 Whither together went (as told whilere)<br>
 To succour Richardetto, maid and peer.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIV.</td><td>
 He next bethinks him of the promise plight<br>
 To meet at Vallombrosa’s sanctuary,<br>
 Deems her gone thither, and that ’twill excite<br>
 Her wonderment himself not there to see.<br>
 Could he at least a message send or write,<br>
 That he with reason might not censured be,<br>
 Because not only he had disobeyed,<br>
 But was departing hence, and nothing said!</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXV.</td><td>
 He, having thought on many things, in the end<br>
 Resolves on writing what behoves; and, though<br>
 He knows not how his letter he shall send,<br>
 In the assurance it will safely go,<br>
 This hinders not; he thinks that, as they wend,<br>
 Chance in his way some faithful Post may throw;<br>
 Nor more delays: up leaps the restless knight,<br>
 And calls for pen and paper, ink and light.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVI.</td><td>
 That which is needed, in obedience meet,<br>
 Aldigier’s valets bring, a careful band,<br>
 The youth begins to write; and, first, to greet<br>
 The maid, as wonted courtesies demand;<br>
 Next tells how Agramant has sent to entreat,<br>
 In his dispatches, succour at his hand;<br>
 And, save he quickly to his comfort goes,<br>
 Must needs be slain or taken by his foes.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVII.</td><td>
 Then adds, his sovereign being so bested,<br>
 And praying him for succour in his pain,<br>
 She must perceive what blame upon his head<br>
 Would light, if Agramant applied in vain;<br>
 And, since with her he is about to wed,<br>
 ’Tis fitting he should keep him with stain;<br>
 For ill he deems a union could endure<br>
 Between aught foul and her to passing pure.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td><td>
 And if he erst a name, renowned and clear,<br>
 Had laboured to procure by actions fair,<br>
 And having gained it thus, he held it dear,<br>
 —If this had sought to keep—with greater care<br>
 He kept it now,—and with a miser’s fear<br>
 Guarded the treasure she with him would share;<br>
 Who, though distinct in body and in limb,<br>
 When wedded, ought to be one soul with him;</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">LXXXIX.</td><td>
 And, as he erst by word, he now explained<br>
 Anew by writing, that the period o’er,<br>
 For which he was to serve his king constrained,<br>
 Unless it were his lot to die before,<br>
 He would in deed a Christian be ordained,<br>
 As in resolve he had been evermore;<br>
 And of her kin, Rinaldo and her sire,<br>
 Her afterwards in wedlock would require.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XC.</td><td>
 “I would,” he said, “relieve, with your good will,<br>
 My king, besieged by Charlemagne’s array,<br>
 That the misjudging rabble, prone to ill,<br>
 Might never, to my shame and scandal, say:<br>
 Rogero, in fair wind and weather, still<br>
 Waited upon his sovereign, night and day,<br>
 And now that Fortune to King Charles is fled,<br>
 Has with that conquering lord his ensign spread.’</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCI.</td><td>
 “I fifteen days or twenty ask, that I<br>
 Yet once again may to our army speed;<br>
 So that, by me from leaguering enemy<br>
 The African cantonments may be freed:<br>
 I will some fit and just occasion spy,<br>
 Meanwhile, to justify my change of creed,<br>
 I for my honour make this sole request;<br>
 Then wholly yours for life, in all things, rest.”</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCII.</td><td>
 Rogero is such words his thoughts exposed,<br>
 Which never could by me be fully showed;<br>
 And added more, nor from his task reposed,<br>
 Until the crowded paper overflowed:<br>
 He next the letter folded and enclosed,<br>
 And sealed it, and within his bosom stowed;<br>
 In hopes to meet next morning by the way<br>
 One who might covertly that writ convey.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIII.</td><td>
 When he had closed the sheet, that amorous knight<br>
 His eyelids closed as well, and rest ensued:<br>
 For Slumber came and steeped his wearied might<br>
 In balmy moisture, from a branch imbued<br>
 With Lethe’s water; and he slept till—white<br>
 And red—a rain of flowers the horizon strewed,<br>
 Painting the joyous east with colours gay;<br>
 When from her golden dwelling broke the day:</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCIV.</td><td>
 And when the greenwood birds ’gan, far and wide,<br>
 Greet the returning light with gladsome strain,<br>
 Sir Aldigier (who wished to be the guide,<br>
 Upon that journey, of the warlike twain,<br>
 Who would in succour of those brethren ride,<br>
 To rescue them from Bertolagi’s chain)<br>
 Was first upon his feet; and either peer<br>
 Issues as well from bed, when him they hear.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCV.</td><td>
 When clad and thoroughly in arms arrayed—<br>
 Rogero with the cousins took his way,<br>
 Having that pair already warmly prayed<br>
 The adventure on himself alone to lay:<br>
 But these, by love for those two brethren swayed,<br>
 And deeming it discourtesy to obey,<br>
 Stood out against his prayer, more stiff than stone,<br>
 Nor would consent that he should wend alone.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVI.</td><td>
 True to the time and place of change, they hie<br>
 Whither Sir Aldigier’s advices teach;<br>
 And there survey an ample band who lie<br>
 Exposed to fierce Apollo’s heat; in reach,<br>
 Nor myrtle-tree nor laurel they descry,<br>
 Nor tapering cypress, ash, nor spreading beech:<br>
 But naked gravel with low shrubs discerned,<br>
 Undelved by mattock and by share unturned.</td></tr>
 
 <tr><td align="right" valign="top">XCVII.</td><td>
 Those three adventurous warriors halted where<br>
 A path went through the uncultivated plain,<br>
 And saw a knight arrive upon the lair,<br>
 Who, flourished o’er with gold, wore plate and chain,<br>
 And on green field that beauteous bird and rare,<br>
 Which longer than an age extends its reign.<br>
 No more, my lord: for at my canto’s close<br>
 I find myself arrived, and crave repose.</td></tr>
